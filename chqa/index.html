What is cryptoeconomics and why use the term crypto?

Cryptoeconomics is the field studying how blockchain technology drives innovations in how companies are organized, contracts are set up, and incentives are established.
—The word crypto comes from the Greek kryptos, meaning hidden.
—Cryptography uses computer algorithms to encode and protect data and messages.
—Ciphers were used over 2,000 years ago by Ancient Egyptians and the Roman Empire.
—Cryptoassets are the purest form of fintech innovation, made possible by technology.

Chapter 5 · Bitcoin, Blockchain & Crypto

What is Bitcoin? How did its creator Satoshi Nakamoto address the double-spend problem?

Bitcoin is the first cryptocurrency, launched in 2009 by the anonymous Satoshi Nakamoto to create a peer-to-peer electronic cash system without a central authority.
—Double-spend problem: verifying money was not spent twice without a trusted intermediary.
—Satoshi used a time-stamped electronic ledger secured against alteration using cryptography.
—It relied on a P2P network of nodes where transactions were collected in blocks and hashed.
—Identical public copies of the ledger are distributed across the network, over 46,000 nodes in 2023.

Chapter 5 · Bitcoin, Blockchain & Crypto

What is blockchain? What are its main differences relative to traditional ledgers?

A blockchain is an electronic distributed ledger recording ownership of a single asset, Bitcoin, with transactions batch-processed in blocks and chained together using cryptographic hashes.
—Each block holds a timestamp, reference to the previous block, verified transactions, and a nonce.
—It is public, permissionless, and fully transparent, so anyone can download and view it.
—It is immutable and append-only, secured against tampering using SHA-256 hashing.
—Identities are pseudo-anonymous, shown by a hashed public key rather than real names.

Chapter 5 · Bitcoin, Blockchain & Crypto

What is hashing and SHA-256? How are these two concepts related to proof-of-work and mining of Bitcoin?

Hashing is a one-way mathematical algorithm mapping data of any size to a fixed-size string; Bitcoin uses SHA-256, which produces a 256-bit output.
—A hash cannot be decrypted or inverted to reveal the source data.
—Including each block's hash in the next makes altering prior blocks require redoing all of them.
—Miners race to find a nonce that, hashed with a block, yields a hash with leading zeros.
—This search via trial and error is called mining, and the process is proof-of-work.

Chapter 5 · Bitcoin, Blockchain & Crypto

What are the transaction fees associated with Bitcoin and how are they determined and allocated among miners?

Bitcoin imposes no official transaction fees, but users offer voluntary fees to incentivize miners to prioritize their transactions from the mempool.
—Fees arose after mid-2016 when transactions exceeded the 1-MB block size, causing congestion.
—Miners choose transactions with the highest fee-to-size ratio to maximize each block's aggregate fee.
—The fee, called the feerate, is measured in Satoshis-per-byte; 1 bitcoin equals 100,000 Satoshis.
—Fees became de facto mandatory and are highly volatile as the network became more crowded.

Chapter 5 · Bitcoin, Blockchain & Crypto

What is a hard fork and how did one lead to the creation of Bitcoin Cash?

A hard fork is a disagreement among cryptocurrency holders where a minority breaks away to create a new coin on a new blockchain, splitting the original like a stock spin-off.
—Both coins share the same transaction history up to the point of the fork.
—Bitcoin Cash forked on August 1, 2017, at block 478,558 on the Bitcoin blockchain.
—A minority wanted an 8-MB block size to process more transactions per 10 minutes.
—After the fork, one Bitcoin was ~$2,741 and one Bitcoin Cash ~$380, totaling $3,121.

Chapter 5 · Bitcoin, Blockchain & Crypto

What are cryptowallets? What are cryptoexchanges?

A cryptowallet is a software application or hardware device that stores a private key or seed to send and receive cryptocurrencies, while a cryptoexchange is an electronic marketplace for buying and selling them for a fee.
—Wallets are hot (internet-connected software) or more secure cold storage like the Trezor hardware wallet.
—Wallets do not hold coins but store a secret seed used to generate addresses and authorize transactions.
—Exchanges include fiat-to-crypto and crypto-to-crypto types; leaders are Binance, Coinbase, and Kraken.
—Exchanges hold customers' private keys, becoming honey pots vulnerable to hacks like Mt. Gox's 850,000 bitcoins.

Chapter 5 · Bitcoin, Blockchain & Crypto

What are the risks of using Bitcoin as a means of payment?

Bitcoin has been unsuccessful as a medium of exchange because verifying transactions via mining is slow and costly, taking hours to days, and few merchants accept it.
—Extreme price volatility undermines its usefulness as a store of value.
—The 1-MB block limit and miner incentives crowd out low-value payments.
—Settlement risk: transactions may be delayed and payments are irreversible.
—Operational, counterparty, governance, and legal/regulatory risks also threaten HODLers.

Chapter 5 · Bitcoin, Blockchain & Crypto

What have researchers found explains price changes and the cross-section of returns in Bitcoin?

Researchers found cryptocurrency returns are driven by user adoption, investor attention, and momentum, while three factors explain the cross-section of returns across coins.
—Returns were driven by adoption metrics like wallet users, active addresses, transactions, and payments.
—Returns were unrelated to electricity or computing costs; Google Bitcoin searches and momentum predicted returns.

Chapter 6 · Ethereum & DeFi

What is the Ethereum network and who created it?

Ethereum is an open-source, programmable blockchain supporting smart contracts, dapps, and DAOs, created by Russian-Canadian programmer Vitalik Buterin.
—Buterin posted the Ethereum whitepaper in November 2013 at age 19.
—Its native currency ether (ETH) buys the 'gas' needed to run dapps.
—It is the foundation for decentralized finance (DeFi), a disruptive financial innovation.
—The Ethereum Foundation officially launched the blockchain on July 30, 2015.

What is ether (ETH) and what role does it play on the Ethereum network?

Ether (ETH) is Ethereum's native token, described by Buterin as the internal crypto fuel used to buy the 'gas' required to run dapps.

—There is no cap on ETH; around 120 million were in circulation in April 2022.

Chapter 7 · Crowdfunding & P2P

What is a balance sheet lender and why are the incentives different from a P2P/marketplace lender?

A balance sheet lender acts like a bank, supplying loans directly and holding them on its own balance sheet, so it bears the default risk.
—P2P/marketplace lenders are intermediaries that pass default losses to the lender, not the platform.
—P2P underwriting commissions create a principal-agent problem with misaligned incentives.
—Balance sheet lenders' incentives are aligned because they suffer any losses they originate.

How is crowdfunding regulated and what are the risks to individual investors or lenders?

Regulation varies widely by country with no global standard, and investors face four principal risks: credit risk, illiquidity, poor disclosure, and fraud.
—US funding portals must register with the SEC and join FINRA under the 1933 Securities Act.

—Losses fall on investors, who bear default and fraud risk without recourse to the borrower.

What is a robo-advisor and how do they create a personalized portfolio for a customer? What does it cost to use a robo-advisor?

What is passive investing and how does it explain the growth of exchange traded funds (ETFs)?
