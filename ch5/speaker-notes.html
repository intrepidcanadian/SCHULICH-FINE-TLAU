Slide 01 ~2 min
Cover — Bitcoin, Blockchain, and Cryptocurrencies
  • Welcome the class. Frame the session: Chapter 4 valued fintechs using DCF and multiples. Today we shift to a category that breaks those tools — cryptoassets with no cash flows and no balance sheet.
  • Preview the arc: Satoshi’s double-spend solution → PoW mining → altcoins → ICOs → wallets & exchanges → FTX collapse → CBDCs → what the research actually says about returns.
  • Opening question: “If you had bought one bitcoin in 2010 at $0.06 and held to the 2021 peak of $69K, what is your return?” (Roughly 1,150,000x — over a million-fold.) Then ask: “Would you have held?”
  • Housekeeping: ~55–75 min. Pause for questions after PoW (slide 5), after FTX (slide 9), and at the end.
Transition: “Let’s start with what cryptoassets actually are — and why economists care.”
Slide 02 ~4 min
Cryptoeconomics & Caveat Emptor
  • Define cryptoasset: a digital asset whose ownership is on a distributed ledger and secured by cryptography. Etymology: kryptos = hidden. Ancient Egyptians used ciphers.
  • Walk through the four token flavors: utility (BAT), security (digital common stock), governance (Maker), NFT (non-divisible art/media). Stress that token is an umbrella term — covers many shapes.
  • Market context: $1.3T (Apr 2023) down from $2.0T (year-end 2021). Crypto winters are recurring — this is not the first or the last.
  • Behavioral angle: FOMO drives top-tick buying. Caveat emptor — informed consent matters in a market with no investor protection regime.
Transition: “The asset class begins with one coin. Why did Satoshi build it — and how?”
Slide 03 ~4 min
Bitcoin & Crypto Winters
  • Origin: nine-page whitepaper, October 2008. Bitcoin launched January 2009. Satoshi’s genesis block references the Times headline “Chancellor on Brink of Second Bailout for Banks” — a political statement, not just a timestamp.
  • Identity: still unknown. Satoshi’s public key holds 1,125,150 BTC — worth tens of billions at peak. Untouched.
  • Walk Fig 5.1 and Tab 5.1 across the boom-bust cycles: 2011 ($35 → $4), 2013 ($1,150 → $177), 2017 ($19K → $4K), 2021 ($69K → $17K). The pattern: 75–85% drawdowns are normal.
  • Ask: “What does volatility this extreme tell us about Bitcoin as a store of value?” (Answer: not great. But narrative-driven assets behave this way until liquidity normalizes.)
Transition: “How does this actually work under the hood? Let’s solve the hardest problem — double-spending.”
Slide 04 ~5 min
Double-Spend, Blockchain & Hashing
  • Set up the double-spend problem: digital coins are bits — perfect copies. Without a central mint, how do you know I haven’t already spent the same coin? Earlier digital cash projects (DigiCash, b-money) failed this test.
  • Satoshi’s solution: a time-stamped, append-only, distributed ledger where every transaction is publicly visible. Identical copies on 46,000+ nodes (2023). Hashing chains the blocks.
  • Explain SHA-256: 256-bit one-way function. Same input → same hash. Quantum computing will eventually break this — signal a future research/policy question.
  • Walk the five building blocks: blockchain, hashing, P2P network, digital keys, Bitcoin script. The architectural choices solve the trust problem without a trusted intermediary.
  • Throughput call-out: 2.5 TPS vs. Visa’s 24,000 TPS. Block size cap of 1 MB is a hard ceiling — this becomes the source of every later fight (Bitcoin Cash hard fork, transaction fee inflation).
Transition: “Verifying transactions takes work — literally. Let’s talk mining.”
Slide 05 ~5 min
Proof-of-Work, Mining & PoS
  • Mining is random number guessing. Miners search for a nonce that, hashed with the block, gives a hash starting with N leading zeros. First to find it wins. Difficulty adjusts to keep blocks 10 min apart.
  • Walk the halving schedule: 50 BTC → 25 → 12.5 → 6.25 (May 2020) → 3.125 (Apr 2024). Long-run incentive: as block rewards shrink, transaction fees must replace them. This is where mempool fee auctions come in.
  • Energy: 73 TWh/yr (2022) — Austria. Carbon footprint of Switzerland. Down from a 200 TWh peak in early 2022 because BTC fell and Ethereum left for PoS.
  • Concentration: ~75% of hash power in a handful of mining pools. Research suggests collusion to leave blocks partially empty — extracting fees from urgent users while crowding out small payments.
  • Pivot to PoS: validators stake collateral instead of burning electricity. Slashing penalizes downtime or double signing. Ethereum’s Merge (Sept 2022) cut energy by 99.95%. Bitcoin will not follow — the community treats PoW as definitional.
Transition: “Bitcoin spawned thousands of imitators. Which ones matter?”
Slide 06 ~4 min
Altcoins & the Top 10
  • Open-source legacy: Satoshi posted Bitcoin Core on GitHub in 2009. Modify the code, change a few parameters, launch a new coin. Litecoin (2011), Namecoin (2011), Peercoin (2012), Dogecoin (2013), XRP (2013) all followed.
  • Tab 5.2 (Apr 2023): top 10 = $1.5T — 79% of total market cap. BTC alone = 41%. Stablecoins (USDT, USDC, BUSD) sit at #3, #5, plus others.
  • Ask: “Why is Ether (ETH) #2?” (Programmable blockchain — smart contracts. We’ll do Ch. 6 next week entirely on Ethereum.)
  • Hard forks: Bitcoin Cash (Aug 2017, 8 MB blocks), Bitcoin Gold (Oct 2017), Bitcoin SV (Nov 2018). The day after the BCH fork, holders had both coins — sum of parts > whole. Magic.
Transition: “If launching a coin is easy, raising money against one is easier still. Enter the ICO boom.”
Slide 07 ~5 min
ICOs — The Boom and the Crackdown
  • ICO mechanics: pre-mine a token, write a whitepaper, post it online, open a one-month subscription window. Buyers send BTC/ETH, get the new token. No investment bank, no offering document.
  • Scale: $20B raised across 860 ICOs from 2014 to mid-2018. EOS ($4.2B), Telegram ($1.7B). 2017 alone: 343 ICOs, $5.5B.
  • The Howey test (July 25, 2017): the SEC asserted that ICO tokens are investment contracts — therefore securities. (1) investment of money, (2) common enterprise, (3) profits from efforts of others. The market shut down by mid-2018.
  • Fraud statistics: WSJ reviewed 3,291 whitepapers and flagged 2,000 with signs of fraud. Studies estimate 80% of 2017 ICOs were scams. The SEC even launched a fake ICO (“HoweyCoin”) as an investor education campaign.
  • Ripple XRP case study: classic over-promise, under-deliver. Founders pre-mined 100B XRP, kept 20B. RippleNet for interbank payments was real. But banks used fiat, not XRP. Price collapsed when this became clear in 2018. SEC fined for AML violations.
Transition: “Buying a token is one thing. Holding it safely is another.”
Slide 08 ~5 min
Wallets & Exchanges — Honey Pots
  • A cryptowallet doesn’t hold coins — it holds keys. The coins are on the blockchain. Lose the private key, lose access to the coins permanently. Estimates: 20% of all BTC are lost forever.
  • Evolution: paper → software (hot) → hardware (cold). Trezor (2014) was first — PIN, passphrase, USB, open-source. Ledger came later.
  • Three components every wallet has: address (public, share freely), private key (sign transactions), recovery seed (restore if lost). Phishing attacks target the seed.
  • Exchanges are honey pots. Tab 5.5 covers Binance, Coinbase, Kraken. When coins sit on an exchange, the exchange holds the private keys — on-chain, they own the coins. “Not your keys, not your coins.”
  • Hack history: Mt. Gox 2014 (850K BTC, $460M, 70% of global BTC volume), Bitfinex 2016 ($72M), Coincheck 2018 ($535M). The list keeps growing — sets up the FTX collapse next.
Transition: “Speaking of exchanges that vaporize — let’s talk FTX.”
Slide 09 ~5 min
The FTX Collapse — Case Study
  • SBF: MIT ’14, Jane Street, Centre for Effective Altruism, Alameda Research, FTX (2019). At peak in 2021: FTT $80, FTX 3rd-largest cryptoexchange, SBF net worth $26.5B. He was crypto’s public face — testifying before Congress, lobbying regulators.
  • The fatal structure: FTX created FTT token. Alameda held $5.8B of FTT against $14.6B balance sheet. FTX lent Alameda customer funds against FTT collateral. Circular — like a bank pledging its own stock to itself.
  • Timeline: Nov 2 CoinDesk leaks Alameda’s balance sheet. Nov 6 CZ (Binance, FTT investor) dumps $2B FTT. FTT crashes. Bank run on FTX. Nov 8 trading halted. Nov 10 Binance walks away from rescue after finding $9.4B hole. Nov 22 bankruptcy.
  • The legal indictment: SBF used customer funds to finance Alameda trading. Customer escrow rules — standard in regulated exchanges — were not followed. Sullivan & Cromwell called it “a personal fiefdom.”
  • Aftermath: $1B in customer funds missing. SBF convicted on all 7 counts, Nov 2023. Sentenced to 25 years.
Transition: “After FTX, regulators woke up. What about the people who don’t trade — the HODLers?”
Slide 10 ~4 min
CBDCs & the Six Risks to HODLers
  • CBDCs: 80+ central banks exploring. Three open questions: blockchain or centralized ledger? Does it threaten monetary policy? Retail or wholesale only? Most countries focus on wholesale (interbank settlement).
  • Critique of Bitcoin as money: fails as medium of exchange (few merchants), as store of value (extreme volatility), barely works as unit of account. CBDCs are central banks’ answer.
  • Why crypto enthusiasts dislike CBDCs: centralized = surveillance. Not decentralized. Defeats the original purpose. Government can freeze accounts. Privacy concerns.
  • Walk Tab 5.6 — six risks every HODLer should know: price, counterparty, settlement, operational, governance, legal/regulatory. Ask: “Which of these did the FTX customers experience?” (Answer: all six.)
Transition: “What do academics say about all this? Three findings.”
Slide 11 ~4 min
Cryptoeconomics Research
  • Three families of research findings: (1) trust is broken — illegal activity, manipulation, suspicion; (2) Bitcoin cannot scale — structural throughput limits, oligopolistic mining; (3) volatility is a feature, not a bug — multiple equilibria, sunspot dynamics.
  • The 25% illegal activity finding was high-profile — later disputed by other researchers using better data (less than 3% of volume). The truth is in between, but the stigma sticks.
  • Connect to slide 5: the “empty blocks” finding from researchers studying every block since genesis. Mining pools may strategically leave blocks empty to inflate fees. This is collusive behavior.
  • Bottom line for academics: Bitcoin is unlikely to replace fiat. But the blockchain layer — tokens, smart contracts, programmable money — may still transform finance. That’s next week.
Transition: “So if Bitcoin doesn’t work as money, does it work as an investment?”
Slide 12 ~4 min
Asset Pricing — Crypto vs. Stocks
  • Liu & Tsyvinski (2021): monthly return 20.4% vs. 0.94% S&P. Standard deviation 70.8% vs. 3.42%. Sharpe ratio 0.29 vs. 0.27 — nearly identical risk-adjusted return.
  • Walk Tab 5.7 across daily / weekly / monthly. The high returns are real — but so is the volatility. The Sharpe says you’re paid for the risk you take.
  • What drives crypto returns? User adoption — wallet users, active addresses, transaction count. NOT electricity, NOT mining cost. Network effects. Adoption begets value begets adoption.
  • Three factors explain the cross-section: market beta, size (small coins > large), momentum (past 1–4 month winners outperform 1–4 weeks ahead). Familiar from equity markets.
  • Side note: Google searches for “Bitcoin” predict returns 1–6 weeks ahead. Attention-driven momentum — not surprising for retail-dominated markets.
Transition: “Let’s zoom out and pull the threads together.”
Slide 13 ~3 min
Closing — Code Is Law
  • Synthesis: Satoshi’s technical achievement was real — SHA-256 + PoW + P2P ledger solves double-spend. But the institutional layer (exchanges, ICOs, stablecoins) reintroduced every problem of traditional finance: fraud, insolvency, conflicts of interest.
  • Three numbers: 21M (the hard cap), $20B (ICO total raise, 80% scams), 99.95% (Ethereum’s energy savings from PoW → PoS).
  • Where the research lands: Bitcoin probably isn’t money. But blockchain may still be transformative for tokens, smart contracts, decentralized markets. That’s the bridge to Chapter 6.
  • Preview next class: Chapter 6 — Ethereum, Smart Contracts, and DeFi. Programmable blockchains, ether and gas, NFTs and DAOs, stablecoins (and TerraUSD’s death), MakerDAO as a decentralized central bank.
  • Final question: “If you had to design a successor to Bitcoin that addressed the failures we covered today, what would you change?” (Lead toward: PoS, programmable contracts, native stable unit of account — which is exactly what Ethereum + DeFi tries to do.)
End of lecture. Open floor for questions. Reading for next week: Chapter 6.
