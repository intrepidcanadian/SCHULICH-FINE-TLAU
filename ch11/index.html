Techfins & Bigtech

The biggest threat to incumbents isn’t the agile fintech start-up — it’s the platform giants. Alibaba, Tencent, Amazon, Apple, Facebook, and Google bundle finance into ecosystems with billions of users.

§11.1 · The most disruptive fintechs?
02 / 13
The threat came from outside the industry, not inside it

Not start-ups — tech platforms.

Early forecasts had agile fintechs disrupting finance. They mostly haven’t: customer acquisition is costly, regulators scrutinize, and incumbents fought back. The real disruptors are the global technology companies — techfins (Alibaba, Tencent) and bigtech (Amazon, Apple, Facebook, Google).

We see over $4.7 trillion of revenue at traditional financial services companies at risk — a $470 billion total profit pool.
— Goldman Sachs analyst report
Two terms · technology first, finance second
2016 · Jack Ma
Techfin
Coined to describe Alipay — the fintech acronym reversed. Tech platforms first, financial services second.
Bigtech
Bigger than banks
Market caps far exceed VISA, JPMorgan, ICBC, and Bank of China. Vast platforms, billions of users.
The pattern
E-commerce → finance
Start with shopping, social, gaming — then add payments, money funds, wealth, and insurance.

§11.1 · Why incumbents should worry
03 / 13
Finance as a loss leader, not a profit center

Customer-centric, not product-centric.

Techfins and bigtech don’t sell financial products to make a profit. Finance is an enabler — a loss leader to attract and retain users. Profits come from network effects and the other sides of their multi-sided platforms.

The incumbents’ risk. Banks, insurers, and asset managers are product-centric — they view customers as a source of profit. With platforms owning the user relationship, incumbents risk becoming mere manufacturers of commodity products — “bakers and farmers” fighting for shelf space on marketplaces controlled by Alibaba and Amazon.
The massive fear is you’re beholden to the platform to sell your customer back to you — and take a disproportionate share of the margin. Or worst case, meet that need themselves.
— CEO of Canada’s largest bank
The counter-view. Critics argue the threat is overstated — that these firms simply monetize user data via a thin financial layer, helped by permissive regulation that is now tightening. Like the fintech disruption hype, the threat may fade as techfins and bigtech are brought inside the regulatory perimeter and partner with regulated banks.

§11.2 · Chinese techfins · Alipay
04 / 13
Filling institutional voids in China’s financial system

Alibaba builds mobile payments.

China had few cards and an underdeveloped payment system. Buyers feared sellers wouldn’t deliver. Alipay (2004) — modeled on PayPal — charged no fees and held payments in escrow until goods arrived, building trust in e-commerce. It wasn’t a revenue strategy; it solved customer pain points.

Built on bank partnerships. From 2005 Alipay signed deals with the 5 major state-owned banks, 15 national banks, and VISA — gaining the payment rails and letting customers pay by card for the first time. Product innovations followed: theft compensation, voice and biometric payments, utility bill pay, and the Alipay Wallet with offline QR-code scanning.

§11.2 · From Alipay to Ant Group
06 / 13
“Small and beautiful changes” · financial inclusion

Ant Group: a full-service intermediary.

Spun out ahead of the 2014 IPO, Ant Financial added consumer lending (Hua Bei), wealth (Ant Fortune), insurance (Ant Insurance, 80+ partners), equity crowdfunding (ANTSDAQ), and global payments — managing customers’ “FinLives.”

The IPO that wasn’t. Set to list at end-2020, the deal was pulled days before launch after Jack Ma criticized Chinese regulators. The banking regulator accused Ant of regulatory arbitrage. As of May 2022 the IPO remained shelved.

§11.3 · North American bigtech · Amazon
08 / 13
Following the techfin playbook — build a payments foothold first

Amazon builds a bank for itself.

Amazon was first into finance (2007), building internally through trial and error to support its marketplace — not to become a conventional bank. Three core products: payments, merchant loans, and cash accounts.

Three core products
Amazon Pay (2007) — digital wallet, no interchange fee, on Amazon and third-party sites.
Amazon Lending (2011) — invite-only merchant loans of $1K–$750K (line of credit to $1M) with Bank of America and Goldman Sachs; rates reportedly 7–21%.
Amazon Cash (2017) — cash deposits via CVS, 7-Eleven, and Coinstar kiosks for the unbanked.
Amazon isn’t building a traditional bank that serves everyone. In a sense, Amazon is building a bank for itself.
— CB Insights
Experiment and fail. Amazon Go cashierless stores (2018) and Login & Pay (rivaling PayPal) succeeded — but Flexible Payments, Webpay, Local Register (vs. Square), and a 2014 mobile wallet were all shut down. It spreads small bets, tests, and kills what fails.

§11.3 · Facebook & Google
10 / 13
Stuck at payments — regulators and product-market fit

Facebook faces the regulators.

Facebook Pay (2019). Free P2P across Facebook, Instagram, Messenger, and WhatsApp, processed via PayPal and Stripe. Monetized through advertising — linking ad targeting to spending. India took two years; Brazil’s merchant payments stalled in 2022.
Libra → Diem: cancelled. A 2019 stablecoin with 28 partners — but no bank. Regulators and the G7 attacked it; PayPal, VISA, MasterCard, Stripe, and eBay dropped out. Rebranded Diem (2020), shut down January 2022, code sold off.
Google struggles with product-market fit

Reboot after reboot.

Google Wallet (2011), Android Pay (2015), and a merged Google Pay (2018) all lagged Apple Pay. By late 2020: 150M users vs. Apple’s 500M+ — and just 3% of mobile-wallet transaction volume vs. Apple’s 92%.
Plex abandoned. The 2020 bank-account plan with Citigroup drew terrible reviews; Google killed it in Oct 2021 and pivoted to B2B. The consolation: GV and CapitalG profited early in Stripe, Robinhood, Credit Karma, LendingClub, and Lemonade.

§11.4 · Competitive strengths
11 / 13
Three strengths that matter in financial services

Platforms, brands, and data.

1
Platform ecosystems
Core product (e-commerce, social, search, phones) plus same- and cross-side network effects create stickiness and a barrier to entry.
2
Customers & brand
Billions of loyal users as banks’ branch networks and brand trust erode — eroded by tech and by crises and scandals.
3
Big-data insight
Logging every click and message, analyzed with AI/ML — to understand a customer’s needs before they do.
The advantage isn’t the data itself — it’s how it’s used to anticipate needs. Alibaba inverts B2C into C2B: demand insight drives what gets supplied.

§11.5 · Assessing the threat
12 / 13
Will techfins and bigtech make banks obsolete? No.

Mixed success — same playbook, different outcomes.

Chinese techfins succeeded by filling institutional voids — payments, credit, wealth, insurance — always working with domestic banks. North American bigtech built strong payments footholds but, outside partnerships, faced regulatory opposition and poor product-market fit.

Bigtech never broke out. Amazon offers payments, merchant loans, and a cash account; Apple, payments and a card; Facebook and Google, only payments. This is for the best — a bigtech monopoly wouldn’t serve customers as well as competition does.
The winning combination. Bigtech-bank partnerships. Banks bring cheap stable funding, scale, risk management, and compliance; bigtech and fintechs bring technology, innovation, design thinking, and customer focus. What’s good for the customer is good for all.
Banks deserve a kick in the pants. Too product-centric, they’ve viewed customers as a means to profit — an estimated 7 million US households are unbanked. The fear of disruption has, at least, forced incumbents to innovate and refocus on the customer journey.

Chapter 11 · Closing
13 / 13
Key takeaways · the threat is real but bounded

Platforms first, finance second.

Chinese techfins filled real institutional voids and grew organically — but always with the banks, and only at home and in similar emerging markets. North American bigtech mastered payments yet stalled beyond them, blocked by regulators and product-market fit. The likely future isn’t displacement — it’s bigtech-bank partnerships that pair scale and compliance with technology and customer focus.
