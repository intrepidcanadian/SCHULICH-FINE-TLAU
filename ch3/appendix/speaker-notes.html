Slide 01 — The 2024-2026 Funding Reset 3 min
  • Walk through the bar chart: 2021 peak ($140B global fintech VC), crashed to $39B in 2023, stabilized at $43B in 2024, estimated ~$55B in 2025. The market is at roughly one-third of the 2021 peak — a "new normal" not a recovery to prior levels.
  • Two key shifts: (1) AI-native fintechs command 38% of mega-rounds in 2024 — capital is concentrating in AI-wrapped financial services. (2) ~30% of late-stage 2024 rounds were down-rounds (Klarna, Stripe, Brex, Plaid all repriced).
  • The IPO window: Klarna filed March 2025, Chime followed. The 2025 cohort raised $3.2B in combined IPO proceeds. Discipline returned — companies are listing at sober valuations rather than the 2021 mania marks.
  • Ask: "What does it mean for the industry that AI-native firms get preferential funding? What happens to the vintage neobanks that are not AI-native?" This sets up slide 17.
Transition: "Let's zoom into the IPO window specifically — four companies that defined the 2025-2026 exit cycle."
Slide 02 — Fintech IPO Window Reopens (2025-2026) 3 min
  • Four defining listings: Klarna ($15.1B, NYSE Sept 2025 — down from $45.6B peak), Chime ($11.6B, June 2025 — +37% day-one close), Circle (+168% day-one pop, USDC at $60B cap), eToro ($4.2B, Nasdaq May 2025).
  • The common thread: profitability-first. All four companies proved they could generate profit before going public. This is a 180-degree shift from the 2021 cohort that listed on growth alone.
  • Key lesson: down-round IPOs can clear if expectations are properly reset. Klarna at $15B is "only" one-third of its $45.6B private peak, but it still raised $1.37B in fresh capital and gave early investors liquidity.
  • Stripe chose NOT to IPO — the tender ladder of $91.5B (Feb 2025) → $106.7B (Sep 2025) → $159B (Feb 2026) shows there is now a credible alternative path. Tease this: "Stripe is staying private. Why? We will explore the tender-offer alternative in slide 19."
Transition: "Now let's look more closely at the IPO resurgence data and what it means for public-market discipline."
Slide 03 — 2025 IPO Resurgence (Detail) 3 min
  • Reinforce the key numbers: Klarna raised $1.37B at $40/share (closed week at $42.92); Chime at $27 with a 59% intraday pop; combined 2025 fintech IPO proceeds of $3.2B across four issuers.
  • The lesson from Chime: 8.6M active members plus profitability plus 67% revenue growth. The market rewarded fundamentals, not hype. Compare to Robinhood's 2021 IPO which had growth but no profitability — and dropped 74% in year one.
  • Stripe comparison: its latest private tender at $159B (Feb 2026, up from $91.5B in Q1 2025) exceeds every 2025 IPO valuation. The Collisons together hold a low-teens% stake; combined paper holdings >$20B at the $159B mark. This connects back to slide 10's dilution arithmetic — they diluted from ~100% to a low-teens% combined stake, but >$20B > $0.
  • Discussion prompt: "If you were advising a fintech at $10B private valuation with 30% growth, would you recommend an IPO in 2025 or a tender offer? What are the trade-offs?"
Transition: "The 2025 IPO class was largely a product of YC-era companies. Let's trace how YC itself has evolved since 2014."
Slide 04 — YC Fintech Evolution: 2014 to 2025 3 min
  • Update the textbook number: YC has now funded 5,000+ companies (up from 3,500+ cited in slide 04). In 2024 alone YC accelerated 1,000+ startups — 4x the W14 cohort that funded Wealthsimple.
  • Composition shift: ~70% of the W25 batch is building on LLMs/agents (vs. less than 5% in W14). The fintech sub-cohort is dominated by agentic operations, AI underwriting, and stablecoin rails. The "standard fintech pitch" has changed fundamentally.
  • Deal terms: $125K SAFE for 7% + $375K MFN uncapped. Total $500K. Compare to Wealthsimple raising $2M from 14 individual angels in 2014 — comparable dilution but far less friction for founders today.
  • Aggregate alumni value: $600B+ (Coinbase, Stripe, Airbnb, DoorDash, Instacart, Brex, Faire). The AI cohort drove the recovery from ~$400B in 2021. This is the power law at portfolio scale.
Transition: "Speaking of Wealthsimple — let's revisit our case study ten years later."
Slide 05 — Wealthsimple Arc: 2014 to 2025 4 min
  • The punchline: C$100B in assets under administration (Oct 2025), three years ahead of plan. Doubled from $50B in just twelve months. Series F: C$750M at C$10B post-money valuation, led by Dragoneer and GIC. Profitable since Q2 2023.
  • Walk through the timeline: $0 AUA (2014) to ~$3B (2018 Power Corp recap) to ~$15B (2021 $5B peak valuation) to C$100B (2025). The growth inflected once they expanded from robo-advisor to full banking stack: invest, save, tax, crypto, FX.
  • 3M+ Canadian clients — roughly 1 in 13 Canadians. Katchen is targeting C$1T AUA over the next decade. Ask: "Is this a reasonable target given Canada's banking oligopoly? What structural advantages does a digital-first platform have?"
  • Connect back to slide 05: those 14 angels who wrote $2M in 2014 now hold positions worth orders of magnitude more than their original cheques. This is the angel dream scenario made real with Canadian data.
  • Relevance for this class: Wealthsimple is a Toronto-headquartered company challenging the Big Five banks. Many of your classmates may end up working there or competing with them.
Transition: "Wealthsimple is the Canadian success story. For the global view of private valuation cycles, Stripe is the canonical example."
Slide 06 — Stripe Valuation Arc: 2014 to 2025 4 min
  • The cycle in one company: $1.75B (2014 Series C) to $20B (2018) to $95B mania peak (2021 Series H) to $50B down-round (2023 tender) to $91.5B (Feb 2025) to $106.7B (Sep 2025) to $159B (Feb 2026 tender). Same enterprise, private mark moved $50B → $159B in three years.
  • What drove the recovery: Bridge acquisition ($1.1B, Oct 2024) bringing stablecoin infrastructure in-house; $1.4T total payment volume in FY2024 (+38% YoY); half the Fortune 100 as customers; and the AI Agents launch (350+ partners, May 2025 Sessions).
  • The Collisons together hold a low-teens% combined stake; at $159B that is >$20B in combined paper holdings. Reference slide 22's closing: they diluted from 100% to a low-teens% combined stake, but a low-teens% of $159B massively exceeds 100% of $0.
  • Structural insight: Stripe chose NOT to IPO even at $159B. They used tender offers to provide employee liquidity without the regulatory burden and quarterly scrutiny of being public. This is a new playbook that is reshaping late-stage finance (covered in slide 19).
  • Ask: "The 2023 down-round was priced at $50B — a 47% drop. How would that affect employee morale? How would it affect the cap table?" Link to vesting, 409A valuations, and retention.
Transition: "Stripe recovered on the back of AI. That is not unique — AI-native fintechs are receiving a massive funding premium. Let's quantify it."
Slide 07 — AI-Native Fintech Funding: 2023 to 2025 4 min
  • The standout: Ramp went from $5.8B (Aug 2023) to $32B (Nov 2025) — a 5.5x private mark in 27 months. They built AI-agent expense management into the core product, not as a feature bolt-on. $1B+ ARR run-rate.
  • Mercury: $1.6B flat through 2022-23, then $3.5B (Sequoia-led, Mar 2025), reportedly targeting $5B (Apr 2026). Three years GAAP profitable. The pattern: profitability + AI narrative = multiple expansion.
  • Contrast with Brex: peaked at $12.3B in 2022, no priced up-round since. Pivoted from SMB to enterprise, had layoffs in 2023-24. Cautionary tale: being "first to market" is not enough if you do not ride the AI wave.
  • The "autonomous finance" thesis: AI-agent-led platforms command 2-3x the revenue multiples of vintage neobanks (Founders Fund / Lightspeed memos). Ask: "Is this justified, or is it a repeat of 2021 hype with an AI label?"
  • Synthesis: capital is concentrating, not expanding. The total pie ($55B in 2025) is still much smaller than 2021. But within that smaller pie, AI-native firms are getting a disproportionate share.
Transition: "These are all North American examples. For the emerging-market perspective, Nubank is the gold standard."
Slide 08 — Nubank Arc: 2014 to 2025 4 min
  • Scale: 131M customers across Brazil, Mexico, and Colombia (Q4 2025). ~58% of Brazil's adult population. From credit-card-only in 2014 to full banking stack: deposits, lending, investing, marketplace. Customer base grew 10x from 12M (2019).
  • Financial performance: $11.5B revenue FY2024 (+58% YoY FX-neutral), $1.97B net income, ROE in the 20-30% band. Eight straight profitable quarters. ARPAC ~$11, CAC ~$7 — this unit economics is exceptional for a neobank.
  • Market cap journey: $50B IPO (Dec 2021 NYSE), crashed to ~$11B trough (Jul 2022), recovered to $70B+ by 2025. Berkshire Hathaway exited Q1 2025 — stock continued to compound. This mirrors slide 08's IPO underperformance data but with a twist: Nubank recovered because it actually grew into its valuation.
  • Geographic expansion: Mexico (12M+ customers, full banking license Apr 2024), Colombia (~3M). This is the TAM/SAM/SOM framework in action — Nubank's TAM expanded as it entered new countries.
  • Lesson: emerging markets with large unbanked populations offer the biggest fintech TAMs. Brazil had 45M unbanked adults when Nubank launched. That is a structural opportunity that does not exist in Canada or the US.
Transition: "Nubank went public. Stripe stayed private. How do private companies provide liquidity without an IPO? Through secondary markets and tender offers."
Slide 09 — Secondary and Tender Markets: 2024-2026 4 min
  • The big picture: global secondary volume hit ~$240B in 2025 (up from $162B in 2024). Tender offers have replaced the IPO as the primary liquidity venue for late-stage tech. Companies can re-price every 6-12 months without going public.
  • Stripe: $50B (Mar 2023) to $91.5B (Feb 2025) to $106.7B (Sep 2025) to $159B (Feb 2026). All through tenders. +218% in three years without a single day of public trading. The cap table arithmetic of slide 10 still applies — but "exit" now includes a recurring private liquidity event.
  • OpenAI and SpaceX show the model at extreme scale: $500B+ and $350B+ respectively via tenders. This is creating a parallel private market with institutional infrastructure (Goldman Sachs Capital Solutions, Industry Ventures, Carta, Forge Global).
  • Implications for the textbook model: Chapter 3's growth stages assumed IPO or acquisition as the terminal event. In 2024-2026, many companies are "staying private indefinitely" while still providing exit paths. This changes VC fund structures (10-year fund lives may need to extend).
  • Ask: "If you are an employee at a $50B private company, how do you access your equity value? What are the constraints on selling in a secondary transaction?" (ROFR, company approval, accredited buyer requirements.)
Transition: "Not every story is about rising valuations. Plaid shows the full cycle — including the painful down-round in between."
Slide 10 — Plaid: Down-Round to IPO (2021-2026) 4 min
  • The full cycle in one company: Visa tried to acquire Plaid for $5.3B (blocked Nov 2020). Series D at $13.4B (Apr 2021). Then silence through 2022-2024. April 2025: $575M secondary at $6.1B — a 54% markdown from peak. FY2025 ARR: $546M, +40% YoY.
  • Why accept a down-round? To clean the cap table for a 2026 IPO. New investors (Franklin Templeton, BlackRock, Fidelity) at $6.1B have a better entry point. Employees and early holders get some liquidity. The company can go public at $8-10B without the baggage of a $13.4B last-round price.
  • Connect to Open Banking: CFPB Rule 1033 (Oct 2024) is a structural tailwind for Plaid. It mandates that banks share customer data with authorized third parties. Plaid connects 12,000+ fintech apps — it is the plumbing layer for open finance.
  • Ask: "Is a 54% down-round a failure? Or is it healthy discipline?" Push toward the nuance: the $13.4B was a mania-era price. The company grew revenue 3.2x while the valuation halved. The multiple compressed, but the business is stronger.
  • Preview: Plaid's projected 2026 IPO at $8-10B (15-18x FY2026 ARR) would still represent a healthy outcome for Series C investors and earlier, even though Series D investors are underwater.
Transition: "Let's close the IPO section by looking at how the entire 2025 class traded through Q1 2026 — the real report card."
Slide 11 — Class of 2025 IPOs: Q1 2026 Performance 3 min
  • The scorecard: Chime delivered its first profitable public quarter (Beat). Circle's USDC compounded under GENIUS Act tailwinds (Beat). Klarna got squeezed by rising delinquencies on its lending book (Mixed). eToro held steady, quietly profitable (Hold).
  • Key insight: the IPO is the easy day. The next four earnings prints decide whether the listing multiple sticks. Chime and Circle proved their models post-IPO. Klarna is still proving. This maps directly to slide 08's data on 1-year post-IPO returns.
  • Compare to the 2021 cohort: Robinhood -74%, Wise -64%, Marqeta -90%. The 2025 class is performing better because they priced at sober valuations. The bar was set low enough that fundamental execution (not hype) drives returns.
  • Discussion prompt: "Every issuer in 2025 priced below their 2021 private peak. Is that a bug or a feature? Who benefits from pricing discipline — the company, the public investors, or both?"
Transition: "Let's bring it all together with the single most important concept from this chapter — the dilution paradox."
