§3.1 · Growth stages
02 / 10
Capital lifecycle

Three stages of growth.

Every fintech traverses a predictable capital arc: early-stage survival funding, growth-stage scaling capital, and late-stage liquidity events. Each stage carries distinct risk profiles, investor types, and governance trade-offs.

Early Stage
Pre-Seed → Seed → Series A
Bootstrapping, FFF (friends, family, fools), angels. Proving product-market fit while managing burn rate through the Valley of Death.
Growth Stage
Series B → Series F
Venture capital firms deploy $10M–$500M+ rounds. Focus shifts from viability to market dominance and unit-economics proof.
Late Stage
Pre-IPO → IPO / Acquisition
Liquidity events for early investors. IPO (Coinbase, Robinhood) or acquisition (Plaid by Visa, blocked at $5.3B). Secondary markets emerge.

§3.2 · Pre-seed & seed
03 / 10
Burn rate · the Valley of Death
$15K
Average monthly burn rate per employee at a seed-stage fintech. A team of four burns $60K/month — 18 months of runway requires ~$1M before a single dollar of revenue.

The Valley of Death is the 12–18 month period between initial funding and sustainable revenue. Seed rounds typically raise $500K–$2M, selling 10–20% of the company. An MVP (minimum viable product) is the bridge — the cheapest version of the product that can attract paying users or the next round of capital.

§3.3 · Angel investors
04 / 10
Early capital · the elevator pitch

Angel investors.

High-net-worth individuals who invest $25K–$500K at the earliest stage. The average US angel is 57 years old, male (78%), with prior founding/CEO experience. They restrict investments to ~10% of net worth, averaging 11 investments of ~$25K each. 9 out of 10 investments either return the original amount or fail completely.

The Elevator Pitch — Four Questions (The Lens)
01 · Problem
What pain point exists?
Identify the specific market failure — cost, latency, exclusion, or opacity — that incumbent solutions leave unresolved.
02 · Solution
Why is your approach 10× better?
Not incrementally better — transformationally different. Stripe reduced merchant onboarding from weeks to seven lines of code.
03 · Market
How large is the addressable opportunity?
TAM/SAM/SOM framework. Investors seek markets large enough to support a $1B+ outcome even at modest penetration.
04 · Team
Why can this team win?
Domain expertise, technical depth, and prior founder experience. At pre-seed, the team is the product.
The average angel targets a 9–10× money-on-money return over an eight-year horizon (~33% IRR). Fewer than 10% of investments generate a positive return, so one big winner must cover losses on the rest.

§3.5 · Growth stage
06 / 10
Venture capital · Series A through F

Growth-stage capital.

Venture capital firms deploy institutional capital across lettered rounds. Each round typically doubles the prior valuation — provided the firm hits milestones on revenue, retention, and market expansion.

Series A
$2.5M–$5M
First priced round with explicit valuation. Product-market fit proven. Lead VC takes a board seat.
Series B–C
$20M–$200M
Unit economics validated. Geographic or vertical expansion. Sequoia, a16z, Accel compete for allocation.
Series D–F
$200M–$1B+
Pre-IPO mega-rounds. Crossover funds (Tiger Global, Coatue) enter. Stripe raised $2.235B total through Series H at $95B (2021).
A typical VC portfolio: 50% fail, 20% break even, 20% earn 2–4×, and 10% earn supernormal returns (50–60×) — targeting a portfolio IRR of 20–30%. VC funds are limited partnerships with a 10-year life. Sequoia Capital ($85B AUM, founded 1972) has backed Klarna, Nubank, PayPal, Robinhood, Square, and Stripe.

§3.6 · VC decision framework
07 / 10
Market sizing · the VC lens

TAM / SAM / SOM.

VCs size every opportunity through three concentric circles using top-down (industry research), bottom-up (customers × revenue), or theoretical (willingness-to-pay) approaches.

Circle 01
TAM — Total Addressable Market
The entire revenue opportunity if you captured 100% of the global market. Wise example: cross-border payments = $20T (2021).
Circle 02
SAM — Serviceable Available Market
The subset reachable with your current product, geography, and channel. Wise: individual cross-border transfers for digital nomads = $80B of the $2T individual segment.
Circle 03
SOM — Serviceable Obtainable Market
Realistic capture in 3–5 years. Wise: 50% of $80B SAM at 0.5% fee = $200M revenue. The number VCs actually underwrite to determine if a fund-returning outcome is possible.

§3.7 · Late stage & exit
08 / 10
IPO performance · the disappointing track record

Most fintech IPOs disappoint.

IPOs are expensive: underwriters charge ~7% of proceeds, plus legal, accounting, and road-show costs. Insiders face a 6–9 month lock-up period. The alternative exit — acquisition — is faster and cleaner (e.g. JPMorgan acquired Nutmeg; Visa acquired Tink). Among 13 fintech IPOs studied, the average stock was flat after 1 month, down 29% after 6 months, and down 48% after 1 year.

§3.8 · Financial securities
09 / 10
Securities across rounds · capitalization

Financial securities & the cap table.

Different funding stages use different instruments: convertible notes and SAFEs at seed, preferred shares in Series A+, common shares at IPO. Each instrument carries distinct rights — liquidation preferences, anti-dilution, and conversion mechanics.

Tab. 3.2 · Growth Stages & SecuritiesSource: Fintech Explained
Tab. 3.3 · Hypothetical Cap TableSource: Fintech Explained
SAFE
Simple Agreement for Future Equity
No interest, no maturity date. Converts at next priced round. Key provisions: valuation cap, 15–25% conversion discount, liquidation preference, MFN clause, and pro-rata rights. Y Combinator standard since 2013.
Convertible Note
Debt that becomes equity
~10% interest (accrued, not paid), ~2-year maturity. Converts at the lower of the conversion discount or valuation cap price. Higher claim than equity in bankruptcy but no voting rights.
Preferred Shares
VC standard instrument
Liquidation preference (1×–2×), anti-dilution protection, conversion rights to common, redemption rights. Preferred claim on dividends and bankruptcy. Series A onward.
Common Shares + Venture Debt
Residual claim & secured lending
Common: lowest claim, voting rights, pro-rata and tag-along/drag-along rights. Venture debt: 12–24 month secured loan at 15–25% p.a., may include warrants. Non-dilutive but expensive.

The dilution paradox

Ownership shrinks. Value grows.

A founder who owns 100% of a $0 company has nothing. A founder who owns 8% of a $10B company has $800M. In the textbook example, founders dilute from 50% (seed) to 7.4% (IPO) — but their stake grows from $0.5M to $2.9M (5.94×). Dilution formula: post-money % = pre-money % × (1 − new investor %).
