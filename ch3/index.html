§3.1 · Growth stages
02 / 13
Capital lifecycle

Three stages of growth.

Every fintech traverses a predictable capital arc: early-stage survival funding, growth-stage scaling capital, and late-stage liquidity events. Each stage carries distinct risk profiles, investor types, and governance trade-offs.

Early Stage
Pre-Seed → Seed
Bootstrapping, FFF, angels. Unpriced rounds. Proving product-market fit through the Valley of Death.
Growth Stage
Series A → Series F
Series A is the first priced round. VC firms deploy rounds from low millions to $500M+; focus shifts to market dominance and unit-economics proof.
Late Stage
Pre-IPO → IPO / Acquisition
Liquidity events for early investors. IPO (Coinbase, Robinhood) or acquisition (Visa-Plaid $5.3B deal announced 2020, blocked on antitrust).

§3.2 · Pre-seed & seed
03 / 13
Burn rate · the Valley of Death
$15K
Average monthly burn rate per employee at a seed-stage fintech. A team of four burns $60K/month — 18 months of runway requires ~$1M before a single dollar of revenue.

The Valley of Death is the 12–18 month period between initial funding and sustainable revenue. Seed rounds typically raise $500K–$2M, selling 10–20% of the company. An MVP (minimum viable product) is the bridge — the cheapest version of the product that can attract paying users or the next round of capital.

§3.3 · Angel investors
04 / 13
Early capital · the elevator pitch

Angel investors.

High-net-worth, accredited individuals who write the earliest cheques. Average US angel: 57yo, male (78%), prior CEO/founder experience. Caps investments at ~10% of net worth; spreads ~11 cheques averaging $25K each (~$250K portfolio). 9 in 10 investments either return original capital or fail.

The Elevator Pitch — Four Questions (The Lens)
01 · Who
Who is the customer?
A specific segment — B2C, B2B, B2B2C, or C2C — defined tightly enough to target with a single product.
02 · What
What is the pain point?
A negative experience customers will pay to fix — cost, latency, exclusion, opacity — that incumbents leave unresolved.
03 · Why
Why will customers pay?
A value proposition that is cheaper, easier, faster, or more convenient — and worth swapping money for.
04 · How
How does it make money?
Monetization model — fees, commissions, spread, SaaS subscription, data — that captures part of the value created.
The average angel targets 9–10× money-on-money over 8 years (~33% IRR). Fewer than 10% of investments generate a positive return.

§3.5 · Growth stage
06 / 13
Venture capital · Series A through F

Growth-stage capital.

VC firms deploy institutional capital across lettered rounds. Each round typically doubles the prior valuation — provided the firm hits milestones on revenue, retention, and expansion.

Series A
$2.5M–$5M val.
First priced round — pre-money valuation typically $2.5M–$5M (textbook). PMF proven. Lead VC takes a board seat.
Series B–C
$20M–$200M
Unit economics validated. Geographic or vertical expansion. Sequoia, a16z, Accel compete.
Series D–F
$200M–$1B+
Pre-IPO mega-rounds. Crossover funds enter. Stripe: $2.235B through Series H at $95B (2021).
A typical VC portfolio: 50% fail, 20% break even, 20% earn 2–4×, 10% earn 50–60× — portfolio IRR of 20–30%. Sequoia ($85B AUM) has backed Klarna, Nubank, PayPal, Robinhood, Square, Stripe.

§3.6 · VC decision framework
07 / 13
Market sizing · the VC lens

TAM / SAM / SOM.

VCs size every opportunity through three concentric circles using top-down (industry research), bottom-up (customers × revenue), or theoretical (willingness-to-pay) approaches.

Circle 01
TAM — Total Addressable Market
The entire revenue opportunity if you captured 100% of the global market. Wise: cross-border payments = $20T (2021).
Circle 02
SAM — Serviceable Available Market
The subset reachable with your current product, geography, and channel. Wise: digital-nomad cross-border = $80B of the $2T individual segment.
Circle 03
SOM — Serviceable Obtainable Market
Realistic capture in 3–5 years. Wise: 50% of $80B at 0.5% = $200M revenue — the number VCs underwrite for fund-returning outcomes.

§3.7 · Late stage & exit

IPO performance · the disappointing track record

Most fintech IPOs disappoint.

IPOs are expensive: ~7% underwriter fees, plus legal, accounting, road-show. 6–9 month lock-ups. The alternative — acquisition — is faster (JPMorgan/Nutmeg, Visa/Tink). Among 13 fintech IPOs: flat after 1 mo, −29% after 6 mo, −48% after 1 yr.

§3.8 · Financial securities
09 / 13
Securities across rounds

Financial securities by stage.

Different funding stages use different instruments: convertible notes and SAFEs at seed, preferred shares in Series A+, common shares at IPO. Each carries distinct rights — liquidation preferences, anti-dilution, and conversion mechanics.

Tab. 3.2 · Growth Stages & SecuritiesSource: Fintech Explained
SAFE
Simple Agreement for Future Equity
No interest, no maturity. Converts at next priced round. Provisions: valuation cap, 15–25% discount, liquidation pref, MFN, pro-rata. YC standard since 2013.
Convertible Note
Debt that becomes equity
~10% interest (accrued), ~2-yr maturity. Converts at lower of discount or cap price. Higher claim than equity in bankruptcy.
Preferred Shares
VC standard instrument
Liquidation pref (1×–2×), anti-dilution, conversion to common, redemption rights. Series A onward.
Common + Venture Debt
Residual claim & secured lending
Common: lowest claim, voting, pro-rata, tag/drag-along. Venture debt: 12–24 mo at 15–25% p.a., may include warrants.

§3.8b · Rights comparison
10 / 13
Investor rights · what each security gives you

Typical rights by security.

Each instrument comes with a distinct bundle of rights that determines claim priority, governance power, and economic upside. Negotiation of these rights is the substance of every term sheet.

SAFE
Simple Agreement for Future Equity
Non-priced equity instrument — no interest, no maturity. Converts at the next priced round into preferred or common shares.
Valuation cap — maximum pre-money valuation for conversion
Conversion discount — 15–25% below Series A price
Liquidation preference — preferred claim in bankruptcy
Most favored nation — right to match better future terms
Pro-rata rights — purchase more shares to avoid dilution
Preferred Shares
VC Standard Instrument
Equity with upside of common but higher claim in bankruptcy. The default security from Series A onward.
Preferred dividends — fixed or variable, paid before common
Voting rights — typically on an as-converted basis
Liquidation preference — 1×–2× money back before common
Conversion rights — convert into common shares
Redemption rights — force company to buy back shares
Common Shares
Founder & IPO Equity
Proportional ownership with the lowest claim in bankruptcy (residual claim) but full voting rights.
Pro-rata / pre-emptive — right to maintain ownership %
Right of first refusal — buy shares before outsiders
Tag-along (co-sale) — sell alongside a majority seller
Drag-along — force minority to join an approved sale
Price protection — anti-dilution in down rounds

§3.8c · Worked examples
11 / 13
Pre-money & post-money · conversion mechanics

Three worked examples.

How do valuation caps and conversion discounts translate into shares? These examples from the textbook walk through the arithmetic that determines what early investors actually receive.

Example 1 · Valuation Cap
SAFE with a $5M cap
Investor buys a SAFE for $100K with a $5M valuation cap. Series A raises $1M at a $10M pre-money valuation. 1M shares outstanding.
Series A price = $10M ÷ 1M = $10.00
SAFE price = ($5M/$10M) × $10 = $5.00
Shares = $100K ÷ $5.00 = 20,000
Post-money shares: 1,120,000
Post-money valuation: $11.2M
The cap gives the SAFE holder a 50% discount on Series A price.
Example 2 · Conversion Discount
Convertible note at 80% discount
Investor buys a convertible note for $20K with an 80% conversion discount. Series A raises $400K at $2M pre-money. 10M shares outstanding.
Series A price = $2M ÷ 10M = $0.20
Conv. price = 80% × $0.20 = $0.16
Shares = $20K ÷ $0.16 = 125,000
Post-money shares: 12,125,000
Post-money valuation: $2.425M
The 20% discount rewards early risk-taking vs. Series A investors.
Example 3 · Acquisition Exit
SAFE at acquisition ($50M offer)
Same $100K SAFE with a $5M cap. A strategic buyer offers $50M. 11.5M shares outstanding.
Buyer price = $50M ÷ 11.5M = $4.348
SAFE price = ($5M/$50M) × $4.348 = $0.870
Shares = $100K ÷ $0.870 = 115,008
Value to SAFE holder: $500,000
Money-on-money: 5.0×
$100K → $500K in a $50M exit — the cap protects early investors.

The dilution paradox

Ownership shrinks. Value grows.

A founder who owns 100% of a $0 company has nothing. A founder who owns 8% of a $10B company has $800M. Textbook example: founders dilute from 50% (seed) to 7.4% (IPO) — but stake grows from $0.5M to $2.9M (5.94×). Formula: post-money % = pre-money % × (1 − new investor %).
