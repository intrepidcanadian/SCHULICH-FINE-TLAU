Slide 01 — Cover: Funding Fintech Start-Ups 2 min
  • Welcome the class. Frame the session: today we follow money from a founder's savings account all the way to a public listing or acquisition. The capital lifecycle is what separates ideas from companies.
  • Remind students to read Chapter 3 of Fintech Explained before next week's tutorial. The textbook anchors slides 2-10; slides 11-22 are updated market data through April 2026.
  • Preview the structure: we will move from early-stage survival (Valley of Death, angels), through VC growth rounds, to exits (IPO, acquisition, and the newer tender-offer route). We close with dilution arithmetic.
  • Ask: "How many of you have seen a pitch deck before? By the end of today you will know exactly what an investor looks for in one."
Transition: "Let's start with the big picture — the three stages every fintech traverses."
Slide 02 — Three Growth Stages 4 min
  • Walk through the capital arc. Emphasize that these are not arbitrary labels — each stage has distinct investor types, risk profiles, and governance. Ask: "Why would the same company need different kinds of investors at different stages?"
  • Early Stage (Pre-Seed to Seed): bootstrapping, friends/family/fools, angels. The key constraint is survival. No revenue, maybe no product. Unpriced rounds (SAFEs/convertible notes).
  • Growth Stage (Series A-F): first priced round is the watershed moment. The company must prove product-market fit. VC firms deploy institutional capital. Each round roughly doubles valuation if milestones are hit.
  • Late Stage (Pre-IPO to exit): liquidity events. Reference Coinbase, Robinhood as IPO examples; Visa-Plaid $5.3B as an acquisition attempt (blocked on antitrust). Point out the figure on the right showing the S-curve.
  • Anchor point: emphasize that most companies die before reaching Series A. The Valley of Death kills the majority — which is our next slide.
Transition: "Most fintechs never make it past the first stage. Let's look at why — the Valley of Death."
Slide 03 — Valley of Death 4 min
  • Let the $15K per-employee burn rate land visually. Do the math live: 4 employees times $15K = $60K/month. At 18 months of runway, that is over $1M before a single dollar of revenue comes in. Ask: "Where does that million dollars come from?"
  • Define the Valley of Death: the 12-18 month gap between initial funding and reaching sustainable revenue or the next funding milestone. Most startups fail here.
  • Seed rounds typically raise $500K-$2M, selling 10-20% of the company. The goal is to build an MVP — the minimum viable product that can attract either paying users or the next tranche of capital.
  • Pose the question: "If you are burning $60K a month and you raised $1M, how many months until you are dead? What happens at month 14 if you still have no revenue?" This makes the urgency tangible.
  • Connect to fintech specifically: regulatory costs (compliance, licensing) make fintech burn rates higher than typical SaaS. A banking license application alone can cost $500K in legal fees.
Transition: "So who writes those first cheques that keep the company alive? Enter the angel investor."
Slide 04 — Angel Investors 5 min
  • Profile the typical angel: 57 years old, male (78%), former CEO or founder. They cap at ~10% of net worth, spread across ~11 investments averaging $25K each. The portfolio approach is critical — 9 in 10 investments either break even or fail outright.
  • Walk through "The Lens" — four questions every angel asks during an elevator pitch: Who is the customer? What is the pain point? Why will they pay? How does the company make money? These are the same questions students should ask when evaluating any fintech in their assignments.
  • Target return: 9-10x money-on-money over 8 years (~33% IRR). Fewer than 10% of investments generate a positive return. This is a power-law game — one big win subsidizes nine losses.
  • Mention Y Combinator as the institutional version of angel investing: 3,500+ companies funded (at time of textbook; we will update this number in slide 14). The standard YC deal compresses the angel model into a repeatable factory.
  • Ask students: "If you had $250K to angel-invest, how would you structure your portfolio? Would you write one $250K cheque or ten $25K cheques?" Drive home diversification logic in venture.
Transition: "Let's see what a successful angel pitch actually looks like. Wealthsimple's 2014 seed deck is our case study."
Slide 05 — Case Study: Wealthsimple 4 min
  • Mike Katchen, Canadian founder, pitched a simpler wealth management platform for millennials. Raised $2M from 14 angel investors in 2014. Two deck slides are on screen: industry transformations (timing argument) and competitive comparison (differentiation argument).
  • Apply "The Lens": Who? Millennials underserved by banks. What pain? High fees, minimum balances, opaque portfolios. Why pay? 0.5% fee vs. 2%+ at banks, zero minimums. How? Management fee on AUM, later expanding into trading commissions and subscription.
  • Trace the trajectory: $2M seed (2014) to $100M Series B (2020) to $5B valuation (2021) to $15B+ AUM. This is the angel dream scenario — we will revisit Wealthsimple's 2025 update in slide 15.
  • Ask: "What made this pitch work for 14 angels? Was it the idea, the founder, the timing, or the market?" Push them toward the answer: it is always some combination, but timing (post-2008 distrust of banks, smartphone adoption) was critical.
Transition: "Once a company crosses the Valley of Death, it enters the growth stage. Now the cheques get much larger, and the investors are institutions."
Slide 06 — Growth Stage: Series A through F 4 min
  • Series A is the first priced round — meaning the company gets an explicit valuation. Typically $2.5M-$5M pre-money (textbook). Product-market fit must be proven. A lead VC takes a board seat and governance becomes institutional.
  • Series B-C ($20M-$200M): unit economics validated, geographic or vertical expansion. The big names compete here — Sequoia, a16z, Accel. By this stage the company has meaningful revenue and retention metrics.
  • Series D-F ($200M-$1B+): pre-IPO mega-rounds. Crossover funds (Fidelity, T. Rowe Price) enter. Stripe's example: $2.235B raised through Series H at a $95B valuation in 2021.
  • VC portfolio math: 50% fail, 20% break even, 20% return 2-4x, 10% return 50-60x. The entire fund depends on that last decile. Sequoia ($85B AUM) has backed Klarna, Nubank, PayPal, Robinhood, Square, Stripe — the hits subsidize everything else.
  • Point to the Stripe chart on the right. Walk through how each funding round doubled or tripled the valuation. "Each round is a bet that the next milestone will be hit. What happens if it is not?" Answer: down round (we will see this with Stripe in slide 16).
Transition: "Before a VC writes a cheque, they need to believe the market is big enough. That is where TAM/SAM/SOM comes in."
Slide 07 — TAM / SAM / SOM: The VC Decision Framework 4 min
  • Three concentric circles: TAM (total addressable market — 100% capture globally), SAM (serviceable available market — reachable with current product/geography), SOM (serviceable obtainable market — realistic 3-5 year capture).
  • Use the Wise example: TAM for cross-border payments is $20T (2021). SAM narrows to $80B of the $2T individual segment (digital nomads). SOM: 50% of $80B at 0.5% take-rate = $200M revenue. That is the number VCs actually underwrite.
  • Three approaches to sizing: top-down (industry reports), bottom-up (customers x revenue per customer), theoretical (willingness-to-pay surveys). VCs want to see at least two approaches that converge.
  • Power law link: a $200M fund needs one $2B+ exit to return 10x to LPs. If SOM cannot support that outcome, the VC will not invest regardless of team quality. Ask: "Why does this explain why VCs ignore many great small businesses?"
  • Practical application: students working on their fintech project should be able to articulate TAM/SAM/SOM for their chosen company. This will appear in the midterm case analysis.
Transition: "If TAM/SAM/SOM checks out and the company scales through Series rounds, the endgame is an exit — IPO or acquisition. Let's look at how fintechs have actually performed post-IPO."
Slide 08 — IPO Performance: The Disappointing Track Record 4 min
  • This is the sobering slide. Among 13 fintech IPOs analyzed in the textbook: flat after 1 month, -29% after 6 months, -48% after 1 year. Robinhood dropped 74% in its first year. Only Adyen was positive (+55.9%, but it listed in 2018 pre-mania).
  • IPOs are expensive: ~7% underwriter fees, plus legal, accounting, road-show costs. 6-9 month insider lock-ups mean early investors cannot sell immediately, creating a cliff of supply when lock-ups expire.
  • The alternative is acquisition: JPMorgan/Nutmeg, Visa/Tink. Faster, less regulatory burden, but founders lose independence. The Visa-Plaid $5.3B deal would have been the biggest fintech acquisition, but DOJ blocked it on antitrust.
  • Ask: "Why do you think fintech IPOs underperform? Is it overhyped private valuations, or something structural about public-market investors?" Push toward the answer: private-market VCs priced in years of growth that public investors then discount once they see the actual quarterly metrics.
  • Foreshadow: "The 2025 IPO class learned from these mistakes. They priced at sober valuations. We will see whether that discipline worked in slides 12-13 and 21."
Transition: "Before we move to the 2024-2026 update, we need to understand the financial instruments that make all of this work — the securities that define who owns what."
Slide 09 — Financial Securities by Stage 5 min
  • Different stages use different instruments. Walk through each: SAFE (Simple Agreement for Future Equity) — no interest, no maturity, converts at next priced round. YC standardized this in 2013. Key provisions: valuation cap, 15-25% discount, MFN clause, pro-rata rights.
  • Convertible Note: debt that becomes equity. ~10% accrued interest, ~2-year maturity. Converts at the lower of the discount price or the cap price. Higher claim than equity in bankruptcy — this is the investor's downside protection.
  • Preferred Shares (Series A onward): the VC standard. Liquidation preference (1x-2x), anti-dilution provisions (full-ratchet vs. weighted-average), conversion to common, redemption rights. Ask: "Why would a VC demand a 1x liquidation preference? What scenario is it protecting against?"
  • Common Shares + Venture Debt: common is the residual claim (founders, employees). Venture debt (12-24 months at 15-25% p.a.) may include warrants — used to extend runway without dilution.
  • Key insight for students: the waterfall at exit flows top-down. Preferred gets paid first, then common. A company sold for $10M with $8M in liquidation preferences means common shareholders split $2M. This is why the cap table matters so much.
Transition: "Now let's see how these instruments stack up in a capitalization table — the single most important document in venture finance."
Slide 10 — The Cap Table and Dilution 4 min
  • The cap table is a live ledger of who owns what after each round. Walk through Table 3.3: at incorporation the founders own 100%. After seed (selling 20%), they own 80%. After Series A (selling another 25%), they own 60% of 80% = 60%. And so on.
  • Dilution formula: post-round ownership = pre-round ownership x (1 - new investor percentage). Work through an example on the board or whiteboard if time permits.
  • The paradox: ownership shrinks but absolute value grows. A founder at 50% of a $1M company has $500K; at 8% of a $10B company has $800M. The goal is never to preserve percentage — it is to grow the pie faster than your slice shrinks.
  • Point to Figure 3.4 on the right showing equity stake values through rounds. The lines slope down (percentage) but the dollar values go up (if the company succeeds). "This is the core trade-off every founder makes every time they raise."
  • Pro-rata rights: some investors can maintain their percentage by investing proportionally in future rounds. This protects early believers from getting diluted by later, larger investors.
Transition: "That covers the textbook foundations. Now let's bring this into the present — what has the funding climate looked like from 2024 to today?"
Slide 11 — The 2024-2026 Funding Reset 3 min
  • Walk through the bar chart: 2021 peak ($140B global fintech VC), crashed to $39B in 2023, stabilized at $43B in 2024, estimated ~$55B in 2025. The market is at roughly one-third of the 2021 peak — a "new normal" not a recovery to prior levels.
  • Two key shifts: (1) AI-native fintechs command 38% of mega-rounds in 2024 — capital is concentrating in AI-wrapped financial services. (2) ~30% of late-stage 2024 rounds were down-rounds (Klarna, Stripe, Brex, Plaid all repriced).
  • The IPO window: Klarna filed March 2025, Chime followed. The 2025 cohort raised $3.2B in combined IPO proceeds. Discipline returned — companies are listing at sober valuations rather than the 2021 mania marks.
  • Ask: "What does it mean for the industry that AI-native firms get preferential funding? What happens to the vintage neobanks that are not AI-native?" This sets up slide 17.
Transition: "Let's zoom into the IPO window specifically — four companies that defined the 2025-2026 exit cycle."
Slide 12 — Fintech IPO Window Reopens (2025-2026) 3 min
  • Four defining listings: Klarna ($15.1B, NYSE Sept 2025 — down from $45.6B peak), Chime ($11.6B, June 2025 — +37% day-one close), Circle (+168% day-one pop, USDC at $60B cap), eToro ($4.2B, Nasdaq May 2025).
  • The common thread: profitability-first. All four companies proved they could generate profit before going public. This is a 180-degree shift from the 2021 cohort that listed on growth alone.
  • Key lesson: down-round IPOs can clear if expectations are properly reset. Klarna at $15B is "only" one-third of its $45.6B private peak, but it still raised $1.37B in fresh capital and gave early investors liquidity.
  • Stripe chose NOT to IPO — the tender ladder of $91.5B (Feb 2025) → $106.7B (Sep 2025) → $159B (Feb 2026) shows there is now a credible alternative path. Tease this: "Stripe is staying private. Why? We will explore the tender-offer alternative in slide 19."
Transition: "Now let's look more closely at the IPO resurgence data and what it means for public-market discipline."
Slide 13 — 2025 IPO Resurgence (Detail) 3 min
  • Reinforce the key numbers: Klarna raised $1.37B at $40/share (closed week at $42.92); Chime at $27 with a 59% intraday pop; combined 2025 fintech IPO proceeds of $3.2B across four issuers.
  • The lesson from Chime: 8.6M active members plus profitability plus 67% revenue growth. The market rewarded fundamentals, not hype. Compare to Robinhood's 2021 IPO which had growth but no profitability — and dropped 74% in year one.
  • Stripe comparison: its latest private tender at $159B (Feb 2026, up from $91.5B in Q1 2025) exceeds every 2025 IPO valuation. The Collisons together hold a low-teens% stake; combined paper holdings >$20B at the $159B mark. This connects back to slide 10's dilution arithmetic — they diluted from ~100% to a low-teens% combined stake, but >$20B > $0.
  • Discussion prompt: "If you were advising a fintech at $10B private valuation with 30% growth, would you recommend an IPO in 2025 or a tender offer? What are the trade-offs?"
Transition: "The 2025 IPO class was largely a product of YC-era companies. Let's trace how YC itself has evolved since 2014."
Slide 14 — YC Fintech Evolution: 2014 to 2025 3 min
  • Update the textbook number: YC has now funded 5,000+ companies (up from 3,500+ cited in slide 04). In 2024 alone YC accelerated 1,000+ startups — 4x the W14 cohort that funded Wealthsimple.
  • Composition shift: ~70% of the W25 batch is building on LLMs/agents (vs. less than 5% in W14). The fintech sub-cohort is dominated by agentic operations, AI underwriting, and stablecoin rails. The "standard fintech pitch" has changed fundamentally.
  • Deal terms: $125K SAFE for 7% + $375K MFN uncapped. Total $500K. Compare to Wealthsimple raising $2M from 14 individual angels in 2014 — comparable dilution but far less friction for founders today.
  • Aggregate alumni value: $600B+ (Coinbase, Stripe, Airbnb, DoorDash, Instacart, Brex, Faire). The AI cohort drove the recovery from ~$400B in 2021. This is the power law at portfolio scale.
Transition: "Speaking of Wealthsimple — let's revisit our case study ten years later."
Slide 15 — Wealthsimple Arc: 2014 to 2025 4 min
  • The punchline: C$100B in assets under administration (Oct 2025), three years ahead of plan. Doubled from $50B in just twelve months. Series F: C$750M at C$10B post-money valuation, led by Dragoneer and GIC. Profitable since Q2 2023.
  • Walk through the timeline: $0 AUA (2014) to ~$3B (2018 Power Corp recap) to ~$15B (2021 $5B peak valuation) to C$100B (2025). The growth inflected once they expanded from robo-advisor to full banking stack: invest, save, tax, crypto, FX.
  • 3M+ Canadian clients — roughly 1 in 13 Canadians. Katchen is targeting C$1T AUA over the next decade. Ask: "Is this a reasonable target given Canada's banking oligopoly? What structural advantages does a digital-first platform have?"
  • Connect back to slide 05: those 14 angels who wrote $2M in 2014 now hold positions worth orders of magnitude more than their original cheques. This is the angel dream scenario made real with Canadian data.
  • Relevance for this class: Wealthsimple is a Toronto-headquartered company challenging the Big Five banks. Many of your classmates may end up working there or competing with them.
Transition: "Wealthsimple is the Canadian success story. For the global view of private valuation cycles, Stripe is the canonical example."
Slide 16 — Stripe Valuation Arc: 2014 to 2025 4 min
  • The cycle in one company: $1.75B (2014 Series C) to $20B (2018) to $95B mania peak (2021 Series H) to $50B down-round (2023 tender) to $91.5B (Feb 2025) to $106.7B (Sep 2025) to $159B (Feb 2026 tender). Same enterprise, private mark moved $50B → $159B in three years.
  • What drove the recovery: Bridge acquisition ($1.1B, Oct 2024) bringing stablecoin infrastructure in-house; $1.4T total payment volume in FY2024 (+38% YoY); half the Fortune 100 as customers; and the AI Agents launch (350+ partners, May 2025 Sessions).
  • The Collisons together hold a low-teens% combined stake; at $159B that is >$20B in combined paper holdings. Reference slide 22's closing: they diluted from 100% to a low-teens% combined stake, but a low-teens% of $159B massively exceeds 100% of $0.
  • Structural insight: Stripe chose NOT to IPO even at $159B. They used tender offers to provide employee liquidity without the regulatory burden and quarterly scrutiny of being public. This is a new playbook that is reshaping late-stage finance (covered in slide 19).
  • Ask: "The 2023 down-round was priced at $50B — a 47% drop. How would that affect employee morale? How would it affect the cap table?" Link to vesting, 409A valuations, and retention.
Transition: "Stripe recovered on the back of AI. That is not unique — AI-native fintechs are receiving a massive funding premium. Let's quantify it."
Slide 17 — AI-Native Fintech Funding: 2023 to 2025 4 min
  • The standout: Ramp went from $5.8B (Aug 2023) to $32B (Nov 2025) — a 5.5x private mark in 27 months. They built AI-agent expense management into the core product, not as a feature bolt-on. $1B+ ARR run-rate.
  • Mercury: $1.6B flat through 2022-23, then $3.5B (Sequoia-led, Mar 2025), reportedly targeting $5B (Apr 2026). Three years GAAP profitable. The pattern: profitability + AI narrative = multiple expansion.
  • Contrast with Brex: peaked at $12.3B in 2022, no priced up-round since. Pivoted from SMB to enterprise, had layoffs in 2023-24. Cautionary tale: being "first to market" is not enough if you do not ride the AI wave.
  • The "autonomous finance" thesis: AI-agent-led platforms command 2-3x the revenue multiples of vintage neobanks (Founders Fund / Lightspeed memos). Ask: "Is this justified, or is it a repeat of 2021 hype with an AI label?"
  • Synthesis: capital is concentrating, not expanding. The total pie ($55B in 2025) is still much smaller than 2021. But within that smaller pie, AI-native firms are getting a disproportionate share.
Transition: "These are all North American examples. For the emerging-market perspective, Nubank is the gold standard."
Slide 18 — Nubank Arc: 2014 to 2025 4 min
  • Scale: 131M customers across Brazil, Mexico, and Colombia (Q4 2025). ~58% of Brazil's adult population. From credit-card-only in 2014 to full banking stack: deposits, lending, investing, marketplace. Customer base grew 10x from 12M (2019).
  • Financial performance: $11.5B revenue FY2024 (+58% YoY FX-neutral), $1.97B net income, ROE in the 20-30% band. Eight straight profitable quarters. ARPAC ~$11, CAC ~$7 — this unit economics is exceptional for a neobank.
  • Market cap journey: $50B IPO (Dec 2021 NYSE), crashed to ~$11B trough (Jul 2022), recovered to $70B+ by 2025. Berkshire Hathaway exited Q1 2025 — stock continued to compound. This mirrors slide 08's IPO underperformance data but with a twist: Nubank recovered because it actually grew into its valuation.
  • Geographic expansion: Mexico (12M+ customers, full banking license Apr 2024), Colombia (~3M). This is the TAM/SAM/SOM framework in action — Nubank's TAM expanded as it entered new countries.
  • Lesson: emerging markets with large unbanked populations offer the biggest fintech TAMs. Brazil had 45M unbanked adults when Nubank launched. That is a structural opportunity that does not exist in Canada or the US.
Transition: "Nubank went public. Stripe stayed private. How do private companies provide liquidity without an IPO? Through secondary markets and tender offers."
Slide 19 — Secondary and Tender Markets: 2024-2026 4 min
  • The big picture: global secondary volume hit ~$240B in 2025 (up from $162B in 2024). Tender offers have replaced the IPO as the primary liquidity venue for late-stage tech. Companies can re-price every 6-12 months without going public.
  • Stripe: $50B (Mar 2023) to $91.5B (Feb 2025) to $106.7B (Sep 2025) to $159B (Feb 2026). All through tenders. +218% in three years without a single day of public trading. The cap table arithmetic of slide 10 still applies — but "exit" now includes a recurring private liquidity event.
  • OpenAI and SpaceX show the model at extreme scale: $500B+ and $350B+ respectively via tenders. This is creating a parallel private market with institutional infrastructure (Goldman Sachs Capital Solutions, Industry Ventures, Carta, Forge Global).
  • Implications for the textbook model: Chapter 3's growth stages assumed IPO or acquisition as the terminal event. In 2024-2026, many companies are "staying private indefinitely" while still providing exit paths. This changes VC fund structures (10-year fund lives may need to extend).
  • Ask: "If you are an employee at a $50B private company, how do you access your equity value? What are the constraints on selling in a secondary transaction?" (ROFR, company approval, accredited buyer requirements.)
Transition: "Not every story is about rising valuations. Plaid shows the full cycle — including the painful down-round in between."
Slide 20 — Plaid: Down-Round to IPO (2021-2026) 4 min
  • The full cycle in one company: Visa tried to acquire Plaid for $5.3B (blocked Nov 2020). Series D at $13.4B (Apr 2021). Then silence through 2022-2024. April 2025: $575M secondary at $6.1B — a 54% markdown from peak. FY2025 ARR: $546M, +40% YoY.
  • Why accept a down-round? To clean the cap table for a 2026 IPO. New investors (Franklin Templeton, BlackRock, Fidelity) at $6.1B have a better entry point. Employees and early holders get some liquidity. The company can go public at $8-10B without the baggage of a $13.4B last-round price.
  • Connect to Open Banking: CFPB Rule 1033 (Oct 2024) is a structural tailwind for Plaid. It mandates that banks share customer data with authorized third parties. Plaid connects 12,000+ fintech apps — it is the plumbing layer for open finance.
  • Ask: "Is a 54% down-round a failure? Or is it healthy discipline?" Push toward the nuance: the $13.4B was a mania-era price. The company grew revenue 3.2x while the valuation halved. The multiple compressed, but the business is stronger.
  • Preview: Plaid's projected 2026 IPO at $8-10B (15-18x FY2026 ARR) would still represent a healthy outcome for Series C investors and earlier, even though Series D investors are underwater.
Transition: "Let's close the IPO section by looking at how the entire 2025 class traded through Q1 2026 — the real report card."
Slide 21 — Class of 2025 IPOs: Q1 2026 Performance 3 min
  • The scorecard: Chime delivered its first profitable public quarter (Beat). Circle's USDC compounded under GENIUS Act tailwinds (Beat). Klarna got squeezed by rising delinquencies on its lending book (Mixed). eToro held steady, quietly profitable (Hold).
  • Key insight: the IPO is the easy day. The next four earnings prints decide whether the listing multiple sticks. Chime and Circle proved their models post-IPO. Klarna is still proving. This maps directly to slide 08's data on 1-year post-IPO returns.
  • Compare to the 2021 cohort: Robinhood -74%, Wise -64%, Marqeta -90%. The 2025 class is performing better because they priced at sober valuations. The bar was set low enough that fundamental execution (not hype) drives returns.
  • Discussion prompt: "Every issuer in 2025 priced below their 2021 private peak. Is that a bug or a feature? Who benefits from pricing discipline — the company, the public investors, or both?"
Transition: "Let's bring it all together with the single most important concept from this chapter — the dilution paradox."
Slide 22 — Closing: Ownership Shrinks, Value Grows 3 min
  • The one-liner students should remember: "A founder who owns 100% of a $0 company has nothing. A founder who owns 8% of a $10B company has $800M." Write the formula on the board: post-money % = pre-money % x (1 - new investor %).
  • Textbook example: founders dilute from 50% (seed) to 7.4% (IPO), but stake value grows from $0.5M to $2.9M (5.94x). The percentage is vanity; the dollar value is sanity.
  • Real-world proof: Stripe's Collison brothers together hold a low-teens% combined stake in a $159B company — combined paper holdings >$20B (Feb 2026). They have been diluted across eight funding rounds. They are also among the wealthiest self-made people on the planet.
