§9.1 · Payments & money-transfer jargon
04 / 31
Same money, different words — getting the vocabulary straight

Payment, transfer, remittance — not the same thing.

We’ll use “payment” loosely for the rest of the chapter, but the distinctions matter — especially once money crosses a border and a currency conversion enters the picture.

Payment. A transfer of money from one party to another in exchange for goods or services.
Method of payment. How you pay. Consumers: cash, debit, credit, checks, prepaid. Businesses: EFTs, checks, credit, bank wire. Bitcoin is the newest.
Money transfer. Moving money from one place to another — a payment, or simply shifting funds between accounts. Venmo (US), Interac e-Transfer (Canada).
Remittance. Money sent to relatives or friends abroad — often by a migrant worker supporting family back home.
Cross-border payment. Money crossing national borders. It always involves an FX transaction — one currency exchanged for another.
FX transaction & exchange rate. The exchange rate converts one currency to another; the transaction carries a fee or commission — fixed or a % of the amount.
Why the distinction earns its keep. A bank wire costs ~$35 and takes days even domestically; an overseas remittance can cost ~10% and take weeks. Each added layer — a border, a currency, an intermediary — is another fee and another delay for fintechs to attack.

§9.1 · The payment rails
05 / 31
The plumbing of the economy — sized by value, speed, and how it settles

Highways, local roads — and a real-time express lane.

Payments travel a physical network of cables, wires, and fiber — mostly government-owned and bank-run. Rails differ by value and speed, but the real distinction is how they settle: one-by-one in full, or batched and netted.

Large value · LVTS / RTGS
Like a wire — gross settlement. Every payment clears one at a time, in full, in seconds, and is final, in central-bank money. Highest value, lowest volume, costliest per payment. US: Fedwire & CHIPS · Canada: Lynx · Europe: TARGET2.
gross settlement · clears in seconds · highest cost
Small payments · ACH / ACSS
Batch & net settlement. Transactions are bundled and netted — banks offset what they owe and settle only the net, usually end-of-day. Slower (next-day+), cheapest per payment, huge volume. Built for checks, debit & credit. US: ACH · Canada: ACSS · Europe: EUCHA.
net settlement · next-day+ · lowest cost
Retail real-time · Interac e-Transfer
Where Interac fits. A consumer overlay: with Autodeposit the money lands in seconds, so it feels instant — but with low, bank-set limits (often a few thousand dollars) and, underneath, it still clears over the retail rails by deferred netting. US analogs: Zelle, Venmo. The true 24/7 rails (FedNow, UPI, Pix; Canada’s coming Real-Time Rail, built with Interac) are the upgrade — see slide 24.
instant to the user · low limits · nets underneath
Who polices the rails. US: the Federal Reserve plus federal/state regulators; Treasury’s FinCEN registers money service businesses (MSBs). Canada: the Bank of Canada oversees payments; FINTRAC enforces AML and keeps the MSB registry. Cross-border traffic passes “customs” checks to stop crime.

§9.1 · Inside Interac e-Transfer
06 / 31
Why Autodeposit lands in seconds — even though settlement is slow

Real-time message, deferred settlement.

The trick: Interac doesn’t move the money in real time — it moves an authenticated message in real time, and your bank credits you on its strength. The actual interbank money settles later, batched and netted.

How one e-Transfer actually moves
1 · send
Funds secured up front
Your bank debits and holds the money the instant you hit send — the “good-funds model.” The cash is locked before anything else happens.
2 · route
An authenticated message
Interac, acting as the clearing facility, routes a secure message bank-to-bank through its central hub — finding the recipient by their email or phone alias.
3 · credit
The recipient’s bank fronts it
On the trusted good-funds message, that bank credits the account in seconds, advancing the cash to its customer. Autodeposit drops the old security-question claim step, so it’s automatic.
4 · settle
Real money moves later
Interbank settlement happens afterward — deferred and netted through Payments Canada’s infrastructure, off the user’s critical path.
The speed is in the messaging. What lands “instantly” is the recipient’s bank advancing funds against the sender’s already-secured balance, trusting Interac’s good-funds guarantee — so the bank carries brief pre-settlement risk, which is partly why limits are low. True real-time settlement is what Canada’s Real-Time Rail will add (Payments Canada’s system; Interac as exchange provider, Mastercard’s Vocalink for settlement; phased launch targeted ~Q4 2026).

§9.1 · Clearing vs. settlement
07 / 31
Three layers people collapse into one — and where SWIFT really sits

SWIFT moves the message; settlement moves the money.

A payment is really three layers: the message (the instruction), clearing (netting out who owes whom), and settlement (moving the value, finally, in central-bank money). SWIFT is only the first — which is why “I sent it by SWIFT” is a myth.

Layer 1 · Messaging — SWIFT
Carries the instruction, not the money: “Bank A, pay Bank B $1M” (an MT103 / ISO 20022 message). ~11,000 institutions, 200+ countries. It holds no accounts, moves no value, keeps no ledger — the email between banks.
messages only · no money · no ledger
Layer 2 · Clearing — ACH / ACSS
Batches retail payments and nets them — working out the single amount each bank owes the others. Fast and cheap, but the figures are provisional: only the net settles, later. A clearing layer — not a ledger.
batch · net · provisional
Layer 3 · Settlement — Lynx / Fedwire
The value actually moves — in central-bank money, with finality — across banks’ accounts at the central bank, the real interbank ledger. RTGS does it per payment in real time; ACH’s nets land here too, just deferred.
gross · real-time · final · central-bank money
Why this is the heart of cross-border pain. There is no single global ledger. An international wire is a SWIFT message + a chain of correspondent banks (each holding accounts with the others) + each country’s domestic RTGS — many hops, each settling separately, each taking a fee and an FX spread. That friction is exactly what stablecoins and Ripple attack (slides 15 & 25).

§9.2 · Card networks
09 / 31
A private network sitting on top of the banking rails

Card networks: the four-party switch.

A switch, not a bank. The network routes the authorization and sets the rules — but it holds no deposits and keeps no ledger. The money settles between the issuing and acquiring banks over the banking rails (slide 8).
Open vs. closed loop. Visa & Mastercard are open four-party networks (separate issuers and acquirers). Amex & Discover run closed three-party loops — the network is also the issuer and the acquirer.

§9.2 · Inside a card transaction
12 / 31
The same players, now in motion · Figure 9.2 continued

Authorize in seconds, settle in days.

Every card purchase runs in two phases. Authorization just asks whether the money is there — a one-to-two-second round trip across the players. Settlement actually moves it, in end-of-day batches, over the next day or two.

Authorization · 1–2 seconds · “are the funds there?”
1You tap a card at a POS machine or enter details online — the data hits the network.
2Details go via API to the payment gateway, which encrypts them and passes them to the processor.
3The payment processor routes the request to your issuing bank — “enough credit?” (Chase Merchant Services, First Data, FIS, Moneris, WorldPay).
4The issuing bank answers yes or no — back to the processor, then the gateway.
5The gateway decrypts and tells the merchant: approved or denied — you see it, or get a receipt.
6If approved, the processor tells the merchant bank to pay the merchant. The merchant is paid; you’ve borrowed from your issuing bank.
Settlement · up to 2 business days · moving the money
1The issuing bank sends the transaction to be processed — typically batched at day’s end (hence two dates: transaction and posting).
2Funds transfer from the issuing bank to the merchant bank within one to two days.
3The merchant bank deposits the money to the merchant, minus fees — a processing cost plus the interchange fee.
4The issuing bank posts the charge to your account and bills you monthly — it has lent you the funds and carries credit exposure to you.
Why your bill shows two dates. Authorization confirms the money can move; settlement actually moves it. Because transactions clear in end-of-day batches, the posting date trails the transaction date — and in between, the issuing bank is carrying the credit.

§9.4 · The fintech value proposition
15 / 31
Beaver Inc. (Canada) pays Eagle Ltd. (US) — four routes

Faster, cheaper, transparent.

The value proposition: same payment, lower cost, clearer fees, better experience. Cross-border, every route carries a transfer fee, a conversion fee, and a bid-ask spread — but they differ sharply.

option 1
Bank wire
CA$25 sender + $10 recipient + 3% FX. CA$1,000 costs $65 (6.5%); CA$10,000 costs $335 (3.35%). Fixed costs hurt small transfers.
option 2
PayPal
Travels LVTS–ACH rails. CA$2.98 fixed + 3.5% FX; 1–5 days. Cheaper than a wire for small sums, dearer for large.
option 3
RippleNet
Private interbank network linking member banks directly — near-instant, near-zero bank cost; in principle the cheapest.
option 4
Cryptocurrency
P2P over the blockchain — but wallets, exchanges, and processors add intermediaries, hidden fees, and delays.
The strategy. Start with one use-case — a trusted means of payment, mobile money, a fair FX rate, a cheap remittance — build a foothold, then expand across the value chain to grow sticky, recurring revenue.

Update · Real-time payment rails
24 / 31
The instant, near-free rails the chapter saw coming — now mainstream

Real-time, account-to-account payments won.

The textbook described real-time gross settlement as an upgrade in progress. Since 2022 it has become the default in the world’s largest markets — displacing cards and cash, and now mandated in Europe.

US · FedNow (Fed)
1,500+ banks
Launched Jul 2023; live at 1,500+ institutions across 50 states by late 2025; per-payment cap raised $1M→$10M (Nov 2025). Still early, scaling fast.
India · UPI (NPCI)
21.6B txns / mo
Record ~21.6B transactions in Dec 2025; ~85% of India’s digital-payment volume; the IMF calls it the world’s largest real-time system (~49% of global RTP).
Brazil · Pix (BCB)
~47% of txns
~47% of all financial transactions by end-2024; used by 93% of adults / 175M+ people; cash use collapsed as Pix overtook cards.
Europe made it compulsory. The EU Instant Payments Regulation took effect 9 Oct 2025: euro-area banks must send and receive SEPA instant transfers (settled in <10 seconds, 24/7), priced no higher than a standard transfer, with mandatory Verification of Payee. The “coming” RTGS upgrade is now law.

29 / 31
When the customer is an AI agent — a new rail for machine-to-machine money

Agentic payments: x402 and the machine-money stack.

Checkout was built for a human holding a card. Autonomous agents instead need to discover, authorize, and settle payments themselves — often sub-cent micropayments for APIs, data, and compute, with no one at the keyboard. A new payment rail is forming to make that possible.

x402 · the open standard. Coinbase revived the dormant HTTP 402 “Payment Required” status code (May 2025): an agent hits a paywalled endpoint → the server returns a 402 with a price, token, and chain → the agent pays in USDC inside an HTTP header → the request is retried and settled on-chain — no accounts, API keys, or human checkout. Now a Linux Foundation standard (Apr 2026) whose members include Visa, Mastercard, Stripe, Google, AWS, Circle, Cloudflare, and Shopify.
What the companies are building
checkout
Stripe
Agentic Commerce Protocol (with OpenAI) powers ChatGPT Instant Checkout; a Shared Payment Token and single-use virtual cards (Issuing) let agents pay within set limits.
settlement L1
Tempo
Stripe + Paradigm payments blockchain — ~100k TPS, ~600ms finality, gas paid in stablecoins; mainnet Mar 2026; its Machine Payments Protocol; backers incl. Visa, Deutsche Bank, OpenAI.
crypto rail
Coinbase
Authored x402; AgentKit gives every agent an on-chain wallet; “Bazaar” lets agents discover and pay x402 services in ~200ms.
the dollar
Circle
USDC is the default settlement asset; its Agent Stack adds policy-bound agent wallets and nanopayments as small as $0.000001.
trust layer
Visa & Mastercard
Intelligent Commerce / Trusted Agent Protocol and Agent Pay / Agentic Tokens add card-network identity and fraud controls to agent transactions.
Four standards converging: ACP (commerce checkout) · Google AP2 (rail-neutral authorization) · x402 (crypto micropayments) · Stripe/Tempo MPP — with the card networks bridging into all of them. Fast-moving; specs & figures as of mid-2026.
