§9.1 · The payment rails
03 / 15
The plumbing of the economy — and control of it brings wealth

Highways for the big payments, local roads for the small.

Payments travel a physical network of cables, wires, and fiber. In most countries the rails are government-owned and were run by banks. There are separate roads by size, plus border checkpoints for anti-money laundering (AML).

Large value transfer system (LVTS) · same-day or intraday
B2B payments and securities settle bank-to-bank, increasingly at real-time gross settlement (RTGS). US: Fedwire (central bank) and CHIPS (private). Canada: Lynx. Europe: TARGET2, overseen by the ECB.
Small payments system · slower, high volume
Built for checks, credit and debit cards. US: ACH. Canada: ACSS. Europe: EUCHA. Each country controls its own network; cross-border traffic passes “customs” checks to prevent crime.
Who polices the rails. In the US, the Federal Reserve plus federal/state regulators; Treasury’s FinCEN registers money service businesses (MSBs). In Canada, the Bank of Canada oversees payments and FINTRAC enforces AML and maintains the MSB registry.

§9.2 · The payment players
04 / 15
Who touches a credit-card purchase · Figure 9.2

Many players, two steps: authorize, then settle.

Banks are everywhere — but so are non-bank money service businesses (MSBs) like Western Union, PayPal, Square, and Stripe. Authorization takes 1–2 seconds; settlement up to two business days.

The acquirer; provides the POS machine and is paid by the merchant for the sale.

Encrypts and routes transaction data to the correct network (e.g. PayPal).
delivers messages

The “postal service” between gateway and banks — First Data, FIS, WorldPay, Moneris.
does both
Combined fintechs
Adyen, PayPal, and Square act as gateway and processor in one.

The interchange (1.75%) plus assessment (0.11%) is the swipe fee. The flat $0.50 processing fee shrinks to 0.05% on a $1,000 sale.
Two engines of revenue
Fees. Interchange, assessment, processing, plus account, maintenance, NSF, and trade-finance fees. Cross-border adds an FX conversion fee — up to 3% for consumers, often hidden in the rate.
Risk income. Banks earn the bid-ask spread on volatile FX, plus returns for bearing liquidity and credit risk — small per trade, vast across trillions.
Paytm’s take rate. Payment-processing revenue = GMV × take rate. On $137B of GMV, $159M of revenue — a take rate of just 12 bps.

§9.4 · The fintech value proposition
07 / 15
Beaver Inc. (Canada) pays Eagle Ltd. (US) — four routes

Faster, cheaper, transparent.

The value proposition: same payment, lower cost, clearer fees, better experience. Cross-border, every route carries a transfer fee, a conversion fee, and a bid-ask spread — but they differ sharply.

option 1
Bank wire
CA$25 sender + $10 recipient + 3% FX. CA$1,000 costs $65 (6.5%); CA$10,000 costs $335 (3.35%). Fixed costs hurt small transfers.
option 2
PayPal
Travels LVTS–ACH rails. CA$2.98 fixed + 3.5% FX; 1–5 days. Cheaper than a wire for small sums, dearer for large.
option 3
RippleNet
Private interbank network linking member banks directly — near-instant, near-zero bank cost; in principle the cheapest.
option 4
Cryptocurrency
P2P over the blockchain — but wallets, exchanges, and processors add intermediaries, hidden fees, and delays.
The strategy. Start with one use-case — a trusted means of payment, mobile money, a fair FX rate, a cheap remittance — build a foothold, then expand across the value chain to grow sticky, recurring revenue.

§9.5 · Fintechs disrupting payments
08 / 15
One pain point each — trust, access, FX, remittances, cash

Five solutions to five customer pain points.

From a single use-case to a platform
trust · China
Alipay
Escrow until goods arrive created trust in e-commerce. 1.3B users; 50%+ of China mobile payments. Spun into Ant Group.
access · Kenya
M-PESA
“Mobile money” for the unbanked via agents. 52M customers in 7 countries; 90% Kenya penetration; 19B transactions.
FX · UK
Wise
Mid-market rates, one transparent flat fee. Up to 8× cheaper than UK banks; 56 currencies. $11B 2021 listing.
remittance · UK
WorldRemit
100% cashless, $3.99 fee + 1% FX; 90%+ in minutes. Send from 50, receive in 130+ countries via M-PESA, MTN, bKash.
cashless · HK
Octopus
Tap-and-go stored-value card. Used by 98% of residents; 33M cards; accepted by 140,000 merchants; 15M txns/day.
Incumbents fight back. Partnerships, consortiums, and acquisitions: eight US banks built Zelle to rival Venmo; JPMorgan bought WePay; Fiserv took First Data, FIS took WorldPay; VISA seeded Square, Stripe, and Klarna. Timing helped too — smartphones, RTGS, NFC/QR, stronger authentication, and open-banking rules.

§9.6 · The rise of insurtech
09 / 15
A vast profit pool ripe for disruption · Figure 9.3

A $6 trillion industry — in gross written premiums.

Premiums totaled US$5.7 trillion in 2020, growing ~4% a year since 2010 but slowing. The best opportunities are in emerging markets — Asia Pacific, Latin America, Africa. Insurers price risk, collect premiums, and pay claims.

Life insurance
42% of premiums
Lump sum to beneficiaries on disability or premature death of the insured.
Property & casualty
31% of premiums
Property covers fire, theft, weather; casualty covers auto, theft, and liability.
Health insurance
27% of premiums
Covers medical treatment, drugs, and checkups. Plus two B2B pools: distribution & reinsurance.
Why it’s ripe for disruption. Crude average-based pricing, paper-based onboarding and claims, costly distribution, and poor service — products generic and commoditized. Complexity, regulation, and the need for scale built high barriers that protected incumbents.

§9.8 · The insurance value chain
11 / 15
Figure 9.5 · Two customer-facing steps, two back-office steps

Where insurtechs attack the chain.

Technology alone is no moat — it can be replicated, licensed, or acquired. The industry “does not need hundreds of insurtechs that do the same thing, worse.” Disruptors must win a specific step of the chain.

Customer-facing
step 1
Product design & pricing
Use historical data and statistical models to set a premium that earns a profit while paying expected claims.
step 2
Quote, bind & issue
Customer gets a quote, buys the policy, and the agent puts coverage in place.
step 3
Administration
Records management — policy, customer, and beneficiary details, billing, and changes over time.
step 4
Claims & settlement
Investigate, assess damage, contract repairs, and disburse cash — where the rubber hits the road.
Back-office →
Why scale wins. Underwriting needs large pools — premiums from non-paying policies subsidize the minority that pay out. Concentrated risk relies on portfolio diversification, so insurance does not lend itself to a P2P model.

§9.9 · Customer-facing insurtechs
12 / 15
Personalization from sensors, biometrics, IoT & machine learning

Pricing the individual, not the average.

Root · usage-based auto. Smartphone sensors measure acceleration, braking, cornering, speed, and route consistency to score driving behavior. Because 30% of drivers cause ~45% of accident costs, Root insures only the safer drivers — and prices them lower.

§9.10 · Emerging risks & the back office
13 / 15
New risks to underwrite — and old processes to automate

From cyber risk to three-second claims.

Emerging risks · Cyence. Cyber-attacks cost the insurance industry over $1 trillion a year, but P&C insurers lacked the claims history to price them. Founded 2014, Cyence fused cybersecurity, data science, and economics with internet-scale data and machine learning. Raised $40M Series A; acquired by Guidewire for $275M in 2017. Peers: Climate Corporation, Traffk.
Back office · Snapsheet. Photo-based auto claims: computer vision returns a repair estimate in 2.7 hours on average, and 90% of auto claims are handled electronically within 30 days — where rivals take weeks to months. Founded 2011; $100M raised; 100+ carrier partners. Peers: Shift Technology, Clara Analytics.

Onboarding
Faster applications and KYC via automation and smart contracts.
Underwriting
Optimized pricing and portfolio risk with data science and ML.
Claims
AI triage, fraud detection, and customer comms by chatbot and video.
HR & benefits
Cloud HR and payroll — Zenefits, Gusto, Namely.

Chapter 09 · Closing

Research findings · attacking complex, regulated profit pools

Solve a pain point, automate the processing.

Fintechs can take the profit pools of payments and insurance — both complex, regulated, and fragmented. They start with a single use-case, build a foothold, and expand across the value chain. The winners create trust, cut friction, and reach the underserved — but many burn cash for years, and high-profile names like Paytm and ZhongAn remain deep in losses. The business model, not the technology, decides who survives.
