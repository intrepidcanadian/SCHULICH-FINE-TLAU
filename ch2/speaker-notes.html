Chapter 2: Theories & Business Models — Speaker Notes

FINE 6280 · Fintech · Schulich School of Business · Graduate Seminar · Lecture 02 of 14

Timing Overview (target: ~75 minutes total) Slides 01-09 (theoretical foundations): ~35 min · Slides 10-20 (2024-2026 data updates & cases): ~35 min · Slide 21 (synthesis & closing): ~5 min
01 Cover ~2 min
  • Welcome students. Frame today's lecture: we are building the analytical toolkit that will be applied to every case for the rest of the term. Three bodies of theory working together.
  • Remind them of the reading assignment: Chapter 2 of Fintech Explained. Ask by show of hands who has completed it — calibrate depth accordingly.
  • Set expectations: the first half is conceptual (intermediation theory, Christensen, Pisano, business models). The second half updates these frameworks with 2024-2026 data. By the end, they should be able to diagnose any fintech's strategy using today's frameworks.
  • Pose the driving question: "Why does finance remain expensive despite 130 years of technology? And what, if anything, has changed in the last three years?"
Transition: "Let's start by mapping the three intellectual traditions we'll draw on today."
02 A Multidisciplinary Approach ~3 min
  • Walk through the three "analytical stops" — finance (intermediation), strategy (innovation), and IO (business models). Emphasize that no single discipline is sufficient: a pure finance lens misses platform dynamics; a pure strategy lens misses regulatory capital constraints.
  • Give a concrete preview: "When we look at Stripe later, we'll need all three lenses — intermediation theory explains why payment processing exists, Christensen explains how Stripe displaced legacy gateways, and platform economics explains their multi-sided pricing."
  • Ask the class: "Which lens do you default to in your own analysis? What's your blind spot?" (brief 30-second pair-share if energy is right).
  • Note the Philippon (2015) citation in Stop 01 — flag that we'll return to this with a full data slide shortly.
Transition: "Let's start with Stop 01 — financial intermediation. Why do intermediaries exist at all?"
03 Five Dimensions of Financial Intermediation ~5 min
  • Walk through each dimension slowly. For each, give one fintech example: (i) Information asymmetry — alt-data lending (Upstart); (ii) Transaction costs — APIs and smart contracts (Stripe); (iii) Liquidity creation — neobanks still needing deposit insurance; (iv) Risk sharing — parametric insurance (Lemonade); (v) Trust — blockchain substituting cryptographic verification for reputational capital.
  • Key insight to emphasize: fintech does NOT eliminate these frictions — it re-prices them. The intermediary function persists; the question is who performs it and at what cost.
  • Draw attention to Fig. 2.1. Ask students to identify which dimension a particular fintech they know attacks.
  • Tie to academic lineage: Akerlof (1970), Coase (1937), Diamond & Dybvig (1983), Allen & Gale (1997). For MBA students less familiar with these, a one-sentence summary of each is sufficient.
  • Pose question: "If you were starting a fintech today, which dimension has the most remaining inefficiency?"
Transition: "If these frictions are so persistent, what does the data actually show about the cost of intermediation over time? Let's look at a stunning empirical finding."
04 The ~2% Puzzle (Philippon 2015) ~4 min
  • Let the big number land. Pause on the stat. This is the single most provocative finding in fintech research: 130 years of technology — ATMs, electronic trading, the internet — and the unit cost of intermediation hasn't budged.
  • Explain the implication: "The finance industry has become more efficient at extracting rents, not at lowering them." Technology improved quality but the savings were captured by intermediaries.
  • Facilitate a 2-minute discussion: "If 130 years of technology haven't lowered the cost, what makes this time different? Is the fintech era genuinely different, or will costs simply re-anchor at 2%?" Push for specific mechanisms (competition, open banking, AI cost compression).
  • Foreshadow: "We'll revisit this question on Slide 13 with 2024-2025 data. Spoiler: the answer is nuanced — some segments have compressed, but the aggregate is stubborn."
Transition: "Now that we understand what intermediaries do and what they cost, let's turn to Stop 02 — innovation theory. How do new entrants dislodge these incumbents?"
05 Disruptive Innovation (Christensen 1997) ~5 min
  • Walk through the four-step process methodically. For each step, use the fintech example on the slide: (1) JPMorgan's $15.3B tech spend optimizing for institutions, not the underbanked; (2) M-Pesa targeting non-consumers with SMS payments; (3) Robinhood improving from zero-commission stocks into options, crypto, retirement; (4) Neobanks eroding branch economics.
  • Distinguish sustaining vs. disruptive innovation explicitly. Common student error: "Uber is disruptive." It's not — it targeted existing taxi customers. Goldman's Marcus is sustaining. Stripe is disruptive (redefined the performance axis to developer experience).
  • Give Christensen's strategic advice for incumbents: respond with a separate business unit. Vanguard's 2015 robo-advisor is the canonical correct response — captured 50%+ AUM growth in two years by cannibalizing itself.
  • Ask: "Can anyone think of a fintech that started disruptive but has since become a sustaining innovator?" (Hint: PayPal, Square/Block)
Transition: "Christensen gives us the disruption trajectory. Pisano adds a second dimension — the type of technical and business-model novelty involved."
06 Pisano's Innovation Landscape Map ~4 min
  • Present the 2x2 matrix: x-axis is business model novelty, y-axis is technical competence novelty. Walk through each quadrant with the fintech example: Routine (incremental bank digitization), Disruptive (LendingClub — existing credit scoring, new P2P marketplace model), Radical (blockchain settlement — new tech, same clearing function), Architectural (DeFi/Uniswap — new tech AND new model).
  • Key insight: the quadrant determines the organizational response. Routine innovation can be handled in-house. Architectural innovation requires new organizational capabilities AND new business models — hardest for incumbents.
  • Ask students: "Where would you place each of these: Wealthsimple, Plaid, Compound Finance, Apple Pay?" This is a good diagnostic exercise — have them justify placement.
  • Connect back to Christensen: Pisano's "disruptive" quadrant is where Christensen's framework applies most cleanly. But "architectural" innovation is actually harder to respond to because incumbents lack both the technical and organizational capabilities.
Transition: "We've covered why intermediaries exist and how entrants attack them. Now Stop 03: once a fintech gains traction, what business model captures value?"
07 Business Models: One-Sided vs. Multi-Sided ~4 min
  • Frame the fundamental architectural choice. One-sided: traditional bank, insurer, asset manager — linear value chain (originate, hold, service). Revenue from spread/premium/AUM fee. Multi-sided: platform connecting 2+ distinct user groups whose interactions create value.
  • Use the Borrowell example for Canadian context: free credit scores for consumers (subsidy-side), commissions from banks selling products (money-side). Students will know this company.
  • Make the historical point: multi-sided platforms are not new — newspapers, stock exchanges, and shopping malls are all MSPs. What's new is the scalability of digital platforms and winner-take-most dynamics.
  • Ask: "Is a traditional bank truly one-sided? Or is it multi-sided (depositors on one side, borrowers on the other)?" This is a productive conceptual debate — the answer is that banks are proto-platforms but historically priced as one-sided (both sides pay).
  • Preview: next slide dives into the strategic mechanics — network effects and subsidy/money-side pricing.
Transition: "The defining strategic challenge for any platform is solving the chicken-and-egg problem. How do you get both sides on board? That's where network effects and pricing structure come in."
08 Network Effects & Platform Pricing ~4 min
  • Distinguish same-side (direct) from cross-side (indirect) effects clearly. Venmo = same-side (each new user makes the app more useful to existing users; Metcalfe's law). Visa = cross-side (more cardholders attract merchants, which attract more cardholders).
  • Explain the Rochet & Tirole (2003) insight: the price LEVEL matters less than the price STRUCTURE. You can raise total fees and lose volume, or cut one side's price and grow both sides. This is the key strategic insight for platform fintechs.
  • Robinhood as the canonical case: zero commission trades (subsidy side = retail investors), PFOF revenue from Citadel ($810M in 2021, money side = market makers). The product is free but the platform earns more per user than E*Trade ever did.
  • Three strategic findings from the literature: subsidize marquee users, limit access to create scarcity, and combat multihoming (prevent users from using multiple platforms). Ask: "How does Robinhood combat multihoming?" (Answer: Gold subscription, cash management, crypto — lock-in through breadth).
Transition: "Once you've solved the chicken-and-egg and have both sides on the platform, the question becomes: how do you actually monetize? That's the agency vs. principal distinction."
09 Monetization: Agency vs. Principal Revenue ~4 min
  • Draw the fundamental split clearly: Agency = fee/commission revenue (transaction fees, interchange, advisory fees, PFOF, subscription). Capital-light, scalable, low regulatory burden. Principal = spread/gain revenue (net interest income, trading gains, underwriting profit). Requires regulatory capital, balance sheet risk.
  • Use Stripe vs. SoFi as the contrast: Stripe earns 2.9% + $0.30 per transaction (pure agency). SoFi, post-bank-charter, earns NIM of 5.9% (principal). SoFi carries credit risk; Stripe does not.
  • Note JPMorgan derives ~65% from principal activities. The irony: fintechs that started as pure agency players (Robinhood, SoFi, Nubank) are migrating toward principal revenue. Why? Higher margins, stickier deposits, and a rate environment that rewards balance-sheet strategies.
  • Foreshadow Slide 15: "We'll see exactly how dramatically this mix shifted in 2021-2025 when the Fed rate cycle inverted the economics."
  • Ask: "What are the regulatory implications of moving from agency to principal? What changes when you become a bank?" (Capital requirements, deposit insurance, supervisory burden, but also access to the Fed window and stable NIM.)
Transition: "That completes our theoretical toolkit. Now let's stress-test these frameworks against the most current data — starting with the biggest disruption vector of 2024-2026: AI agents."
10 AI Agents: A New Disruption Vector (2024-2026) ~4 min
  • Apply Christensen's four steps explicitly to AI agents: (1) Incumbents overshoot — wealth managers serve $1M+ AUM clients with $10K+ fees, mass-affluent underserved; (2) Low-end entry — Morgan Stanley GPT-4 copilot automates 80% of analyst prep, cost per query under $0.05; (3) Move upmarket — Klarna AI handles 2.3M chats, replaces 700 FTEs, saves $40M/year; (4) Displace — agent-to-agent payments emerging (Stripe Agents, Visa Intelligent Commerce).
  • Hit the three data points hard: Klarna $40M/year savings, Morgan Stanley 16K financial advisors augmented, JPMorgan 60K seats on their LLM Suite. These are not pilots — these are production deployments at scale.
  • Key conceptual point: AI agents are targeting the most overserved tier with a 10x cost structure that incumbents cannot match without dismantling their own labor model. This is textbook Christensen.
  • Ask the class: "If you're a wealth management firm with 5,000 financial advisors, what is your correct strategic response? Separate unit? Augment existing advisors? Wait and see?"
Transition: "AI agents aren't just a cost play — they're creating entirely new platform sides. Let's look at agent-to-agent commerce."
11 Agentic Commerce 2025-26 ~3 min
  • Frame the paradigm shift: multi-sided platforms just got a third side. Previously it was consumers and merchants. Now AI agents are an autonomous transacting party. Stripe Agents processes $50B+ run-rate volume routed by AI agents.
  • Visa Intelligent Commerce (Apr 2025): Anthropic, OpenAI, Microsoft as partners. Agents can transact within consumer-set guardrails. This is not a concept — it launched with 100+ partners.
  • Connect to business model theory from Slide 07-08: Who is the subsidy side in a three-sided platform? Who is the money side? The answer isn't obvious and is still being determined by the market.
  • ChatGPT shopping with 700M weekly active users converting the chat surface into a new acquisition funnel above merchants — this disintermediates Google Shopping, not just banks.
Transition: "Agentic commerce is one expression of a broader trend: embedded finance. Financial services are becoming SDK calls, not destinations."
12 Embedded Finance & BaaS (2025 snapshot) ~3 min
  • Frame embedded finance through Christensen's "jobs to be done": financial services are no longer destinations, they're SDK calls inside vertical SaaS, marketplaces, and AI agents. The consumer doesn't go to a bank; the bank comes to them inside Shopify, Toast, or Uber.
  • Key numbers: $148B global revenue in 2025, up 36% from $108.6B in 2024. Stripe processed $1.4T in 2024 — approximately 1.3% of global GDP. The platform opportunity is $185B (BCG x Adyen estimate).
  • The 350+ banks and fintechs in production AI-agent commerce by end-2025 signals this is past the experimentation phase. Stripe Agents, Visa Intelligent Commerce, Mastercard Agentic Tokens are all live.
  • Ask: "What does this mean for the traditional bank branch? For the bank's digital app? If finance is embedded in every platform, what remains of the standalone banking relationship?"
Transition: "Let's return to our 2% puzzle from Slide 04. Ten years after Philippon, has fintech actually moved the needle?"
13 The 2% Puzzle Revisited (2015-2025) ~4 min
  • Call back explicitly to Slide 04. The U.S. unit cost has come down marginally — from ~2% to ~1.7% (NYU Stern updated series). That's progress but not revolution.
  • The nuance is distributional: some segments compressed dramatically (EU retail FX spread down 80% thanks to Wise and Revolut; stablecoin L2 transfers at $0.04), while others held firm (custody, asset management, core payments infrastructure).
  • Remittance fees: down from 7.5% to 6.4% globally, but mobile-money corridors (Wise, Remitly) average 3.4%. The SDG target of 3% is still unmet — ask why.
  • The critical insight: "compute savings get captured by intermediaries, not consumers." Even as marginal costs approach zero on-chain, the wedge accrues to wallets and on-ramps. This validates Philippon's original thesis — technology benefits intermediaries first.
  • Discussion prompt: "Given this data, are you optimistic or pessimistic that the 2% will finally break? What would it take?"
Transition: "Let's update our network effects framework — two-sided platforms are evolving into agent-sided commerce."
14 Agent-Sided MSP Evolution (2025-26) ~3 min
  • Connect directly to Slide 08's two-sided platform theory. Visa was the textbook two-sided platform (cardholders and merchants). In April 2025, Visa Intelligent Commerce opened rails to a THIRD side: autonomous AI agents acting on consumers' behalf.
  • Mastercard launched Agent Pay in parallel with Shared Payment Tokens. 30+ partners building in Visa's sandbox by Q4 2025. 47% of US shoppers already using AI tools for at least one shopping task.
  • Theoretical question for the class: "Does Rochet & Tirole's pricing framework still hold with three sides? Who gets subsidized? The consumer, the merchant, or the agent?" This is genuinely unsettled — good exam question territory.
  • Klarna context: 2.3M customer chats in one month, IPO at $15B in Sep 2025. The AI-native fintech is being valued as a platform company, not a BNPL lender.
Transition: "Now let's see how the agency vs. principal revenue split from Slide 09 actually played out in real broker P&Ls over the last three years."
15 Broker Revenue Mix Shift (2021-2025) ~4 min
  • This is the empirical payoff for Slide 09's theory. Call back explicitly: "Remember the agency vs. principal split? Here's what happened in practice." Three years of Fed hikes fundamentally inverted the revenue mix at Robinhood, SoFi, Coinbase, and Nubank.
  • Robinhood: from 75% PFOF/crypto in 2021 to NII of $1.10B (37% of revenue) by FY2024. Gold cash-sweep now holds $150B+ in deposits. SoFi: bank charter in 2022 took deposits from $1B to $26B — a 26x increase enabling principal-heavy NIM.
  • Key thesis: "The agency-only fintech archetype is largely extinct." Every successful fintech is migrating toward principal revenue because the unit economics are superior in a positive rate environment.
  • Nubank is the most dramatic case: ~131M customers (Q4 2025), $9.5B NII and $2.0B net income (FY2024), 30%+ ROE. It went from interchange-led and unprofitable to Latin America's largest financial institution by users.
  • Ask: "What happens to this thesis when rates come down? Does the mix shift reverse?" (Partially, but deposits are sticky and bank charters are permanent.)
Transition: "Let's look at another real-world test of our network effects theory — the P2P payments war between Zelle, Venmo, and Cash App."
16 P2P Payments Evolution (2020-2025) ~3 min
  • Call back to Slide 08: we used Venmo as the canonical same-side network effects example. Five years later, the bank-owned Zelle has won the volume war ($1.2T vs. $340B Venmo). But Venmo has more mindshare among younger users. Distribution channel (your bank app vs. your peer group) shapes which network effect loop wins.
  • Theoretical insight: direct network effects compound, but the DISTRIBUTION mechanism determines which loop wins. Zelle won because it's embedded in 145M+ bank accounts — users don't need to download anything. Venmo won the social graph but lost on volume.
  • FedNow (launched Jul 2023) as the infrastructure layer beneath all of this. 1,400+ banks live, 1B+ cumulative transactions. This is the rails that neutralizes speed-based competitive advantages.
  • Ask: "Zelle has 54.6% volume share but Venmo has 97M MAU. Who 'won'? Does it matter?" This surfaces the difference between volume-based and engagement-based platform metrics.
Transition: "P2P payments illustrate network effects in action. Now let's look at a regulatory intervention that attacks information asymmetry directly — CFPB Rule 1033."
17 Open Banking: CFPB Rule 1033 (2024-2026) ~4 min
  • Connect directly to Slide 03's information asymmetry dimension. Rule 1033 attacks it legislatively: consumer access to financial data becomes a federally enforceable right via authorized third-party APIs. This is the US catching up to the UK's PSD2/Open Banking framework.
  • Timeline matters: finalized Oct 22, 2024. First compliance wave (banks >$250B, the top ~14 institutions) by April 2026. Full rollout through 2030. But litigation (Bank Policy Institute lawsuit) and administration change have reopened the rule for amendment.
  • Impact framework: banks become data holders with mandatory API obligations; aggregators (Plaid, MX) become authorized "representatives" with statutory access rights; fintechs get lower customer acquisition costs but face data-use limits (no secondary marketing).
  • For Schulich students: compare to Canada's Consumer-Directed Finance framework (still voluntary). Ask: "Should Canada follow the US's mandatory approach or the UK's outcomes-based approach?"
  • The 100M+ active users already connected via Plaid/MX/Finicity means this codifies existing practice — but forces migration from screen-scraping to permissioned APIs.
Transition: "We've seen AI reshape cost structures and regulation reshape data access. Let's look at the best single case study of AI in fintech operations: Klarna."
18 Klarna AI Hybrid Model (2023-2026) ~4 min
  • This is the most complete AI-in-finance case study we have. Frame it as a three-act story: Act 1 (2023-24) — "AI replaces customer service," hiring freeze, 700-FTE workload absorbed. Act 2 (early 2025) — cost-per-transaction falls 40% ($0.32 to $0.19), workforce shrinks 40%. Act 3 (mid-2025) — CEO admits "gone too far," quality degraded on complex/emotional cases, human agents rehired.
  • The unit economics are real AND the quality ceiling is real. This is not a failure story — it's a maturation story. The hybrid model (AI handles triage and volume, humans own the long tail) is likely the equilibrium for the next 2-3 years.
  • Connect to Christensen: AI entered at the low end (simple FAQ queries), moved upmarket (handling 2/3 of all conversations), then hit the quality ceiling on the most complex cases. The disruption trajectory stalled partway up.
  • Key question for the class: "At what point does cost reduction from AI get competed away? If every BNPL provider deploys AI customer service, does Klarna retain a cost advantage?" (First-mover advantage in training data and process optimization may be durable.)
  • Klarna IPO at $15B (Sep 2025) — the market valued the hybrid model, not the "replace all humans" model.
Transition: "Klarna shows AI in operations. Let's look at AI in the advisory/wealth space — robo-advisors 2.0."
19 Robo-Advisor 2.0: AI Wealth (2024-2026) ~3 min
  • Frame the evolution: a decade after Vanguard's incumbent counter-attack, the "robo" layer has moved from low-fee allocation to AI tax-loss harvesting at the individual-stock level. Wealthfront at $90B AUM filed for IPO (WLTH). Betterment at $65B AUM acquiring for direct-indexing capabilities.
  • The wedge has shifted: it's no longer about 0.25% pricing (that's table stakes). It's about AI-driven tax-loss harvesting, goal-based personalization, and CFP-on-demand. Direct indexing (replacing an S&P 500 ETF with 500 underlying stocks) creates vastly more tax-alpha opportunities.
  • Canadian context: Wealthsimple at C$100B+ AUA — the "super-app" pivot (trading, crypto, tax, mortgages) beat the pure-robo path. Students will know this company well.
  • Incumbent counter: Vanguard PAS >$300B, Schwab Intelligent >$80B. Incumbents still own the largest pools, but marginal customers are going to AI-personalized challengers. This is Christensen in slow motion — incumbents retain the installed base while challengers win net flows.
Transition: "Let's pull the camera back one more time on embedded finance — the year-over-year trajectory tells the story of where the industry is going."
20 Embedded Finance YoY (2024-2026) ~3 min
  • The trajectory: $108.6B (2024) to $148B (2025) to $173B (2026). 26-31% blended CAGR — faster than every other fintech sub-sector. Projected $454B by 2031. This is the macro trend that subsumes many of the individual stories we've discussed.
  • Break down by layer: embedded payments (~$92B by 2026, +59%), embedded lending (~$36B, +64%), BaaS/banking infra (~$30B, +36%), embedded insurance + wealth (~$15B, +88%). Insurance and wealth are the fastest-growing segments — small base, large opportunity.
  • Key structural shift: merchants and SaaS platforms (not banks) are now the primary distribution layer for financial services. This is the platform theory from Slide 07-08 in action at industry scale.
  • Tie it all together: "Every theory we discussed today — intermediation, disruption, platform economics — converges on this trend. Embedded finance IS the unbundling of the five intermediation dimensions, delivered through multi-sided platforms, following Christensen's trajectory upmarket."
Transition: "Let me bring this all together with a synthesis framework you can apply to any case this term."
21 Closing: Disruption Vulnerability ~5 min
  • Synthesize with McKinsey's four vulnerability signs: (1) cross-subsidizing customers, (2) forced bundle buying, (3) delivery mismatched to demand, (4) experience that lags global best practice. For each, ask students to name an incumbent that exhibits the sign.
  • Recap the three numbers: 130 years of ~2% intermediation cost, 4 steps in Christensen's model, 2 sides to every platform. These three facts are the diagnostic toolkit for the rest of the course.
  • Give the course roadmap: subsequent chapters apply this toolkit — funding (Ch. 3), valuation (Ch. 4), crypto & blockchain (Ch. 5), Ethereum & DeFi (Ch. 6). For every vertical, the question is: which dimension of intermediation is under attack, and what business model is the attacker using?
  • Final question to leave them with: "Pick one financial product you personally use. Apply today's framework: which of the five intermediation dimensions does it rely on? Where on Pisano's map is the most likely attacker? What would the disruptive price structure look like?"
  • Remind them: next class is Chapter 3, Funding Fintech Start-Ups. Reading due before next session.
End of lecture. Invite questions. Office hours reminder.
