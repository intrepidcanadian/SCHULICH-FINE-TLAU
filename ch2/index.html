Three analytical stops

A multidisciplinary lens.

Understanding fintech requires three bodies of theory working in concert — financial intermediation, innovation strategy, and business-model design. Each supplies a distinct diagnostic question; together they form the analytical engine for every case we examine this term.

Stop 01 · Finance
Financial Intermediation
Why do intermediaries exist? Five dimensions — information asymmetry, transaction costs, liquidity creation, risk sharing, and trust — explain the enduring ~2% cost of intermediation (Philippon 2015). Fintech attacks each dimension differently.
Stop 02 · Strategy
Innovation Theory
Christensen’s disruptive innovation (1997) and Pisano’s innovation landscape map (2015) answer how new entrants dislodge incumbents — and why most incumbents respond too late.
Stop 03 · Industrial Organization
Business Models
One-sided vs. multi-sided platforms, network effects, subsidy-side / money-side dynamics (Rochet & Tirole 2003), and the agency-vs.-principal revenue split.
The multidisciplinary approach is essential because fintech sits at the intersection of finance, technology, and strategy. No single discipline is sufficient.

Who are the intermediaries?

Six types of financial intermediary.

The financial system features a bewildering array of intermediaries fulfilling many different functions. Fintech is challenging each of them — sometimes by replacing them, sometimes by making them better.

01
Banks & Credit Unions
Accept deposits and extend loans; some focus on commercial clients, some on individuals, and some on a specific type of loan.
02
Brokers & Dealers
Facilitate the buying and selling of stocks, bonds, and other financial assets.
03
Investment Banks
Help corporations to issue bonds or equity or to acquire other companies.
04
Asset (or Money) Managers
Pool savings and invest in a portfolio of securities on behalf of investors. Includes pension funds, mutual funds, hedge funds, private equity firms, and VCs.
05
Insurance Companies
Collect premiums from policy holders in exchange for covering the cost if some bad event occurs.
06
Exchanges
Run the infrastructure of financial markets — exchanges, clearinghouses, and depositories that enable price discovery and settlement.

§2.2 · Financial intermediation
05 / 15
Why intermediaries persist

Five dimensions of financial intermediation.

Financial intermediaries exist because of five persistent frictions. Fintech does not eliminate these frictions — it re-prices them.

i. Information Asymmetry
One party knows more than the other
Leads to adverse selection (hidden info before a deal) and moral hazard (hidden action after). The 2008 crisis was moral hazard at scale — bank employees took risks, shareholders bore losses. Fintechs increase transparency but face the same problems (FTX).
Akerlof (1970) · Stiglitz & Weiss (1981)
ii. Transaction Costs
Search, bargaining, and enforcement
Direct costs (commissions, fees) and indirect costs (enforcement, regulation). Coase argued firms exist to internalise these. Despite 130 years of technology, unit cost of intermediation has stayed at ~2% — gains captured as higher wages, not lower prices.
Coase (1937) · Philippon (2015)
iii. Liquidity Creation
Deposit-to-loan & maturity transformation
Banks create liquidity three ways: converting deposits into loans, providing payment methods, and market making. Maturity transformation — funding 30-year mortgages with demand deposits — creates a mismatch that justifies the intermediary’s spread.
Diamond & Dybvig (1983)
iv. Risk Sharing
Diversification and hedging across agents
Intermediaries get paid to manage market, credit, liquidity, and operational risk. Tools include derivatives and insurance. Fintech has expanded data, models, and processing power to price and share risk — parametric insurance and DeFi pools are new mechanisms, same function.
Allen & Gale (1997)
v. Trust
Institutional trust vs. algorithmic trust
Historically a relationship business built on repeated interactions, underpinned by rule of law, regulation, deposit insurance, and lender of last resort. Blockchain proposes substituting cryptographic verification for reputational capital — but institutional trust still dominates.

§2.2b · The persistence puzzle
06 / 15
Philippon (2015) · a puzzling constant
~2%

The unit cost of financial intermediation in the United States has remained roughly constant at ~2% of financial transactions for over 130 years — despite ATMs, electronic trading, and the internet.

Technology has improved the quality of financial services, but has not reduced the unit cost. The finance industry has become more efficient at extracting rents, not at lowering them.
— Thomas Philippon, American Economic Review (2015)

§2.3 · Disruptive innovation
07 / 15
Christensen (1997) · applied to financial services

Disruptive innovation.

Christensen’s four-step process explains how inferior products in overlooked market segments eventually overtake incumbents. The pattern recurs across fintech verticals.

The four-step disruption process
Step 01
Incumbents overshoot
Incumbent products exceed what mainstream customers need. JPMorgan’s $15.3B tech spend (2023) optimises for institutional clients, not the underbanked.
Step 02
Entrant targets low-end
New entrants serve over-served or non-consumers with simpler, cheaper products. M-Pesa (2007): SMS-based payments for the unbanked in Kenya.
Step 03
Entrant improves upward
Product quality improves until it meets mainstream needs. Robinhood: zero-commission trades attracted 23M+ accounts by 2023, then added options, crypto, retirement.
Step 04
Incumbent displaced
Incumbents retreat to shrinking premium segments. Netflix vs. Blockbuster (2010). In finance: neobanks eroding branch economics.
Sustaining innovation
Improves existing products along dimensions incumbents already value. Goldman’s Marcus: a better savings account. Uber is sustaining — targeted existing taxi customers with more convenience, not underserved non-consumers.
Disruptive innovation
Redefines the performance axis. Stripe (2010): developer-first payments API. Christensen’s advice: respond with a separate unit — e.g. Vanguard’s 2015 robo-advisor that captured 50%+ AUM in two years.

§2.3b · Sustaining vs. disruptive
08 / 15
Christensen (1997) · the distinction that matters

Not every innovation is disruptive.

Incumbents need to know which type they face. The wrong diagnosis leads to the wrong response. Sustaining innovations are common; true disruptions are rare.

Sustaining innovation
Better product, same customers
Improves an existing product along dimensions that incumbents and mainstream customers already value. Targets the same customers with a better version. Incumbents usually win here because they have scale, brand, and distribution.
Example · Marcus by Goldman Sachs
Goldman launched Marcus in 2016 as a consumer bank: high-yield savings accounts and personal loans, delivered digitally. It was a better savings account — higher APY than branch banks, slick UX — but the same product (deposits + loans) targeting the same mainstream customers. Marcus attracted 4M customers and $50B+ in deposits, but lost $3B over 2020–2022. Goldman pulled back in late 2022. Sustaining innovations by incumbents often fail when the core business model doesn’t change.
Example · Uber
Christensen argued Uber is not disruptive. It targeted existing mainstream customers already served by taxis, offering more convenience but not necessarily a lower price. It improved the taxi experience rather than creating a new market for non-consumers.
Disruptive innovation
New axis, underserved customers
Redefines the performance axis entirely. Starts with overlooked or underserved customers — the low end or non-consumers — with a simpler, cheaper product. Over time, quality improves until mainstream customers switch. By then, it is too late for incumbents to react.
Example · Stripe
Stripe (2010) offered a developer-first payments API — seven lines of code to accept a payment. Traditional processors (First Data, Worldpay) sold to enterprise CFOs through salespeople. Stripe targeted underserved developers and start-ups who found existing payment integration painfully complex. As those start-ups scaled (Shopify, Lyft, DoorDash), Stripe moved upmarket. Now valued at $159B (Feb 2026 tender), processing payments for Amazon, Google, and BMW.
Example · Robo-advisors (Betterment, Wealthsimple)
A robo-advisor uses algorithms to build and rebalance portfolios with minimal human intervention. It targets investors priced out of traditional wealth management ($250K+ minimums). Betterment launched in 2010 with no minimum and ~0.25% fees vs. ~1% for human advisors. Vanguard responded in 2015 with its own robo — capturing 50%+ of automated AUM in two years. Christensen’s playbook executed correctly.

Pisano (2015) · two axes of innovation

The innovation landscape.

Pisano’s 2×2 maps innovation along two dimensions: the degree to which it leverages existing technical competencies and existing business models.

Routine
Existing tech + existing model. Incremental bank digitisation. Low risk, low return.
Disruptive
Existing tech + new model. LendingClub: existing credit-scoring, P2P marketplace.
Radical
New tech + existing model. Blockchain settlement: novel infra, same clearing function.
Architectural
New tech + new model. DeFi (Uniswap, Aave): smart contracts + autonomous intermediation.

One-sided vs. multi-sided platforms

Business models.

The fundamental architectural choice: serve one customer group (product model) or orchestrate two or more groups on a platform (market model). Most transformational fintechs are multi-sided.

One-sided
Traditional bank, insurer, or asset manager. Revenue from spread, premium, or AUM fee. Value chain is linear: originate → hold → service. Economies of scale drive consolidation into financial conglomerates (personal + commercial + investment banking).
Multi-sided
Platform connects 2+ distinct user groups whose interactions create value. Borrowell: free credit scores for consumers (subsidy-side), commissions from banks selling products on the marketplace (money-side). Physical predecessors: newspapers, stock exchanges, shopping malls.

§2.6 · Network effects & platform strategy
12 / 15
Rochet & Tirole (2003) · platform economics

Network effects & platform pricing.

Multi-sided platforms must solve the chicken-and-egg problem. The subsidy side is attracted at or below cost; the money side pays. Getting the price structure right is the defining strategic challenge.

Concept 01
Same-side (direct) effects
Each additional user on one side increases value for other users on the same side. Venmo: payment utility rises with peer adoption. Metcalfe’s law applies — value scales as n².
Concept 02
Cross-side (indirect) effects
More users on side A increase value for side B. More Visa cardholders make the network more valuable to merchants, which attracts more cardholders. Classic two-sided feedback loop.
Strategy 01
Subsidy side
Price-sensitive or hard-to-attract side is subsidised. Robinhood: zero-commission trades. Google: free search. Subsidy is an investment in cross-side pull.
Strategy 02
Money side
Side with higher willingness-to-pay is monetised. Robinhood earns payment for order flow (PFOF) — Citadel pays ~$0.002/share to execute trades, generating $810M for Robinhood in 2021. User pays zero commission; the market maker is the real customer.
The price level matters less than the price structure. A platform can raise total fees and lose volume, or cut one side’s price and grow both.
— Rochet & Tirole, J. of the European Economic Association (2003)

§2.7 · Monetization

Revenue architecture

Agency vs. principal.

How a fintech earns revenue determines its risk profile, capital requirements, and regulatory obligations. The fundamental split: fee-based (agency) vs. balance-sheet (principal).

Agency revenues
Fee & commission
Transaction fees, interchange, advisory fees, PFOF, subscription. Capital-light, scalable. Stripe: 2.9% + $0.30 per txn.
Principal revenues
Spread & gain
Net interest income, trading gains, underwriting profit. Requires regulatory capital. SoFi: NIM of 5.9% (Q4 2023) after bank charter.
JPMorgan derives ~65% of revenue from principal activities (Tab. 2.1). Fintechs historically skew toward agency, but the most ambitious — SoFi, Revolut, Nubank — are migrating to principal revenue via bank charters.

§2.8 · Vulnerability to disruption
14 / 15
McKinsey · four signs of vulnerability

Which businesses are at risk?

A financial intermediary is vulnerable if it offers an undifferentiated, bundled product to a large pool of customers, where the more profitable ones cross-subsidise the less profitable ones.

01 · Cross-subsidisation
Some customers subsidise others
Profitable customers pay high fees that cover losses on unprofitable ones. A fintech can cherry-pick the profitable segment with a targeted, lower-cost product — leaving the incumbent with the unprofitable tail.
02 · Forced bundling
Buy the whole product to get one part
Customers are forced to buy a bundle when they only want one feature. Fintechs unbundle: Wise unbundled cheap FX from the full-service bank account; Wealthsimple unbundled low-cost investing from wealth management.
03 · Access friction
Can’t get it where and when you want
Products tied to branch hours, geography, or legacy systems. Mobile-first fintechs offer 24/7 access from anywhere — Nubank serves ~131M customers (Q4 2025) across Latin America without a single branch.
04 · Poor customer experience
Doesn’t match global best practice
Customers compare their banking experience to Amazon and Apple, not to other banks. When the gap is large enough, switching costs stop mattering. Chime and Revolut grew by delivering consumer-tech-grade UX to basic banking.
Case study · Revolut vs. credit cards
Credit cards were an oligopoly (Visa, Mastercard, Amex) — fixed features, high interest rates, opaque FX markups. All four vulnerabilities present. Revolut entered with no annual fee, interbank FX rates, customisable spend limits, real-time notifications, and built-in budgeting. By 2025: 50M+ customers, UK banking licence, and a $45B valuation. The incumbents still dominate volume, but Revolut proved the margin pool is attackable.
The fintech value proposition
Every successful fintech attack exploits the same four promises:
• Cheaper — lower fees, tighter spreads
• Easier — simpler onboarding, less paperwork
• Faster — real-time settlement, instant approvals
• More convenient — mobile-first, 24/7, anywhere
