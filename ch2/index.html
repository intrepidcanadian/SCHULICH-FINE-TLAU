§2.1 · A multidisciplinary approach
02 / 10
Three analytical stops

A multidisciplinary lens.

Understanding fintech requires three bodies of theory working in concert — financial intermediation, innovation strategy, and business-model design. Each supplies a distinct diagnostic question; together they form the analytical engine for every case we examine this term.

Stop 01 · Finance
Financial Intermediation
Why do intermediaries exist? Five dimensions — information asymmetry, transaction costs, liquidity creation, risk sharing, and trust — explain the enduring ~2% cost of intermediation (Philippon 2015). Fintech attacks each dimension differently. The 2008 GFC eroded trust in incumbents, lowering barriers for new entrants.
Stop 02 · Strategy
Innovation Theory
Christensen’s disruptive innovation (1997) and Pisano’s innovation landscape map (2015) answer how new entrants dislodge incumbents — and why most incumbents respond too late. Dell disrupted IBM’s PC business (sold to Lenovo 2005); Netflix bankrupted Blockbuster over 13 years.
Stop 03 · Economics
Business Models
One-sided vs. multi-sided platforms, network effects, subsidy-side / money-side dynamics (Rochet & Tirole 2003), and the agency-vs.-principal revenue split. Most fintechs earn 90%+ from agency (fees); exceptions like SoFi and ZhongAn earn primarily from principal activities.
The multidisciplinary approach is essential because fintech sits at the intersection of finance, technology, and strategy. No single discipline is sufficient.
— Arner, Barberis & Buckley (2017)

§2.2 · Financial intermediation
03 / 10
Why intermediaries persist

Five dimensions.

Financial intermediaries exist because of five persistent frictions. Fintech does not eliminate these frictions — it re-prices them.

i. Information asymmetry
Adverse selection and moral hazard (Akerlof 1970, Stiglitz & Weiss 1981). Alt-data scoring reduces but does not eliminate information gaps.
ii. Transaction costs
Search, bargaining, and enforcement costs (Coase 1937). APIs and smart contracts compress but never reach zero.
iii. Liquidity creation
Maturity transformation (Diamond & Dybvig 1983). Neobanks still need deposit insurance and wholesale funding.
iv. Risk sharing
Diversification and hedging across agents (Allen & Gale 1997). Parametric insurance and DeFi pools are new mechanisms, same function.
v. Trust
Institutional trust vs. algorithmic trust. Blockchain substitutes cryptographic verification for reputational capital — partially.

§2.2 · The persistence puzzle
04 / 10
Philippon (2015) · a puzzling constant
~2%

The unit cost of financial intermediation in the United States has remained roughly constant at ~2% of assets intermediated for over 130 years — despite ATMs, electronic trading, and the internet.

Technology has improved the quality of financial services, but has not reduced the unit cost. The finance industry has become more efficient at extracting rents, not at lowering them.
— Thomas Philippon, Quarterly Journal of Economics (2015)

§2.3 · Disruptive innovation
05 / 10
Christensen (1997) · applied to financial services

Disruptive innovation.

Christensen’s four-step process explains how inferior products in overlooked market segments eventually overtake incumbents. The pattern recurs across fintech verticals.

The four-step disruption process
Step 01
Incumbents overshoot
Incumbent products exceed what mainstream customers need. JPMorgan’s $15.3B tech spend (2023) optimises for institutional clients, not the underbanked.
Step 02
Entrant targets low-end
New entrants serve over-served or non-consumers with simpler, cheaper products. M-Pesa (2007): SMS-based payments for the unbanked in Kenya.
Step 03
Entrant improves upward
Product quality improves until it meets mainstream needs. Robinhood: zero-commission trades attracted 23M+ accounts by 2023, then added options, crypto, retirement.
Step 04
Incumbent displaced
Incumbents retreat to shrinking premium segments. Netflix vs. Blockbuster (2010 bankruptcy). In finance: neobanks eroding high-street branch economics.
Sustaining innovation
Improves existing products along dimensions incumbents already value. Goldman Sachs Marcus: a better savings account from a bulge-bracket bank. Uber is sustaining, not disruptive — it targeted existing taxi customers with more convenience, not underserved non-consumers.
Disruptive innovation
Redefines the performance axis. Stripe (2010): developer-first payments API. Not a better POS terminal — a different product for a different buyer. Christensen’s advice to incumbents: don’t panic, monitor, and if necessary respond with a separate disruptive unit (e.g. Vanguard launched a robo-advisor in 2015 and captured 50%+ of AUM within two years).

§2.4 · Digital innovation strategy
06 / 10
Pisano (2015) · two axes of innovation

The innovation landscape.

Pisano’s 2×2 maps innovation along two dimensions: the degree to which it leverages existing technical competencies and the degree to which it leverages existing business models.

Routine
Existing tech + existing model. Incremental bank digitisation. Low strategic risk, low return.
Disruptive
Existing tech + new model. Lending Club used existing credit-scoring tech but created a peer-to-peer marketplace model.
Radical
New tech + existing model. Blockchain-based settlement: novel infrastructure, same clearing function.
Architectural
New tech + new model. The most challenging quadrant. DeFi protocols (Uniswap, Aave): novel smart-contract tech combined with autonomous intermediation. Most fintechs start routine/radical, then must pivot to disruptive/architectural to survive.

§2.5 · Business models
07 / 10
One-sided vs. multi-sided platforms

Business models.

The fundamental architectural choice: serve one customer group (product model) or orchestrate two or more groups on a platform (market model). Most transformational fintechs are multi-sided.

One-sided
Traditional bank, insurer, or asset manager. Revenue from spread, premium, or AUM fee. Value chain is linear: originate → hold → service. Economies of scale drive consolidation into financial conglomerates (personal + commercial + investment banking).
Multi-sided
Platform connects 2+ distinct user groups whose interactions create value. Borrowell: free credit scores for consumers (subsidy-side), commissions from banks selling products on the marketplace (money-side). Physical predecessors: newspapers, stock exchanges, shopping malls.

§2.6 · Network effects & platform strategy
08 / 10
Rochet & Tirole (2003) · platform economics

Network effects & platform pricing.

Multi-sided platforms must solve the chicken-and-egg problem. The subsidy side is attracted at or below cost; the money side pays. Getting the price structure right is the defining strategic challenge.

Concept 01
Same-side (direct) effects
Each additional user on one side increases value for other users on the same side. Venmo: payment utility rises with peer adoption. Metcalfe’s law applies — value scales as n².
Concept 02
Cross-side (indirect) effects
More users on side A increase value for side B. More Visa cardholders make the network more valuable to merchants, which attracts more cardholders. The classic two-sided feedback loop.
Strategy 01
Subsidy side
The price-sensitive or hard-to-attract side is subsidised. Robinhood: zero-commission trades for retail investors. Google: free search for consumers. The subsidy is an investment in cross-side pull.
Strategy 02
Money side
The side with higher willingness-to-pay is monetised. Robinhood: PFOF from Citadel ($810M in 2021). Three key findings: (1) subsidise marquee users (e.g. YouTube influencers), (2) limit access to reduce on-platform competition, (3) combat multihoming — often by acquiring competitors.
The price level matters less than the price structure. A platform can raise total fees and lose volume, or cut one side’s price and grow both.
— Rochet & Tirole, RAND J. Economics (2003)

§2.7 · Monetization
09 / 10
Revenue architecture

Agency vs. principal.

How a fintech earns revenue determines its risk profile, capital requirements, and regulatory obligations. The fundamental split: fee-based (agency) vs. balance-sheet (principal).

Agency revenues
Fee & commission
Transaction fees, interchange, advisory fees, PFOF, subscription. Capital-light, scalable, but vulnerable to price competition. Stripe: 2.9% + $0.30 per transaction.
Principal revenues
Spread & gain
Net interest income, trading gains, underwriting profit. Requires balance-sheet capacity and regulatory capital. SoFi: NIM of 5.9% (Q4 2023) after bank charter acquisition.
Incumbent banks derive ~60% of revenue from principal activities (NII). Fintechs historically skew toward agency (fees), but the most ambitious — SoFi, Revolut, Nubank — are migrating toward principal revenue via bank charters and direct lending.
— Fintech Explained, Ch. 2

§2.8 · Disruption vulnerability
10 / 10
Synthesis · who is vulnerable?

Disruption vulnerability.

McKinsey identifies four vulnerability signs: (1) some customers cross-subsidise others, (2) customers must buy the whole bundle to get what they want, (3) delivery doesn’t match when/where customers want it, (4) customer experience lags global best practice. Example: credit cards — an oligopoly (Visa, Mastercard, Amex) ripe for unbundling. Revolut entered with no annual fee, customisable features, and budgeting tools.
