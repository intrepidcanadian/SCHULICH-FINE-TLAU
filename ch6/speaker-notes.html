Slide 01 ~2 min
Cover — Ethereum, Smart Contracts & DeFi
  • Welcome the class. Frame: last week was Bitcoin’s payment ledger and its scandals. Today we cover the second-generation blockchain that Buterin built to do far more than move coins — a general-purpose computer.
  • Preview the arc: Buterin and Ethereum’s origins → ether and gas → smart contracts and dapps → tokens (ERC-20, ERC-721) → DAOs and the DAO Hack → stablecoins → DeFi as crypto LEGO → Curve, MakerDAO, Lido → R3 Corda as the permissioned counter-example.
  • Opening hook: “If you could rebuild banking, brokerage, and asset management without bank charters, broker-dealer licenses, or a clearinghouse — what would it cost? Under a dollar. That’s DeFi.”
  • Housekeeping: pause for questions after the DAO Hack (slide 5), after MakerDAO (slide 10), and at the end.
Transition: “Let’s start with the visionary — and what Bitcoin lacked.”
Slide 02 ~4 min
The Ethereum Network & Vitalik Buterin
  • Buterin’s biography: born in Russia 1994, emigrated to Toronto at 6, learned about Bitcoin at 17 from his programmer father, co-founded Bitcoin Magazine. At 19 he wrote the Ethereum whitepaper (Nov 2013). At 20 he won a Thiel fellowship and dropped out of Waterloo.
  • The critique of Bitcoin: too slow, too narrow, too limited a scripting language. Bitcoin records only BTC transactions. Buterin wanted a blockchain that could host arbitrary applications.
  • Walk the milestones (Tab 6.1): Whitepaper (Nov 2013) → Yellowpaper by Gavin Wood (Apr 2014) → Crowdsale $18.4M (Jul–Sep 2014) → Frontier launch (Jul 2015) → DAO fork (Jul 2016) → Beacon Chain (Dec 2020) → The Merge (Sep 2022).
  • Reference Gavin Wood — first CTO of the Ethereum Foundation, designer of Solidity, author of the Yellowpaper. He later left to build Polkadot.
  • Fig 6.1 (Google searches): Bitcoin dominates media attention. But Ethereum rose steadily and is now associated with real adoption (DeFi, NFTs) rather than just speculation.
Transition: “Running programs on a blockchain isn’t free. You pay for computation in gas. Let’s see how.”
Slide 03 ~4 min
Ether, Gas & Proof-of-Stake
  • The metaphor: if Ethereum is a virtual computer, ETH is the electricity. You buy gas with ETH. Simple programs use little gas. Complex programs use a lot.
  • Why gas matters: forces developers to write efficient code. Prevents infinite loops (any program would burn all the sender’s ETH and halt). Provides spam protection — you can’t flood the network for free.
  • Block reward history: 5 ETH → 3 (late 2017) → 2 (early 2019). No supply cap on ETH (unlike Bitcoin’s 21M). ~120M ETH in circulation (Apr 2022).
  • The Merge (Sept 15, 2022): PoW → PoS. Cut energy consumption 99.95%. Validators stake 32 ETH each. One proposer per 12-sec slot, randomly chosen weighted by stake. A committee votes (attests). Every 32 slots = 1 epoch = a checkpoint vote.
  • Slashing: validators lose stake for downtime (small penalty) or double signing (severe, 5%+). Discuss the new concern post-Merge: stake concentration. Lido alone controls ~30% of all staked ETH — centralizing what was meant to decentralize.
Transition: “Now that we have a programmable blockchain, what do you build on it? LEGO.”
Slide 04 ~5 min
Smart Contracts as LEGO & the Token Zoo
  • Define smart contract: code that executes a transaction with no human intervention. Lives at a contract address on-chain, immutable once deployed. Quote Buterin’s definition — assets in, formulaic redistribution out.
  • Solidity is Turing-complete — you can write any program. Influenced by C++/Python/JS. Runs on the EVM. Other languages: Vyper, Yul. The choice of language affects auditability and bug risk.
  • Why “LEGO”? Because smart contracts are composable. A DEX uses a lending pool. A yield aggregator uses both. A wrapped token references a bridge. Each piece is a brick. Build something new by stacking them.
  • Four dapp rules: open-source, on-chain data, cryptographic token, consensus mechanism. These are the necessary conditions for “decentralized.”
  • Token taxonomy — ERC-20 fungible: payment (ETH), utility (BAT), security (digital stock), governance (Maker, UNI, AAVE). ERC-721 non-fungible: art, collectibles. Use Beeple’s $69.3M sale and CryptoKitties ($110K for one cat) as anchors.
  • Discussion question: “Which token category should be regulated as securities? Why?” (Lead to: security tokens clearly; utility and governance are gray; payment tokens like BTC have been ruled non-securities by the SEC.)
Transition: “If smart contracts can run a company, can they BE a company? Meet the DAO.”
Slide 05 ~5 min
DAOs & The DAO Hack
  • DAO = a virtual company in code. Slock.It’s “The DAO” (April 2016) was the first ambitious one: holders would vote on which projects to fund. Raised $150M in 28 days — 12M ETH, 15% of all ETH in circulation.
  • The hack (June 17, 2016): an attacker exploited a re-entrancy vulnerability in the smart contract code and drained $40M of ETH. The hack was visible on-chain in real time. The Ethereum community could see it happening.
  • The philosophical question: “Is a blockchain capable of being modified or not?” The purists said no — immutability is sacred, the hacker followed the code. The pragmatists, led by Buterin, said the theft must be reversed.
  • The hard fork (July 20, 2016): a majority of ETH holders voted to rewrite the ledger and return the funds. Ethereum split: ETH (modified) and Ethereum Classic (ETC, original chain). Today ETH dominates; ETC is a speculative curiosity.
  • The lesson: the vulnerability was not in Ethereum — it was in the application’s smart contract. Code is law only as long as the code is correct. This is why smart-contract auditing is now a multibillion-dollar industry (OpenZeppelin, Trail of Bits, Certik).
Transition: “Even on Ethereum, prices move violently. To use crypto as money, we need a stable unit. Enter stablecoins.”
Slide 06 ~5 min
Stablecoins — the Peg Problem
  • The volatility problem: BTC daily vol ~4%, daily range often >10%. A medium of exchange and unit of account require stability. Stablecoins solve this by pegging to a reference asset.
  • Four backings: fiat (USDT, USDC, BUSD), commodity (Digix Gold), crypto (DAI), algorithmic (TerraUSD). The first three are collateralized; the last is purely incentive-based. Walk Tab 6.2.
  • Centralized vs. decentralized: Tether is run by Bitfinex founders — opaque reserves, regulator-fined for misrepresentation. DAI is minted by the MakerDAO smart contract — transparent, overcollateralized, governance-token controlled. Trade-off: centralization gives speed; decentralization gives trust.
  • Case study: TerraUSD & LUNA (May 2022). UST market cap peaked at $60B. The Anchor protocol promised 20% APR to UST depositors — unsustainable yields that attracted speculative capital and inflated the ecosystem. Then a coordinated attack triggered a UST de-pegging. The algorithm minted LUNA to defend the peg, hyperinflating LUNA from $119 to $0. UST fell to $0.10. $45B in market cap evaporated in days; $300B across all of crypto via contagion.
  • Lesson: algorithmic stablecoins without external collateral are reflexive — they work only as long as confidence holds. Once a run starts, the algorithm accelerates the collapse. Do Kwon faces criminal charges in multiple jurisdictions.
Transition: “Stablecoins are the unit. Smart contracts are the rails. Put them together and you get DeFi.”
Slide 07 ~4 min
DeFi — The Internet of Money
  • Define DeFi: a financial system built on cryptoassets, blockchains, and smart contracts that disintermediates banks, brokers, and asset managers. Always open. Permissionless. Transparent. Automated.
  • The metric is TVL — total value locked in smart contracts. Peak $175B (late 2021). Year-end 2022 $40B (after Terra, FTX, the broader crypto winter). DeFi = ~5% of total crypto market cap.
  • Ethereum dominates: ~60% of DeFi TVL. Tron 10.8%, BSC 10.5%. Why? Network effects — deepest developer base, ERC-20 standard, Solidity, EVM. Other chains compete on speed and cost but cannot match the ecosystem.
  • Quote Buterin from Harvey et al.’s book: DeFi was always part of the Ethereum vision. The platform was deliberately general-purpose so financial applications could emerge alongside non-financial ones.
  • Surface the paradigm debate explicitly: the textbook names two camps. The transformational view says DeFi will dramatically alter how financial markets operate — disintermediating banks, brokers, and clearinghouses. The traditional view says DeFi will co-exist with the old system but never dominate — regulation, trust, and scale favor incumbents. Ask: “Which camp are you in, and what evidence would change your mind?”
  • Discussion question: “Is DeFi disrupting traditional finance or running parallel to it?” (Lead to: today it’s parallel and small. But it forces incumbents to respond — faster settlement, lower fees, programmable assets. Even if DeFi stays small, its influence on TradFi is large.)
Transition: “DeFi isn’t one thing — it’s ten categories. Let’s map the LEGO.”
Slide 08 ~4 min
DeFi Categories & the LEGO Stack
  • Walk Tab 6.3 and Fig 6.2. The big four: DEXes (40% of TVL), Lending (27%), Liquidity Staking (22%), CDP (20%). Smaller: bridges, yield farming, services, derivatives, algo-stables, yield aggregators.
  • DEXes: 655 protocols. Curve specializes in stablecoin swaps. Uniswap dominates token-to-token. PancakeSwap dominates on BSC. AMMs replace order books with liquidity pools.
  • Lending: AAVE, Compound — pool-based. Lenders deposit; borrowers take overcollateralized loans. Interest rates auto-adjust to supply/demand. This is the same model as MakerDAO — we’ll cover it shortly.
  • Liquidity staking: Lido lets you stake ETH and get stETH back (1:1). You can re-stake stETH elsewhere for additional yield. Composability in action — same dollar of capital earns yield twice.
  • Bridges: blockchains are siloed. Wrapped Bitcoin (WBTC) is an ERC-20 token backed 1:1 by BTC custody. It brings BTC liquidity onto Ethereum. Each bridge is a custodian — and a target. Several bridges have been hacked for hundreds of millions (Ronin $625M, Poly Network $611M).
Transition: “Let’s look at one DEX in detail. Curve is the gold standard for stablecoin swaps.”
Slide 09 ~4 min
Curve Finance — Case Study
  • Curve was built specifically for swapping assets that should be worth the same — stablecoins. The AMM math minimizes slippage when all assets in the pool hover around $1.
  • Walk the 3CRV pool: holds DAI, USDT, USDC. Users swap between any pair, paying 0.04% (vs. ~0.30% on Coinbase Pro and ~0.10% on Binance). Liquidity providers (LPs) earn 50% of the fees pro-rata.
  • Why depositors do it: yield. Early Curve advertised 10–50% APR. That claim has been removed — yields depend on volume and have compressed.
  • CEX vs. DEX trade-off: Coinbase holds your keys (the honey pot). Curve’s smart contract is open-source — you keep your keys. But smart contracts can have bugs. The DAO Hack was a 2016 reminder; bridge hacks are a 2022–2023 reminder. Always audit.
  • TVL collapse: $25B (YE 2021) to $3.6B (YE 2022). Same story as the rest of DeFi — the 2022 crypto winter destroyed leverage and confidence.
Transition: “If Curve is the trading floor, MakerDAO is the central bank.”
Slide 10 ~5 min
MakerDAO & the Decentralized Central Bank
  • MakerDAO is the largest DeFi protocol — $6B TVL at YE 2022, 15% of all DeFi. The model: deposit ETH or other ERC-20 tokens, mint DAI (a $1-pegged stablecoin) against them.
  • 200% collateralization: deposit $100, borrow $50 of DAI. Why so much? Crypto is volatile. If ETH drops 30%, your $100 collateral is now $70 — the smart contract liquidates to keep the system solvent.
  • Why borrow DAI? Liquidity without selling. Avoid taxable events. Lever up by re-depositing DAI in another DeFi protocol. Or just transact in a stable dollar unit on-chain.
  • Why “decentralized central bank”? MakerDAO sets interest rates (the “DAI Savings Rate” and “Stability Fee”) via governance vote. The Maker token holders are the voters. The smart contract enforces. The Federal Reserve has a board; MakerDAO has token holders. The mechanics are similar; the politics are radically different.
  • Abracadabra is the #2 CDP: accepts interest-bearing tokens as collateral — users can stretch collateral twice and create on-chain leverage. Higher yield, higher liquidation risk. Composability cuts both ways.
Transition: “Three more building blocks complete the picture.”
Slide 11 ~4 min
Yield Farming, Derivatives & Bridges
  • Lido: liquidity staking. Stake ETH, get stETH (1:1). Restake stETH elsewhere. $18.7B TVL within 18 months. The double-dipping concern: if you can restake the staked token, you’re creating phantom collateral. Regulators are watching.
  • Synthetix: on-chain derivatives. sBTC tracks BTC. sUSD tracks the dollar. Synthetic equities exist too. Pay 0.30% per trade — no broker, no clearing house. Decentralized derivatives are a TradFi disruption story to watch.
  • Wrapped Bitcoin (WBTC): ERC-20 token backed 1:1 with BTC. Bridges Bitcoin liquidity onto Ethereum. Lets BTC be used as DeFi collateral. The largest bridging token by far.
  • Yield farming: chase the highest APY by moving liquidity between protocols. Deposit in Curve → flip the LP token to Convex → deposit elsewhere. Composability magnifies returns and risks. A bug in any contract in the stack can wipe you out.
  • Discussion question: “If composability is the killer feature of DeFi, what is the systemic risk implication?” (Lead to: a single smart-contract bug can cascade across protocols that share collateral. Think 2008 mortgage CDOs in code.)
Transition: “Before we leave the permissionless world, one more piece of theory: tokenomics.”
Slide 11b ~4 min
Tokenomics — Tokens vs. Equity
  • This is the academic backbone for everything we’ve seen. Tokenomics asks: when is a token a better financing vehicle than equity? The answer depends on the moral-hazard problem between founders, investors, and users.
  • Token pricing is demand-driven, not DCF. The equilibrium token price is determined by total user transaction volume, not discounted cash flows. This is a fundamentally different pricing model than what students learned in corporate finance. Ask: “If I can’t discount cash flows, how do I value a token?” (Answer: model adoption — wallets, transaction volume, network effects.)
  • Filecoin case: users pay FIL to store data; miners post FIL as collateral. As the user base grows, counterparties are easier to find, the token becomes more useful, and its value rises. Network effects drive the price — not earnings.
  • Tokens vs. equity: research concludes tokens dominate equity for start-ups where entrepreneurial effort is crucial and signaling quality to outside investors is hard. The optimal design combines an ICO crowdsale with a revenue-sharing agreement — replicating equity payoffs without equity’s governance overhead.
  • The supply paradox: miner compensation increases token supply, lowering investor willingness to pay. The counter-solution is token burning — deliberately destroying tokens to reduce oversupply and support value. Ethereum’s EIP-1559 does exactly this with base fees.
  • The commitment device: blockchain prevents platform owners from secretly diluting user stakes. Founder payoff depends on profit; token investor payoff depends on transaction volume. These aligned but distinct incentives reduce moral hazard. Ask: “How does this compare to the principal-agent problem in traditional corporate governance?”
Transition: “Now let’s see what banks are doing with this technology — on their own terms.”
Slide 12 ~4 min
R3 Corda — The Permissioned Alternative
  • R3 is the counter-example. David Rutter, ex-ICAP electronic-brokerage CEO, saw blockchain’s back-office potential without the crypto religion. Founded R3CEV in 2014; became R3 in 2016.
  • Funding model: 42 banks joined by YE 2015, each paying up to $5M annual membership and seconding staff. $100M+ raised, zero equity given up. The banks underwrote their own back-office disruption.
  • Five Corda choices vs. Ethereum: (1) Permissioned, not public. (2) Validates transactions individually, not in blocks. (3) Notaries verify, not mining. (4) Data shared only with counterparties. (5) Smart contracts inside the JVM.
  • Throughput: 20K TPS vs. Ethereum’s ~15 TPS. Estimated industry savings: $15B-$20B/year in back-office costs.
  • The takeaway: blockchain doesn’t have to mean “decentralized.” The technology stack — DLT, cryptographic verification, smart contracts — is independent of the governance model. Regulated finance is absorbing DLT without crypto.
Transition: “Let’s pull it together.”
Slide 13 ~3 min
Closing — Crypto LEGO
  • Synthesis: Ethereum gave us programmable money. Smart contracts let strangers transact without intermediaries. DAOs codify governance. Stablecoins solve the volatility problem — when they hold. DeFi composes these primitives into a parallel financial system.
  • Four numbers: 32 ETH (staking minimum), $175B (peak DeFi TVL), $45B (TerraUSD’s death), $69.3M (Beeple’s NFT).
  • The unresolved tension: composability is DeFi’s superpower and its systemic risk. One bug can cascade through the LEGO tower. The Terra collapse rippled through every protocol that held UST. The DAO Hack threatened Ethereum itself.
  • The permissioned alternative (Corda) shows regulated finance can absorb DLT without crypto. Both futures are plausible — decentralized DeFi and permissioned bank-grade DLT — and they may coexist.
  • Preview next class: Chapter 7 — Payments. Real-time rails, card networks, cross-border, the role of stablecoins in mainstream payments.
  • Final question: “If you ran a major bank, would you build on Ethereum, on Corda, or on neither? Why?” (Force them to articulate the trust-vs-openness trade-off.)
End of lecture. Open floor for questions. Reading for next week: Chapter 7.
