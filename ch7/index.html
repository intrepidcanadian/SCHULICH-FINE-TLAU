§7.3 · Online lending
04 / 31
Who holds the loan decides who bears the risk

P2P/marketplace vs. balance sheet lending.

P2P / marketplace lender · the intermediary
Matches borrowers with lenders for a fee. Loans funded by retail and institutional investors. Defaults pass through to the investor — the platform bears no loss. Incentives misaligned: it earns origination fees on volume with no skin in the game.
Balance sheet lender · the principal
Acts like a bank, lending its own funds and holding loans on its balance sheet. It bears the credit losses it originates. Incentives aligned with sound underwriting (e.g. Lendified). Many large platforms run both models.

§7.3 · How marketplace lending works
05 / 31
From posting to funding — the investor’s-eye view

How a marketplace loan actually works.

The original P2P model let the crowd fund loans directly — individuals committing a small slice of each. The portal is the intermediary: it screens, rates, and matches, but the lender carries the credit risk.

Anatomy of a loan listing
1 · Post
The ask
Borrower lists a funding target and how the proceeds will be used.
2 · Rate
Price the risk
The platform assigns a credit rating and interest rate — the lender’s yield-to-maturity.
3 · Subscribe
Buy a slice
Investors fund part of the loan (e.g. $100 each) during a subscription window; a dashboard tracks % committed.
4 · Fund
All-or-nothing
The borrower receives the money only if the loan is fully subscribed.
5 · Spread
Diversify
Holding many small slices spreads exposure so no single default is fatal.
Where the growth is · small business. SMEs raised $43B — 43% of global online lending in 2020, a segment growing even as consumer lending shrinks. Most small firms lack the credit history or collateral banks require. Specialists: Kabbage, OnDeck (US); Lendified, Lending Loop (Canada); Funding Circle (UK).

§7.3 · From P2P to marketplace
06 / 31
“Peer-to-peer” is now a misnomer

The money comes from institutions.

As demand outgrew retail capital, portals opened to hedge funds, banks, and insurers who treat online loans as an alternative asset class. ~85% of Prosper’s funds are institutional; LendingClub and Funding Circle shut their retail channels entirely.

Rise of the robots. Institutions connect by API and screen postings in real time with proprietary algorithms. Robot investors systematically outperform — but only when the portal discloses enough borrower information to assess default risk.

§7.11 · Crowdfunding research findings
18 / 31
What the academic studies actually find — lending, equity, rewards, inclusion & bias

What the research finds.

How lenders & the crowd choose
• Retail lenders favor applications with emotion-evoking keywords; shun spelling errors and too-short/long descriptions.
• They avoid higher-rate loans — reading the rate as a default-risk signal.
• API “robot” investors outperform — but only when the portal discloses enough borrower data.
• “Alternative data” is contested: a 4M-loan study found ratings mostly track ordinary credit reports.
• Sophisticated investors are repelled by promotional language with no facts; retail investors aren’t.
What predicts default
• More likely: renters; medical or small-business loans; five-year maturities.
• Less likely: professionals; loans for a wedding, a car, or debt consolidation.
• Fintech loans default more than equal-risk bank loans — borrowers re-lever and end up worse off.
• Nudges: positive-expectation texts help; reminders of consequences backfire.
What makes a raise succeed
• All-or-nothing campaigns raise more on average; higher-quality projects win.
• Credible disclosure and founder skin in the game signal quality; serial founders leverage social capital.
• More platform due diligence → more successful campaigns, backers, and capital.
• In rewards campaigns, deeper customer-investor involvement lifts success.
• A successful equity raise helps secure VC later; Kickstarter’s 2012 “risks” section cut high-risk backing.
Financial inclusion (contested)
• For: fintechs entered underbanked, fewer-branch areas; after 2008 they lifted small-business lending where banks pulled back — supporting jobs, wages & new businesses for ~8 years.
• Competition boosts inclusion by disrupting monopolistic banks that exploit payment data to gauge credit quality; in China, Ant Group credit access cut small-firm volatility & bankruptcy.
• Against: many target already-banked, creditworthy borrowers; charge lower-rated borrowers more; PPP loans showed 4× more fraud flags by 2021.
• Fintech mortgage lenders are ~20% faster but charge a premium, serve more-educated/older borrowers, and default less.
• Globally, fintech credit grows in richer economies with weaker bank regulation — not emerging ones.
Bias & discrimination
• Women run only 35% of Kickstarters yet enjoy higher success rates.
• Experienced investors show no gender preference; inexperienced female investors favor female founders.
• Founders with trustworthy-looking faces get more pledges — a stronger effect for women.
• Black founders are rated lower on identical equity projects; endorsements and prior success shrink the gap.
• Minority borrowers pay higher risk-adjusted mortgage rates — ML can inherit bias from its training data.

§7.11 · Competition & financial inclusion
19 / 31
Does alternative finance widen access — or just skim the creditworthy?

Competition & financial inclusion.

The optimistic case · filling the gap
• Online lenders move into markets with fewer bank branches, underbanked rural areas, places with more bank-misconduct complaints, and regions banks abandoned in past downturns.
• After 2008, fintechs and non-banks lifted small-business lending as big banks pulled back — supporting jobs, wages and new businesses for ~8 years, and lending more where COVID hit hardest.
• Competition can widen inclusion by disrupting monopolistic banks that exploit payment data to judge credit quality.
The skeptical case · banking the banked
• Other studies find online lenders chase the most creditworthy borrowers — people who already have bank credit, not the marginal or underserved.
• They make smaller loans and undercut banks for high-score borrowers, but charge lower-rated borrowers more — a stretch to call that “access.”
• Fintech mortgage lenders are ~20% faster yet charge a premium and serve more-educated, older borrowers; fintech PPP loans showed 4× more fraud flags by 2021.
Globally: fintech credit grows where the financial system is already developed — higher GDP per capita, stronger investor protection and courts, but weaker bank regulation — not in emerging markets. (In China, though, Ant Group credit access cut small-firm volatility and bankruptcy risk.)

§7.11 · Discrimination & bias
20 / 31
Gender, race — and the algorithms that can encode both

Discrimination & bias in alternative finance.

Gender
• Women run only 35% of Kickstarter campaigns and set similar goals — yet enjoy higher success rates than men.
• Experienced investors show no gender preference; inexperienced female investors favor female founders.
• Founders with trustworthy-looking faces attract more pledges — a stronger effect for women.
Race
• In equity crowdfunding, African American men are significantly less likely than similar white founders to be funded — backers rate identical projects lower when they believe the founder is a Black male.
• In online lending, after adjusting for risk, Latinx and African American borrowers are charged significantly higher mortgage rates.
Algorithms & mitigation
• Machine-learning underwriting can absorb the human biases (gender, race, income) baked into its training data — then reproduce them at scale.
• What helps: pre-treat training data to counter bias; add third-party endorsements and proof of prior success; strip indicators of the founder’s race.

§7.4 · Equity crowdfunding
21 / 31
Selling ownership stakes to the crowd · just 4% of dollars, the most regulated

All-or-nothing vs. keep-it-all.

All-or-nothing. Money is collected only if the campaign hits its target; otherwise pledges are returned. Entrepreneurs bear more risk but raise more on average — and higher-quality, more innovative projects succeed more often.
Keep-it-all. Any money pledged goes to the sponsor even if the target is missed. Lower risk for the entrepreneur, but a weaker quality signal to investors.

Because most start-ups fail, regulators cap retail exposure: US Reg CF limits investment to the greater of $2,200 or 5% of income (10% above $150K income); Canada caps CA$2,500 per deal (CA$10,000 with dealer advice). Accredited-only platforms like AngelList face fewer rules.

It’s not all pure equity. The “equity” family includes ownership stakes in real estate (REIT-like exposure) and royalty / profit-sharing models that trade a share of future revenue for capital. In 2020 real-estate crowdfunding ($2.8B) actually raised more than pure equity stakes ($1.5B).
What makes a campaign succeed
Disclosure
Detailed, credible business information; risk disclosure itself signals quality.
Skin in the game
Founders retaining equity reassures investors of commitment.
Track record
Serial entrepreneurs leverage social capital from past campaigns.

A patchwork of rules. Some countries fold portals into existing bank/securities law (Germany, Singapore); others wrote new fintech-credit rules (UK, France, Switzerland). In Canada, online loans are securities — prospectus costs effectively exclude retail investors. The fintech charter. After years of legal challenges, the OCC granted its first special-purpose national bank charter to SoFi in January 2022 — bringing it “inside the federal bank regulatory perimeter… subject to the full panoply of bank regulations.”

§7.11 · Did online portals disrupt?
30 / 31
Scoring crowdfunding against the five functions of a financial intermediary

Disruption scorecard.

The early hope was that portals would replace banks. Judged on what intermediaries actually do, they succeeded on one dimension, half-succeeded on another, and failed on three.

Reduced ✓
Information asymmetry
More disclosure, transparency, and monitoring — at least for this micro-segment of finance.
Partial ≈
Transaction costs
Cheaper, faster to post a campaign — but still high origination fees and steep rates for low-rated borrowers.
Failed ×
Liquidity
Almost no way to resell a loan or security bought on a portal — you are locked in.
Failed ×
Risk sharing
No skin in the game except balance-sheet lenders. Investors bear the full default risk.
Failed ×
Trust
Same fraud, misrepresentation, and principal-agent problems as the legacy system.
Verdict. Online portals did not disrupt traditional capital raising — they became an additional digital channel for it, carrying some of fintech’s advantages but few of its promised revolutions.
