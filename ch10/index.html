§10.3 · The crisis opens the door
04 / 13
Two hurdles for new entrants — regulation and trust

2008–09 lowered both barriers.

Regulation eased. Banking is a low-margin commodity business (profit margins below 2% on average) that only generates returns through leverage — and high leverage means high risk. Post-crisis, angry voters pushed governments to ring-fence banks and to boost competition: many adopted open banking and waved through fintech start-ups.
Trust collapsed. A 2012 Guardian survey put trust in banking at a five-year low — 71% felt banks had not learned their lesson. The damage was worst among the young: a 2016 survey found 71% of millennials would rather visit the dentist than listen to their banks.

The crisis also unleashed a wave of innovation. Disgruntled insiders left to launch fintech start-ups — they knew exactly where the industry profit pools were located and set about draining them while incumbents were weak and customers were willing to switch.

§10.4 · Open banking fuels disruption
05 / 13
“Data is the new oil” — but banks hoard it

From screen scraping to APIs.

The data belongs to the customer, yet banks guard it. The traditional workaround — screen scraping — forces consumers to hand over their login and password, violating user agreements and creating real security risk. More than 4 million Canadians have done it anyway.

Screen scraping creates “significant risk to consumers’ financial health, including the potential for account takeover, unauthorized payment transactions, and identity theft.”
— US bank CEO, written Senate testimony
The safer alternative · open banking via API
An API lets two computers exchange data securely over a network — used inside banks and with partners like VISA and MasterCard for decades.
Open banking came into force in 2018 in the UK and EU, with countries worldwide following. It gives customers control: a bank must share data with an authorized third party via a secure API, and the customer can revoke access at any time.
A double-edged sword. Research finds open banking promotes competition when it levels borrower screening — but if it hands fintechs monopoly power over data, it can hinder competition and leave all borrowers worse off.

§10.5 · Fintech app developers
06 / 13
Solve one pain point — then add more

Unbundle, then rebundle.

Unbundling means picking one profitable bank product and offering a better, cheaper version — avoiding the loss-leaders (like savings accounts) that drag bank margins. Once a foothold is established with early adopters, rebundling adds complementary products to make customers sticky and pull rivals’ customers over.

App ingredients for success
Onboarding · simple, intuitive UX
Security · APIs and ML, not screen scraping
Personalization · big-data targeting; gamification for engagement
B2C channels: one-time fee, in-app purchase, subscription, or freemium.

§10.5 · Pivoting from B2C to B2B
07 / 13
Acquiring consumers costs $500–$1,000 each — so sell to banks instead

Banks become the perfect customer.

Many fintechs found a consumer business too costly to scale, and older customers still trust incumbents. Banks have huge customer bases, deep pockets, and regulatory expertise but are slow and weighed down by legacy IT — so fintechs license their apps via a software-as-a-service (SaaS) model, often white-labeled under the bank’s brand.

Sensibill · Toronto, 2013. A receipt-management app: snap or forward a receipt, OCR converts the image, and an ML algorithm extracts up to 150 data points for tagging, search, expense reports, and warranty reminders. Raised CA$2M seed in 2015; white-labeled to four Canadian banks, then expanded to the US (2017) and UK (2018) — the bank’s customer never sees the Sensibill name.
Moven · US, 2010. A personal-finance app built on behavioral economics and AI: it aggregates card and credit data, surfaces real-time spending insights, and flags suboptimal decisions — “make smarter decisions and increase savings.” TD Bank embedded it as TD MySpend. By 2022 Moven had rebranded as a financial-wellness platform serving bank and non-bank partners.
The bank gains an innovative product without building it; the fintech gains a large customer base and stable recurring revenue attractive to investors. The licensing terms stay undisclosed — they can be a source of competitive advantage.

§10.6 · Challenger banks
08 / 13
Neobank · digital bank · online bank — banking, done electronically

Open an account in under 10 minutes.

The end-to-end digital experience sets a challenger bank apart: no branch, no paper, no face-to-face meeting (just KYC ID upload). Open 24/7/365, with intuitive dashboards, spending stats, embedded fintech tools, education, and chatbots that escalate to live help.

The playbook · foothold → rebundle → expand
01
Single product
Launch one product at home for retail and small business — N26 a free deposit account; Revolut a multi-currency prepaid card.
02
Rebundle via API
Add products directly or via partners. Starling’s 2018 marketplace had 10 fintech partners by 2022; N26 uses Wise and Allianz.
03
Cross borders
Expand to neighbors — N26 across Europe to the US and Brazil; Revolut to the US, Australia, Japan, Singapore.
04
Licensed or not
A banking license unlocks insured deposits; without one, partner an FDIC bank. Chime had to drop “bank” from its ads in 2021.
COVID-19 lit the fuse. Time on digital banking apps rose 35% year-over-year in the US and up to 85% in emerging markets. Challenger-bank downloads topped 264 million worldwide, led by Brazil and Latin America — now 300+ challenger banks globally.

§10.7 · Financial marketplaces
11 / 13
Multi-sided platforms built on cloud, APIs, and open banking

A full-service bank without the capital.

A financial marketplace connects customers to third-party products — banking, payments, crypto, investments, insurance — charging the seller a fee or commission, plus advertising. The goal: build an ecosystem with network effects, no banking license required.

Germany · 2009
Fidor
“Bank-as-a-marketplace” — an infrastructure platform with a banking license. Serves 120,000 directly; Fidor Market offers third-party loans, crowdfunding, FX, and crypto.
Canada · 2014
Borrowell
Launched as an online lender, struggled to find qualified borrowers, then gave away free credit scores. 1M+ Canadians drew it to pivot into a marketplace with 40+ financial partners.
San Francisco · 2007
Credit Karma
Free credit scores hooked 4M users in four years. Diversified into cards, loans, tax, and identity monitoring — 85M users by 2018, valued at $4B.
The $7B exit. Credit Karma’s formula — give away a free product, build a multi-sided platform, then monetize via commissions from banks selling on it — reached ~half of all US millennials. Intuit acquired it in 2020 for $7 billion.

§10.8 · The response from incumbents
12 / 13
“Silicon Valley is coming.” — Jamie Dimon, 2014

A menu of seven responses.

The right strategy depends on capabilities and scale. Giants like JPMorgan build, invest, and acquire; the 5,000+ smaller US deposit-takers lack the scale and instead license and partner. The biggest still hold competitive moats — regulation, customer bases, funding, and budgets to outspend fintechs.

Beware innovation theater. BBVA opened the first bank innovation lab in 2007; dozens followed 2014–17 — but with little to show. Too often labs are “techwashing” ribbon-cuttings that never reach production.

Chapter 10 · Closing
13 / 13
Findings · unbundle, rebundle, and partner

Better experience — harder economics.

Fintechs attacked banking’s pain points, enabled by post-crisis distrust and open banking. App developers unbundle one product, then rebundle — many pivoting from costly B2C to B2B SaaS. Challenger banks rebundle a full bank digitally; financial marketplaces use APIs to sell third-party products and earn network effects. Yet customer acquisition is brutal and profits elusive — Nubank reached 70M customers while still losing money annually. Incumbents answer with a menu: build, R&D, partner, license, invest, acquire, or target fintechs as customers.
