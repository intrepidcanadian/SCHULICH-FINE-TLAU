§10.7 · Financial marketplaces
11 / 13
Multi-sided platforms built on cloud, APIs, and open banking

A full-service bank without the capital.

A financial marketplace connects customers to third-party products — banking, payments, crypto, investments, insurance — charging the seller a fee or commission, plus advertising. The goal: build an ecosystem with network effects, no banking license required.

Germany · 2009
Fidor
“Bank-as-a-marketplace” — an infrastructure platform with a banking license. Serves 120,000 directly; Fidor Market offers third-party loans, crowdfunding, FX, and crypto.
Canada · 2014
Borrowell
Launched as an online lender, struggled to find qualified borrowers, then gave away free credit scores. 1M+ Canadians drew it to pivot into a marketplace with 40+ financial partners.
San Francisco · 2007
Credit Karma
Free credit scores hooked 4M users in four years. Diversified into cards, loans, tax, and identity monitoring — 85M users by 2018, valued at $4B.
The $7B exit. Credit Karma’s formula — give away a free product, build a multi-sided platform, then monetize via commissions from banks selling on it — reached ~half of all US millennials. Intuit acquired it in 2020 for $7 billion.
