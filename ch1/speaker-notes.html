Chapter 1: Fintech Defined — Speaker Notes

FINE 6280 · Fintech Explained · Schulich School of Business · Lecture 01 of 14
Total target time: ~50 minutes. Part I (Slides 1-5): ~18 min. Part II — The Lens (Slides 6-11): ~22 min. Closing — Financial inclusion (Slide 12): ~10 min. The 2024–2026 data-update slides (formerly slides 12–23) live in the locked Ch 1 Appendix deck for reference.
Slide 01 — Title: Fintech Defined
~2 min
  • Welcome the class. Introduce yourself and the course structure: 14 lectures, one chapter of the textbook each week, building toward a group consulting project.
  • Frame the course goal: by week 14, students should be able to take any fintech firm and decompose it analytically using the lens introduced today.
  • Set expectations: this is a graduate seminar, not a survey. We care about frameworks, not just company names. Participation counts.
  • Point out the required reading: Chapter 1 of Fintech Explained. Ask if everyone has access.
Transition: "Let's start with what seems like a simple question but actually isn't — what is fintech?"
Slide 02 — What is Fintech?
~4 min
  • Start with the definitional challenge: fintech conflates an industry label with a process. Ask the class: "Is Goldman Sachs a fintech? Is Shopify? Why or why not?" Use this to surface the boundary problem.
  • Walk through the three value propositions (speed, access, cost) with concrete examples. T+1 settlement was just implemented by the SEC in May 2024 — ask who follows capital markets and whether they noticed the change.
  • Emphasize the Arner et al. (2015) insight: fintech is a method, not a sector. This is the intellectual foundation for everything that follows.
  • Pause on M-Pesa's 51M active users — ask students if any of them have used mobile money or know someone who has. This anchors the "access" pillar in lived experience.
  • Flag that Wise's FX compression from 3% to 0.4-0.7% is a concrete example of disintermediation rents — we will return to this when discussing the "How" question of monetization.
Transition: "So it's big, it's definitionally tricky, but how big exactly? Let's look at the numbers."
Slide 03 — Growth: $1.2T / 38K Deals
~3 min
  • Let the numbers land: $1.2 trillion cumulative private capital, 38,000+ deals. This is not a niche — it is a structural reallocation of venture and PE capital toward financial services.
  • Draw attention to the cyclicality in Figure 1.1: the 2021 peak at ~$240B was ZIRP-driven euphoria; the 2023 trough at $113B represents the hangover. Ask: "What does this tell us about whether fintech is maturing or was in a bubble?"
  • Point to Figure 1.2 (media mentions) as evidence that the term barely existed before 2014. The discourse created the category, which then attracted capital. Performativity in markets.
  • Foreshadow: we will see the 2025 rebound data on slide 14 — capital is flowing back, but differently.
Transition: "To understand where we are, we need to understand how we got here. Three eras."
Slide 04 — Three Eras of Fintech
~4 min
  • Walk through each era chronologically. For Era 1 (1858-1967): emphasize that technology was built BY and FOR incumbents — there was no disruption threat. The transatlantic cable, Fedwire, telex — all infrastructure that banks controlled.
  • For Era 2 (1967-2008): the Barclays ATM in 1967 is the symbolic start. Ask: "Why did banks digitize?" Answer: cost reduction and customer convenience, but the value chain stayed vertically integrated. Goldman's terminal didn't threaten Goldman.
  • For Era 3 (2006-present): stress the three catalysts — LendingClub/Zopa launching P2P lending (2006), AWS enabling cloud-native startups (2006), and the GFC creating a trust deficit (2007-08). New entrants pushed services OUTSIDE the regulated perimeter.
  • Mention "Fintech 3.5" for developing economies — mobile leapfrogging where formal banking never fully penetrated. We will return to this on slide 24 with financial inclusion.
  • Ask the class: "Are we now in a fourth era? What would define it?" Hold the question — slides 12-22 will propose an answer.
Transition: "Within these eras, firms pursue fundamentally different strategies. Two paradigms."
Slide 05 — Two Paradigms: Traditional vs. Transformational
~5 min
  • Frame this as Kuhn meets Christensen. Traditional fintech = sustaining innovation. JPMorgan's $15.3B tech spend makes existing services faster and cheaper. Transformational fintech = new cost structures that make the old model uncompetitive.
  • Use the "faster horses vs. a different car" metaphor. Ask: "Is Stripe a faster horse or a different car?" The answer is nuanced — it started as transformational (unbundled merchant acquiring) but is now itself an incumbent being attacked.
  • Point to Ant Group as the most dramatic example: re-bundling payments + credit + wealth. Yu'e Bao's $268B AUM peak meant a money-market fund inside a payments app — that is a fundamentally different architecture than what banks offered.
  • Discuss Table 1.1 briefly. The key diagnostic question: does the firm require the existing regulated perimeter to function, or does it create value precisely by operating outside it?
  • This distinction will be the backbone of how we evaluate every case study this term. Flag that we will revisit this paradigm distinction after seeing the data slides on GenAI deployment by banks (slide 17) — traditional fintech is scaling faster than people expected.
Transition: "Now that we have definitions and history, let's build the analytical toolkit you'll use all term. Part II: The Lens."
Slide 06 — The Lens (Section Divider)
~2 min
  • Signal a gear shift: we are moving from "what is fintech" to "how do we analyze any fintech." The Lens is the analytical framework for the entire course.
  • Preview the four moves: Who (customer), What (pain point), Why (value proposition), How (monetization). These map to the four questions on the next slide.
  • Point to Figure 1.3 and explain that every subsequent chapter's case studies will be run through this framework. Their group projects will use it as well.
  • Emphasize: the Lens is sequential. You cannot answer "Why will customers pay?" without first knowing WHO they are and WHAT pain they feel. Order matters.
Transition: "Four questions. Let me walk you through each one with examples."
Slide 07 — Four Questions
~5 min
  • Walk through each question with a live example. Take Wealthsimple: Who? = Canadian millennials underserved by Big 5 banks. What? = high MER fees (2%+) and intimidating advisor minimums. Why? = lower cost (0.4-0.5% management fee), simpler UX, no minimums. How? = AUM-based fee + premium subscription tiers.
  • For Question 1 (Who): stress B2B2C as the often-overlooked model. Borrowell doesn't sell to consumers directly in the traditional sense — it uses free credit scores to generate lead-gen revenue from lenders. That changes everything about unit economics.
  • For Question 2 (What): connect to the value propositions from slide 02. Every pain point maps to one of: cost, speed, access, convenience.
  • For Question 3 (Why): the four value-prop adjectives are "cheaper, easier, faster, more convenient." Ask students: "Can a firm deliver ALL four simultaneously, or do they have to pick?" Discuss trade-offs.
  • For Question 4 (How): flag the variety of monetization models — take-rate (Stripe), spread (Nubank), subscription (Plaid), data (many "free" apps). Ask: "If a fintech app is free to the consumer, who is the real customer?"
Transition: "Now that we know HOW to ask the questions, let's categorize WHAT fintechs actually do. Eight categories."
Slide 08 — Eight Categories of Fintech
~4 min
  • Walk through the eight categories briskly — do not spend equal time on all. The purpose is to show breadth and to note that MOST firms span 2-3 categories. The analytical task is identifying which category is load-bearing for unit economics.
  • Ask: "Where does Stripe fit?" (Payments primarily, but also digital banking via Treasury, and increasingly lending via Stripe Capital.) "Where does Nubank fit?" (Digital banking + payments + online lending + wealth management.) The best firms are category-spanning platforms.
  • Highlight the DeFi category as distinct from crypto — crypto is a store-of-value play; DeFi is about replacing intermediaries with smart contracts. Different thesis, different risk.
  • Note Nubank's ~131M customers as of Q4 2025 — larger than most Canadian banks' entire customer base. Scale is not just a Silicon Valley phenomenon.
  • Flag that we will dedicate one lecture to each of the major categories over the term: payments (Ch. 5), lending (Ch. 6), wealth management (Ch. 7), crypto/DeFi (Ch. 8-9), insurance (Ch. 10).
Transition: "Categories tell us WHAT firms do. But WHO are these firms? Four types."
Slide 09 — Four Firm Types
~4 min
  • Frame this as a competitive topology. Each type has different strengths and weaknesses (Table 1.2). The strategic question is: which strengths are most valuable in the current environment?
  • For Type 1 (Start-Up): they have innovation culture and technical agility, but lack capital, scale, brand, and regulatory expertise. Ask: "What percentage of fintech startups fail?" (roughly the same rate as other VC-backed startups — ~90%). Why?
  • For Type 2 (Mature Fintech): PayPal, Square/Block — they combine innovation with scale, but are lighter on regulatory depth. They are attackable from above (Big Tech) and below (nimbler start-ups).
  • For Type 3 (Digital-Only Bank): distinguish born-digital (Nubank, Chime) from incumbent-launched (Marcus by Goldman, JP Morgan Chase's digital bank). Different DNA, different constraints.
  • For Type 4 (Global Tech Platform): Apple, Google, Amazon, Tencent. They bring unmatched data and distribution. Ask: "Is Apple Pay a fintech or a distribution play for hardware?" This is where the boundary problem from slide 02 gets real.
Transition: "The last piece of the Lens: what technology stack makes all of this possible? Ten technologies."
Slide 10 — Ten Technologies
~4 min
  • Do NOT read all ten descriptions. Instead, explain the analytical use: for any firm, ask "which of the ten, in what combination, and which is the defensibility bottleneck?"
  • Give a worked example: Stripe = APIs + Cloud Computing + Multi-Sided Platform. The defensibility bottleneck is the API network effect — the more merchants integrate, the harder it is to switch. That is not AI or blockchain; it is APIs.
  • Contrast with Uniswap: Blockchain + Smart Contracts + P2P Network. The defensibility bottleneck is liquidity (network effects again), not the code (which is open-source and forkable).
  • Note that AI/ML and Big Data are enabling layers that appear across almost every category. Ask: "Is AI a fintech category or an infrastructure layer?" (Answer: layer — like electricity.)
  • Point out that several "technologies" on this list (Multi-Sided Platform, P2P Network) are really business models or architectural patterns, not technologies per se. Ask students if they think the taxonomy is clean. This is a good place for brief Socratic pushback.
Transition: "Technologies don't deploy in a vacuum. They need an ecosystem. Let's zoom out."
Slide 11 — The Fintech Ecosystem
~3 min
  • Emphasize that fintechs are embedded in an ecosystem: accelerators, regulators, VCs, universities, talent pools. The firm-level analysis from the Lens is necessary but not sufficient — you also need to understand the environment.
  • Point to Table 1.3 (2020 ecosystem rankings) and note that these rankings shift rapidly. Silicon Valley, London, Singapore lead, but the field is becoming more multipolar. We will see updated data on slide 15.
  • Connect to Canadian context: where does Toronto/Canada sit in this ecosystem? MaRS, the Ontario Securities Commission sandbox, Wealthsimple, Nuvei, Lightspeed. Ask students: "What would it take for Toronto to break into the global top 5?"
  • Mention regulatory sandboxes (UK FCA 2016, MAS Singapore) as a competitive tool — governments actively competing for fintech capital through regulatory design.
Transition: "That was the textbook framework — the Lens as published. But the textbook was written in a different market. Let me show you what happened since. Part III: The 2024-2026 wave."
Slide 12 — Financial Inclusion & Closing
~4 min
  • End on purpose. 1.7B adults remain unbanked, but two-thirds own a mobile phone. Over the last decade, 1.2B people gained access primarily via mobile money (M-Pesa, GCash, bKash). Fintech is not just about making rich people's money move faster — it is infrastructure for economic development.
  • Connect to the "Fintech 3.5" concept from slide 04: in developing economies, mobile leapfrogging preceded formal banking. The next billion users will not get bank accounts — they will get stablecoin wallets on feature phones.
  • Ask: "What responsibility do fintech firms have for financial inclusion? Is it a market opportunity, a moral obligation, or both?" This connects to governance and ESG themes we will explore later in the course.
  • Preview Chapter 2: Theories and Business Models. They should read Chapter 2 before next class. The Lens from today will be applied to specific theoretical frameworks (network effects, two-sided markets, Christensen's disruptive innovation).
  • Remind students of any assignments due, office hours, and how to reach you. Thank them for their participation today.
End of lecture. Next session: Chapter 2 — Theories & Business Models.
