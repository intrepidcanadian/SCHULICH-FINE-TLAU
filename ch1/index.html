§1.1 · Definition
02 / 12
Definitional boundaries

What is fintech?

The application of digital technology to deliver, augment, or fundamentally redesign financial services — spanning payments, credit intermediation, asset management, risk transfer, and the market infrastructure beneath them.

Fintech is not a sector — it is a method. The definitional challenge (Arner, Barberis & Buckley 2015) is that “fintech” conflates an industry label with a process of institutional change.
— Arner et al., J. Fin. Perspectives (2015)
The value proposition
i. Speed
Settlement latency compression
T+2 → T+1 (SEC, May 2024). FedNow enables real-time gross settlement on the Fed side. Speed restructures counterparty risk.
ii. Access
Financial inclusion at scale
M-Pesa: 51M active users (2023). Alt-data scoring extends credit to thin-file populations excluded by FICO.
iii. Cost
Disintermediation rents
Wise cut FX spreads from ~3% to 0.4–0.7%. Software marginal cost replaces balance-sheet intermediation.

§1.3 · Historical periodisation
04 / 12
Periodisation · CFA Institute (2017) · after Arner, Barberis & Buckley

Three eras of fintech.

The canonical periodisation, formalised in a 2017 CFA Institute study (after Arner, Barberis & Buckley 2015), frames fintech as three overlapping waves — each defined by who controlled the technology and what regulatory bargain prevailed.

Era 01 · 1858 — 1967
Fintech 1.0 · Infrastructure
Analog-to-electronic wiring: transatlantic cable (1858), electronic ticker tape (1867), Fedwire (1918), telex networks. Technology built by and for incumbents. The plumbing era — no disintermediation threat.
Era 02 · 1967 — 2008
Fintech 2.0 · Digitisation
Barclays ATM (1967), NASDAQ electronic exchange (1971), SWIFT (1973), Bloomberg Terminal (1981), online banking (mid-1990s). Bank-led digitisation; customers migrate to screens but the value chain stays vertically integrated.
Era 03 · 2006 — present
Fintech 3.0 · Democratisation
P2P lending launches (LendingClub & Zopa, 2006), AWS cloud (2006), iPhone (2007), GFC trust deficit (2007–08). New entrants push services outside the regulated perimeter. Arner et al. note a “Fintech 3.5” variant in developing economies where mobile leapfrogging precedes formal banking.

§1.4 · Paradigms
05 / 12
Kuhn (1962) meets financial intermediation

Traditional fintech vs. transformational fintech.

i. Traditional (sustaining)

Faster horses.

Incumbents optimise existing value chains — JPMorgan $15.3B tech spend (2023), Goldman’s Marcus, DBS digibank. Same activity, lower unit cost.

ii. Transformational (disruptive)

A different car.

Stripe unbundled merchant acquiring; Ant Group re-bundled payments + credit + wealth (Yu’e Bao: $268B AUM peak). New cost structures create new defaults.

§2.1 · The four questions
07 / 12
Step 1 of the lens · analytical scaffolding

Four questions.

Apply these to any fintech firm before reading their S-1 or pitch deck.

Question · 01
Who? — Customer
Who is the fintech’s customer? Identify the target segment: B2C (individuals, SMEs), B2B (banks, insurers), B2B2C (via intermediaries like Borrowell), or C2C/P2P (crypto exchanges). Product-market fit starts here.
Question · 02
What? — Pain Point
What customer pain point is the fintech solving? Cost (high interchange), speed (T+2 settlement), access (thin-file exclusion), convenience (driving to a branch). The pain point is the market opportunity.
Question · 03
Why? — Value Proposition
Why will customers pay? The fintech value proposition: cheaper (commission-free), easier (simple online process), faster (send cash in seconds), and more convenient (one app, all things money).
Question · 04
How? — Monetization
How does the fintech make money? Fees, commissions, subscriptions (SaaS), advertising, data sales, risk-taking spread. Also: PaaS, IaaS, BaaS models. Stripe = take-rate; Nubank = deposit spread.

§2.2 · Categories of activity
08 / 12
Step 2 of the lens · activity-based taxonomy

Eight categories of fintech.

Activity-based taxonomy from Fintech Explained (Ch. 1). Most firms span 2–3; identify which is load-bearing for unit economics.

01
Cryptocurrencies
Electronic money and tokens on blockchain ledgers secured by cryptography. Bitcoin (2009), Ethereum, stablecoins (USDC, PYUSD).
02
DeFi
An “internet of money” built on blockchain using smart contracts and dapps for secure P2P transactions without human intervention.
03
Online Lending
Digital platforms (SoFi, Funding Circle, Upstart) allowing individuals and businesses to borrow from other individuals, banks, and institutional investors. Also called debt crowdfunding.
04
Crowdfunding
Platforms (Kickstarter, Indiegogo) allowing individuals, businesses, and social causes to raise capital via equity, donations, and rewards.
05
Digital Banking
Nubank (~131M customers, Q4 2025), Chime, Starling. Fintechs providing day-to-day banking and personal finance management digitally.
06
Digital Wealth Mgmt
Betterment, Wealthsimple, Robinhood. Robo-advisory, automated portfolio management, fractional shares, zero-commission brokerage.
07
Payments
Stripe ($159B val., Feb 2026), Adyen, Wise. Digital payments, money transfers, international remittances, and payments processing software.
08
InsurTech
Lemonade, Root, Zhong An. Digital insurance distribution, AI underwriting, parametric products, data analytics for insurers.

§2.3 · Firm types
09 / 12
Step 3 of the lens · competitive topology

Four kinds of firm.

The textbook identifies four types of fintech companies, each with different strengths in innovation culture, technical expertise, customer base, data, scale, capital, and brand.

Type · 01
Entrepreneurial Start-Up
Low revenues, <10 employees, owned by founders and early-stage investors. Innovation culture and technical expertise, but limited capital, scale, and brand.
Type · 02
Mature Company
Thousands of employees, high revenues, stock exchange listing. PayPal, Square. Innovation culture plus scale, capital, customers, and brand — but lighter on regulatory and risk-management depth.
Type · 03
Digital-Only Bank
Chime, Nubank, Revolut, N26. Incumbent-launched or born-digital. Distinguished by regulatory and risk-management expertise plus financial depth — thinner on innovation culture, scale, and customer reach.
Type · 04
Global Tech Platform
Apple, Google, Amazon, Shopify, Ant Group, Tencent. Bundle financial and non-financial services. Unmatched scale, data, and brand. Also called bigtech or techfin.

§2.4 · Ten technologies
10 / 12
Step 4 of the lens · the technology stack

Ten technologies.

Every fintech firm is built from a subset. For each firm we ask: which of the ten, in what combination, and which is the defensibility bottleneck?

01
APIs
Messenger between computers. Open APIs drive PSD2 / Open Banking (EU 2016, UK 2016), letting licensed fintechs access customer data.
02
AI & ML
Machines mimicking human intelligence. Three learning modes: supervised, unsupervised, and reinforcement learning.
03
Big Data
“Data is the new oil” (Clive Humby, 2006). 90% of world’s data created in two years (Forbes, 2018). Critical input to AI/ML.
04
Blockchain & DLT
Distributed ledger from Bitcoin (Nakamoto, 2008). SHA-256 hash secures 1 MB blocks. DLT synchronises copies across nodes.
05
Cloud Computing
Pioneered by AWS (2006). Enables SaaS, PaaS, IaaS, and BaaS (Banking-as-a-Service) for fintechs to embed banking via APIs.
06
Cryptography
Secures digital currencies via irreversible hash algorithms (SHA-256). Makes encryption inexpensive and widely available.
07
Ethereum
Programmable blockchain conceived by Vitalik Buterin (late 2013) and launched mid-2014. Platform for dapps and smart contracts, powered by ether (ETH) gas fees.
08
Multi-Sided Platform
Digital business connecting two+ user groups to transact online. Model used by Google, Amazon, and Tencent.
09
P2P Network
Decentralised network where computers have equal access. Crypto and DeFi run on distributed networks with no supernode.
10
Smart Contract
Self-executing code on blockchain: “if A happens, then B follows.” Developed for Ethereum, encrypted to prevent alteration.

§2.5 · The fintech ecosystem
11 / 12
Ecosystem · actors, institutions, infrastructure

The fintech ecosystem.

Fintechs do not emerge in isolation. They depend on a diverse ecosystem of innovation labs, accelerators (Y Combinator, Level39), technology hubs, investors, regulators, and universities.

Key actors
Fintech companies — start-ups to decacorns. Investors — angels, VCs, PE, strategic (incumbents). Regulators — sandboxes (UK FCA 2016, MAS, HKMA). Accelerators — Y Combinator, Techstars, Plug and Play.
Ecosystem competition
Cities and countries compete to attract fintech talent and capital. Regulatory clarity, tax incentives, and talent pools are the key differentiators. Silicon Valley, London, and Singapore lead; Hong Kong and Dubai are rising challengers.

The societal dimension

Financial inclusion.

1.7 billion adults remain unbanked (World Bank) — but two-thirds own a mobile phone. Over the last decade, 1.2 billion gained access primarily via mobile money. M-Pesa, GCash, and bKash demonstrate fintech as infrastructure for economic development.
