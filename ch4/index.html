§4.1 · The art of valuation
02 / 12
Negotiation · value is subjective

Valuation is an art, not a science.

Value is what someone will pay. The party in the stronger bargaining position steers the price. For early-stage companies with no revenue history, qualitative factors — team, idea, market size — dominate. Traditional models simply do not work.

The gap between the buyer’s offer price and the seller’s asking price creates the zone of possible agreement (ZOPA). This is where bargaining takes place.
— Fintech Explained, Ch. 4
Founder trade-off
Ownership vs. Growth Capital
Founders must give up equity to raise capital for growth. Understanding the other party’s incentives and interests is the secret to bargaining. Not everyone is rational — behavioral biases shape negotiations.
Fundamental (intrinsic) value
Bottom-Up Models
Spreadsheets filled with assumptions about sales, margins, and earnings. Any analyst can manipulate inputs to justify their desired price. Models are negotiating tools, not reality.
Relative value
Market Multiples of Comparables
Standardized metrics (P/E, EV/Sales, EV/EBITDA) based on similar companies. Change the choice of comparables or the ratio, and you can justify any price. Another negotiating tactic.
At the end of the day, both fundamental and relative valuation models are negotiating tools — used to justify a lower price for a buyer or a higher price for a seller. That’s it.
— Fintech Explained, Ch. 4

§4.2 · Four steps in valuation
03 / 12
Framework · universal method

Four steps.

Most valuation methodologies boil down to four steps: identify value drivers, forecast the future, discount to present value, and divide the pie.

01
Identify
Find value drivers: top-down (TAM, macro) and bottom-up (firm-specific: leadership, brand, unit economics). GIGO principle applies.
02
Forecast
Project future sales and margins using scenario analysis: base, bull, and bear cases with probability weights. Private data is scarce.
03
Discount
PV = FV / (1+r%)^n. Discount each future cash flow using the IRR. $100 at 10% for 7 years = $51.32 today. Compounding works exponentially.
04
Divide
EV = Net Debt + Equity. Creditors get repaid first; shareholders own the residual. Use as-converted basis for SAFEs and convertible notes.
Enterprise Value (EV) = Total Debt − Cash + Preferred + Common + NCI. A going-concern assumption means assets generate cash flows into the future — always higher than liquidation value.
— Fintech Explained, Ch. 4

§4.3 · Angel valuation · Payne Scorecard
04 / 12
Angel investors · scorecards

Bill Payne Scorecard.

A relative valuation method that starts from an average pre-money valuation of $2M for recent angel-funded deals in the region, then adjusts up or down across seven dimensions based on due diligence and expert judgment.

Seven dimensions
1. Strength of entrepreneur & team (30%) · 2. Size of opportunity (25%) · 3. Product / technology (15%) · 4. Competitive environment (10%) · 5. Marketing & sales channels (10%) · 6. Need for additional investment (5%) · 7. Other factors (5%)
In the textbook example, adjustments sum to 115.5% of the $2M base, yielding a pre-money valuation of $2.31M. The scorecard is subjective — two angels may reach different conclusions from the same data.
— Fintech Explained, Ch. 4
10% of angel investments = 90% of total returns·Portfolio avg: 2.6× over 3.5 yr (~27% IRR)

§4.3 · Berkus & Risk Factor Summation

Angel valuation · two more methods

Berkus & Risk Factor Summation.

Dave Berkus Method
Max Pre-Money: $2.5M
Base $500K for a sound idea + up to $500K each for four risk categories: (1) working prototype (technology risk), (2) quality team (execution risk), (3) product rollout/sales (production risk), (4) strategic relationships (market risk). First published 1996, updated 2016. Berkus has invested in 100+ start-ups.
Tab. 4.3 · Berkus Method Example = $1.9MSource: Fintech Explained
Risk Factor Summation
Ohio TechAngels · 12 Risk Categories
Starts from average regional valuation (e.g. $2M). Adjust −2 to +2 for each of 12 risks: management, stage, legislation, manufacturing, sales/marketing, funding, competition, technology, litigation, international, reputation, and exit potential. Each unit = ±$250K. Example yields $2.25M.

§4.4 · VC valuation method
06 / 12
Growth-stage valuation

The VC Method.

VCs target an IRR of 25–35% over 6–10 years. They work backward from the terminal value (TV) at exit, adjust the discount rate for bankruptcy risk, and back out their investment today.

TV = Net Income × Exit Multiple·Discount Rate = [(1 + IRR) / P(survival)] − 1
Worked Example — Series A
Assumptions: 1M pre-money shares (founders + angels), $2M profit in yr 5, 20× P/E exit multiple, 25% target IRR, 20% annual survival probability, $2.5M VC investment, net debt = $0.
Step 1–2
TV & Discount Rate
TV = $2M × 20 = $40M. Discount rate = (1.25/0.20)−1 = 56.25% p.a.
Step 3–4
Post- & Pre-Money
PV = $40M/(1.5625)^5 = $4.3M post-money. Pre-money = $4.3M − $2.5M = $1.8M.
Step 5–7
Ownership & Price
VC owns 58.2%. Post-money shares = 1M/0.418 = 2.39M. Price = $4.3M/2.39M = $1.79/share.
The VC discount rate of 56.25% accounts for 80% annual failure probability. In practice, multiple funding rounds (Series A–F) and an ESOP pool make cap-table calculations far more complex.

§4.5 · Relative valuation
07 / 12
Mature companies · comparable analysis

Market multiples & precedent transactions.

Relative valuation standardizes company value using equity multiples (P/E) and enterprise multiples (EV/Sales, EV/EBITDA). Use 3–5 comparable companies; calculate average and median, dropping outliers. Precedent transactions add a control premium from completed acquisitions.

Tab. 4.5 · Common Market MultiplesSource: Fintech Explained
Three steps to relative valuation
1. Identify comparable companies or transactions (same industry, geography, size) · 2. Choose valuation multiples (LTM and NTM, equity and enterprise) · 3. Generate implied value for target (e.g. 15× P/E × $10M earnings = $150M)
Precedent example: acquisitions at 3×, 2×, 4×, 5×, 8× EV/Sales. Average = 4.4×; target with $50M sales = $220M implied value. Dropping the 8× outlier: 3.5× = $175M — 20% lower.
— Fintech Explained, Ch. 4

§4.6 · Discounted Cash Flow
08 / 12
Fundamental valuation · intrinsic value

The DCF model.

Forecast free cash flows (FCFF) over 5–10 years, discount at WACC (~10% for mature firms), then add a terminal value. Cannot be used for banks or financial intermediaries.

FCFF Formula (Eq. 6)
FCFF = EBIT(1−t) + D&A − CAPEX − ΔNWC
EBIT (not EBITDA) is the starting point. Tax uses marginal rate, not actual. D&A is non-cash so added back. CAPEX and working capital changes represent cash reinvested in the business.
Enterprise Value (Eq. 10)
EV = Σ FCFFt/(1+WACC)t + TV/(1+WACC)n
TV typically 70–80% of EV. Price per share = (EV − Net Debt − Preferred − NCI) / Fully diluted shares.
Terminal Value — Two Approaches
Perpetuity Growth vs. Exit Multiple
Perpetuity growth (Eq. 8): TV = FCFFt+1 / (WACC − g%). Use g% = 2–4% (long-term GDP growth). Example: $10M FCFF, 7% WACC, 3% growth = $257.5M.
Exit multiple (Eq. 9): TV = EBITDAt × EV/EBITDA. Example: $20M EBITDA × 12× = $240M. Both methods should produce similar TV.
Net Working Capital = (Current Assets − Cash) − (Current Liabilities − Short-term Debt). The change in NWC captures additional cash needed to support sales growth each year.
— Fintech Explained, Ch. 4 (Eq. 7)

§4.7 · ARK Invest & adjusted EBITDA
09 / 12
Disruptive valuation · Cathie Wood

ARK Invest & adjusted EBITDA.

ARK Invest argues GAAP metrics penalize disruptive companies investing heavily to scale. Wood proposes five adjustments to EBITDA. ARK rose to $50B AUM by Feb 2021, then collapsed ~70% to $16B by mid-2022.

Five GAAP Adjustments
01
R&D Expense
Add back. ARK median 28.5% vs. Nasdaq 100 median 12%. IFRS allows capitalization; GAAP expenses it all.
02
Sales & Marketing
Add back incremental portion. ARK median 33.2% vs. Nasdaq 17.2%. Customer acquisition cost for scaling.
03
Stock-Based Comp.
Add back as non-cash. ARK median 22.6% vs. Nasdaq 3.2% (7× higher). Like D&A treatment.
04
Deferred Revenue
Count when cash is received. Multi-year prepaid contracts signal validation, not a liability.
05
Asset Impairment
Add back one-time goodwill write-downs from market declines. Non-cash and non-recurring.
•
Skepticism Check
Wood’s incentive is to increase ARK ETF values. Market multiples can be manipulated — always verify how metrics are calculated.

Financial intermediaries · no DCF possible

Valuing banks: JPMorgan.

Banks lack sales and EBITDA, so enterprise multiples and DCF cannot be used. Analysts rely on equity multiples (P/E, P/Book) and financial ratios (NIM, efficiency ratio, ROA, ROE).

Cross-sector comparison

Fintech multiples: a wide spectrum.

Table 4.11 shows NTM multiples and financial ratios for seven listed fintechs. Extreme variation highlights why relative valuation is unreliable for younger fintech companies.

PayPal — most attractively valued?
Highest ROE (11.5%), highest ROA (3.1%), lowest P/E NTM (14.9×), lowest EV/EBITDA NTM (11.0×). But median P/E of 14.9× vs. average 29.5× shows extreme outlier distortion from negative-earnings fintechs.
Wise Plc — highest leverage
Assets/Equity = 18.5× (vs. median 3.4×). ROE of 14.4% but ROA only 0.6%. P/Book NTM of 11.0× — highest in the group. Wise operates more like a bank with customer deposits.
Negative earners
Square, Robinhood, SoFi, and Paytm all have negative ROE. P/E is meaningless when earnings are negative. This is why ARK’s adjusted EBITDA argument resonates — GAAP makes growth-stage companies look unprofitable.

The valuation toolkit

Price is what you pay. Value is what you get.

Early-stage: angel scorecards (Payne $2M base, Berkus max $2.5M, RFS ±$250K per risk). Growth-stage: VC method works backward from terminal value at 25–35% target IRR, adjusted for bankruptcy probability. Mature: market multiples of 3–5 comparables (P/E, EV/Sales, EV/EBITDA) or DCF with WACC ~10% and TV = 70–80% of EV. Banks: P/E and P/Book only (no DCF). Disruptive fintechs: GAAP metrics may penalize growth investing — adjusted EBITDA is one lens but always verify.
