§4.1 · The art of valuation
02 / 12
Negotiation · value is subjective

Valuation is an art, not a science.

Value is what someone will pay. The party in the stronger bargaining position steers the price. For early-stage companies with no revenue history, qualitative factors — team, idea, market — dominate. Traditional models do not work.

The gap between the buyer’s offer and the seller’s ask creates the zone of possible agreement (ZOPA). This is where bargaining takes place.
— Fintech Explained, Ch. 4
Founder trade-off
Ownership vs. Growth Capital
Founders give up equity to raise capital. Understanding the other party’s incentives is the secret to bargaining. Behavioral biases shape negotiations.
Fundamental (intrinsic) value
Bottom-Up Models
Spreadsheets of assumptions about sales, margins, earnings. Analysts can tune inputs to justify any price. Models are negotiating tools.
Relative value
Market Multiples of Comparables
P/E, EV/Sales, EV/EBITDA based on comps. Changing comparables or ratio changes the answer.

§4.2 · Four steps in valuation
03 / 12
Framework · universal method

Four steps.

Most valuation methodologies boil down to four steps: identify value drivers, forecast the future, discount to present value, and divide the pie.

01 · Identify
Value drivers: top-down (TAM, macro) and bottom-up (leadership, brand, unit economics). GIGO applies.
02 · Forecast
Project sales/margins via scenario analysis: base, bull, bear. Private data is scarce.
03 · Discount
PV = FV / (1+r)^n. $100 at 10% for 7 yrs = $51.32 today.
04 · Divide
EV = Net Debt + Equity. Creditors first; shareholders residual. Use as-converted for SAFEs/notes.
Enterprise Value = (Total Debt − Cash) + Equity, where Equity = Preferred + Common + NCI. Going concern always > liquidation.
— Fintech Explained, Ch. 4

§4.3 · Angel valuation · Payne Scorecard
04 / 12
Angel investors · scorecards

Bill Payne Scorecard.

A relative valuation that starts from an average pre-money valuation of $2M for recent angel-funded deals in the region, then adjusts up or down across seven dimensions.

Seven dimensions
1. Entrepreneur & team (30%) · 2. Opportunity (25%) · 3. Product / tech (15%) · 4. Competition (10%) · 5. Marketing / sales (10%) · 6. Need for additional capital (5%) · 7. Other (5%)
Textbook example: adjustments sum to 115.5% of $2M base → pre-money $2.31M. Subjective — two angels may reach different conclusions.
— Fintech Explained, Ch. 4
10% of angel investments = 90% of returns·Portfolio avg: 2.6× / 3.5 yr (~27% IRR)

§4.4 · VC valuation method
06 / 12
Growth-stage valuation

The VC Method.

VCs target an IRR of 25–35% over 6–10 years. They work backward from the terminal value (TV) at exit, adjust the discount rate for bankruptcy risk, and back out their investment today.

TV = Net Income × Exit Multiple·Discount Rate = [(1 + IRR) / P(survival)] − 1
Worked Example — Series A
Assumptions: 1M pre-money shares (founders + angels), $2M profit in yr 5, 20× P/E exit multiple, 25% target IRR, 20% annual survival probability, $2.5M VC investment, net debt = $0.
Step 1–2
TV & Discount Rate
TV = $2M × 20 = $40M. Discount rate = (1.25/0.20)−1 = 56.25% p.a.
Step 3–4
Post- & Pre-Money
PV = $40M/(1.5625)^5 = $4.3M post-money. Pre-money = $4.3M − $2.5M = $1.8M.
Step 5–7
Ownership & Price
VC owns 58.2%. Post-money shares = 1M/0.418 = 2.39M. Price = $4.3M/2.39M = $1.79/share.
The VC discount rate of 56.25% accounts for 80% annual failure probability. In practice, multiple funding rounds (Series A–F) and an ESOP pool make cap-table calculations far more complex.

§4.5 · Relative valuation

Mature companies · comparable analysis

Market multiples & precedent transactions.

Relative valuation standardizes value via equity multiples (P/E) and enterprise multiples (EV/Sales, EV/EBITDA). Use 3–5 comparables; calculate avg/median, drop outliers. Precedent transactions add a control premium.

Three steps to relative valuation
1. Identify comparables (industry, geo, size) · 2. Choose multiples (LTM/NTM, equity/enterprise) · 3. Generate implied value (15× P/E × $10M = $150M)
Precedent: 3×, 2×, 4×, 5×, 8× EV/Sales. Avg = 4.4×; $50M target → $220M. Drop outlier → 3.5× = $175M (−20%).

§4.6 · Discounted Cash Flow
08 / 12
Fundamental valuation · intrinsic value

The DCF model.

Forecast free cash flows (FCFF) over 5–10 years, discount at WACC (~10% for mature firms), then add a terminal value. Cannot be used for banks or financial intermediaries.

FCFF Formula (Eq. 6)
FCFF = EBIT(1−t) + D&A − CAPEX − ΔNWC
EBIT (not EBITDA) is the starting point. Tax uses marginal rate, not actual. D&A is non-cash so added back. CAPEX and working capital changes represent cash reinvested in the business.
Enterprise Value (Eq. 10)
EV = Σ FCFFt/(1+WACC)t + TV/(1+WACC)n
TV typically 70–80% of EV. Price per share = (EV − Net Debt − Preferred − NCI) / Fully diluted shares.
Terminal Value — Two Approaches
Perpetuity Growth vs. Exit Multiple
Perpetuity growth (Eq. 8): TV = FCFFt+1 / (WACC − g%). Use g% = 2–4% (long-term GDP growth). Example: $10M FCFF, 7% WACC, 3% growth = $257.5M.
Exit multiple (Eq. 9): TV = EBITDAt × EV/EBITDA. Example: $20M EBITDA × 12× = $240M. Both methods should produce similar TV.
Net Working Capital = (Current Assets − Cash) − (Current Liabilities − Short-term Debt). The change in NWC captures additional cash needed to support sales growth each year.
— Fintech Explained, Ch. 4 (Eq. 7)

§4.7 · ARK Invest & adjusted EBITDA
09 / 12
Disruptive valuation · Cathie Wood

ARK Invest & adjusted EBITDA.

ARK argues GAAP penalizes disruptors investing heavily to scale. Wood proposes five adjustments to EBITDA. ARK: $50B AUM Feb 2021 → $16B mid-2022 (−70%).

Five GAAP Adjustments
01 · R&D
Add back. ARK 28.5% vs. Nasdaq 12%. IFRS capitalizes; GAAP expenses.
02 · S&M
Add incremental. ARK 33.2% vs. Nasdaq 17.2%. CAC for scaling.
03 · SBC
Non-cash. ARK 22.6% vs. Nasdaq 3.2% (7× higher). Like D&A.
04 · Deferred Rev
Count when cash received. Multi-year prepaid signals validation.
05 · Impairment
Add back one-time goodwill write-downs. Non-cash, non-recurring.
Skeptic
Wood’s incentive is ETF valuations. Always verify metrics.

§4.8b · ROE decomposition
11 / 13
Bank profitability · the leverage multiplier

The ROE equation.

Banks transform thin margins into attractive returns through financial leverage. The decomposition reveals how ROA and the leverage multiplier interact — and why regulators watch both.

Equation 11 · ROE Decomposition
ROE = Net Income / Common Equity
= Net Income / Assets × Assets / Common Equity
= ROA × Financial Leverage
JPMorgan 2022 · Worked Example
ROA
0.6%
$38B NI / $3,666B Assets
(thin but typical for banks)
×
Leverage (A/E)
12.5×
$3,666B / $292B Equity
(92% debt-funded)
Result
ROE = 0.6% × 12.5 = 15.2%
A 0.6% ROA becomes 15.2% ROE through 12.5× leverage. Peers average 0.3% ROA and 11.5× leverage → 9.7% ROE. JPMorgan outperforms on both dimensions.
— Fintech Explained, Ch. 4 (Eq. 11)
Regulatory constraint · Tier 1 Capital
Higher leverage boosts ROE but increases systemic risk. Regulators require minimum capital ratios — JPMorgan: 13.5% Tier 1 vs. peers 13.0%. One obvious way to increase ROA is to take greater risk on loans — higher short-run profits but larger long-run losses.

The valuation toolkit

Price is what you pay. Value is what you get.

Early-stage: angel scorecards (Payne $2M base, Berkus max $2.5M, RFS ±$250K per risk). Growth: VC method works backward from terminal value at 25–35% target IRR, adjusted for survival probability. Mature: market multiples (P/E, EV/Sales, EV/EBITDA) or DCF with WACC ~10%. Banks: P/E and P/Book only. Disruptive fintechs: adjusted EBITDA is one lens — always verify.
