Slide 01 ~2 min
Cover — Valuation of Fintech Companies
  • Welcome the class. Frame the session: today we move from business models (Ch. 3) to putting a number on them. Remind students the reading is Chapter 4 of Fintech Explained.
  • Preview the arc: we start with why valuation is subjective (ZOPA), walk through angel methods for pre-revenue companies, move to VC discount rates for growth stage, then tackle mature-firm tools (multiples, DCF), and end with 2024-2026 market data showing how these multiples actually moved.
  • Pose the opening question: "If I told you a fintech startup has no revenue, no product, and one slide deck — would you invest at a $2M valuation? By the end of today you'll know exactly what assumptions justify that number."
  • Housekeeping: we have ~75 minutes. I'll pause for questions after the VC method and again after the market-data section.
Transition: "Let's start with a fundamental truth about valuation..."
Slide 02 ~4 min
The Art of Valuation & ZOPA
  • Emphasize the negotiation framing: valuation is not a calculation — it is a bargaining outcome. The party with better alternatives (BATNA) steers the price. Ask students: "In an early-stage deal, who typically has more bargaining power — founder or angel?" (Answer: usually the investor, unless the startup is hot.)
  • Explain ZOPA (Zone of Possible Agreement): the buyer's ceiling and seller's floor create the negotiable range. Outside ZOPA, no deal happens. Connect to real estate negotiations students may have seen.
  • Distinguish fundamental (intrinsic) value from relative value. Fundamental = bottom-up DCF; relative = what comparable companies trade at. Both are tools in a negotiation, not objective truth. A model's output depends on who chose the inputs.
  • Founder trade-off: equity dilution vs. growth capital. The emotional dimension — founders anchoring too high, investors anchoring too low. Behavioral biases (anchoring, overconfidence) shape every term sheet.
  • Timing cue: keep this conceptual. The math comes next.
Transition: "Now that we agree valuation is subjective, is there a structured way to approach it? Yes — four universal steps."
Slide 03 ~4 min
Four Steps in Valuation (Fig. 4.1)
  • Walk through the four steps deliberately: (1) Identify value drivers — what makes this company worth anything? Top-down (TAM, macro tailwinds) and bottom-up (team quality, unit economics). Stress GIGO: garbage assumptions in, garbage valuation out.
  • (2) Forecast — project revenues, margins. Scenario analysis: base, bull, bear. For private companies, data is scarce; you often rely on management projections (always skeptically).
  • (3) Discount — time value of money. Quick refresher: PV = FV / (1+r)^n. Ask a student: "$100 in 7 years at 10% — what's it worth today?" ($51.32). The discount rate is where all the controversy lives.
  • (4) Divide the pie — Enterprise Value = Net Debt + Equity. Creditors are senior; equity is residual. For startups with SAFEs and convertible notes, the "as-converted" cap table gets complex fast. Point to Table 4.1 showing pre-money vs. post-money per share math.
  • Reference Fig. 4.1 on slide: a simple flowchart students can anchor the rest of the lecture against.
Transition: "For early-stage companies with no revenue, steps 2 and 3 are basically impossible with traditional models. So angel investors invented scorecards..."
Slide 04 ~4 min
Angel Valuation — Bill Payne Scorecard
  • Context: angel investors face the hardest valuation problem — no revenue, maybe no product. They use heuristics, not spreadsheets. The Payne Scorecard is the most widely taught.
  • Mechanics: start from an average pre-money for recent angel deals in your region (~$2M is the textbook default). Then adjust across seven dimensions, each weighted. The entrepreneur/team dimension alone is 30% — ask students why. (Answer: at pre-revenue, execution risk dominates.)
  • Walk through the textbook example: adjustments sum to 115.5% of the $2M base, yielding $2.31M pre-money. Emphasize subjectivity — two angels looking at the same startup could get $1.8M or $2.6M. That's the point: it's structured subjectivity, not science.
  • Statistics to mention: only 10% of angel investments generate 90% of returns. Portfolio average is 2.6x over 3.5 years (~27% IRR). Angels need the winners to be very big.
  • Ask: "Would this method work for a fintech startup with $500K ARR?" (Answer: probably not — once you have revenue, you should use revenue-based methods.)
Transition: "Payne isn't the only scorecard. Let's look at two more: Berkus and Risk Factor Summation."
Slide 05 ~3 min
Berkus Method & Risk Factor Summation
  • Berkus (1996): simpler than Payne. Start at $500K for a sound idea, then add up to $500K each for four risk-mitigation milestones: prototype, team, rollout, strategic relationships. Max pre-money = $2.5M. The worked example gives $1.9M. Good for very early (idea-stage) companies.
  • Risk Factor Summation (Ohio TechAngels): start from regional average (~$2M), then adjust +/- across 12 risk categories (management, competition, tech, litigation, etc.). Each unit = +/- $250K. More granular than Berkus but still qualitative. Example yields $2.25M.
  • Compare all three angel methods: they all anchor around $1.5M–$2.5M pre-money for a typical pre-revenue startup. The similarity is not coincidental — it reflects empirical angel deal data in North America.
  • Ask students: "Which risk categories do you think are most relevant for a fintech startup specifically?" (Guide toward: regulation/legislation, technology risk, funding risk.)
Transition: "Once a startup raises a Series A, angel scorecards won't cut it. VCs have their own method — working backward from the exit."
Slide 06 ~5 min
The VC Valuation Method
  • This is the first method that uses actual financial projections. VCs target 25-35% IRR over 6-10 years. They work BACKWARD: start at exit, discount to today. The twist: they adjust for bankruptcy risk (most startups fail).
  • Walk through the worked example step by step. TV = $2M profit x 20x P/E = $40M exit. Discount rate = (1.25 / 0.20) - 1 = 56.25% p.a. — that 20% is annual survival probability. Post-money = $40M / (1.5625)^5 = $4.3M. Pre-money = $4.3M - $2.5M investment = $1.8M.
  • Key insight: the 56.25% discount rate is NOT the cost of capital in a CAPM sense. It's an IRR hurdle adjusted for the 80% probability of total loss each year. Ask: "If survival probability were 50% instead of 20%, what happens to the discount rate?" (Answer: it drops to ~150%, making the valuation higher — more survival = higher price today.)
  • Ownership math: VC owns 58.2% ($2.5M / $4.3M). Founders + angels diluted to 41.8%. Post-money shares = 1M / 0.418 = 2.39M shares. Price = $1.79/share.
  • Reality check: multiple rounds (A through F), ESOP pools, and anti-dilution provisions make real cap tables far more complex. But the logic is the same.
Transition: "The VC method works for growth-stage private companies. But what about mature, publicly-traded fintechs? We need market multiples."
Slide 07 ~4 min
Market Multiples & Precedent Transactions
  • Relative valuation: instead of building a model from scratch, ask "what are similar companies worth?" Three steps: (1) identify comparables, (2) choose the right multiple (P/E for equity, EV/Sales or EV/EBITDA for enterprise), (3) apply to your target.
  • Table 4.5: catalog the common multiples. P/E works for profitable firms. EV/Sales works for growth firms with no earnings. EV/EBITDA is the workhorse for mid-stage. LTM (last twelve months) vs. NTM (next twelve months, forward-looking).
  • Adyen example (Table 4.6): Dutch payments processor, $44B market cap. P/E of 75.5x vs. peer average 39.4x. Why the premium? Growth expectations + 50%+ EBITDA margin. But EBITDA margin is only 10% of revenue vs. Visa's 71% — different business models require different multiples.
  • Precedent transactions: add a control premium (typically 20-40%) because you're buying the whole company. Show the outlier problem: dropping one outlier from 3x, 2x, 4x, 5x, 8x changes the answer from $220M to $175M (-20%).
  • Warning: "changing the comparables changes the answer." This is both a feature and a bug of relative valuation.
Transition: "Multiples are quick but shallow. For a deeper, first-principles answer, we need the DCF model."
Slide 08 ~5 min
The DCF Model
  • This is the gold standard of fundamental valuation. Forecast free cash flows (FCFF) over 5-10 years, discount at WACC, add terminal value. CRITICAL LIMITATION: cannot be used for banks or financial intermediaries (no "sales" or "EBITDA" in the traditional sense).
  • FCFF formula: EBIT(1-t) + D&A - CAPEX - change in NWC. Walk through each component. Use marginal tax rate, not effective. D&A is non-cash (add back). CAPEX and working capital are real cash drains to fund growth.
  • Terminal value: typically 70-80% of total EV. Two approaches that should give similar answers: (a) perpetuity growth — TV = FCFF(t+1) / (WACC - g), where g = 2-4% long-term GDP growth; (b) exit multiple — TV = EBITDA(t) x comparable EV/EBITDA.
  • Ask students: "If terminal value is 70-80% of total EV, what does that tell you about the sensitivity of a DCF?" (Answer: the model is extremely sensitive to terminal-value assumptions — growth rate and exit multiple dominate everything.)
  • Net Working Capital definition: (Current Assets - Cash) - (Current Liabilities - Short-term Debt). The CHANGE captures incremental cash needed to support sales growth each year.
Transition: "DCF works beautifully for a mature SaaS company. But what about fintechs that are growing 50%+ and deliberately losing money? Cathie Wood has an answer..."
Slide 09 ~4 min
ARK Invest & Adjusted EBITDA
  • ARK's thesis: GAAP accounting penalizes disruptive companies that invest heavily to scale. Wood proposes five adjustments: (1) add back R&D (28.5% of revenue for ARK holdings vs. 12% Nasdaq average), (2) add incremental S&M (customer acquisition for scaling), (3) add back stock-based compensation (non-cash, like D&A), (4) count deferred revenue when cash is received, (5) add back goodwill impairments.
  • Critical discussion point: Who benefits from these adjustments? ARK manages ETFs — higher adjusted EBITDA means higher implied valuations means higher AUM means higher fees. Always consider incentives. Ask students: "Is this analysis or marketing?"
  • ARKF performance: $50B AUM (Feb 2021) crashed to $16B (mid-2022), a -70% decline. Fig. 4.2 shows ARKF vs. S&P 500 indexed. The portfolio concentrated in fintechs with negative GAAP earnings.
  • Table 4.7 observation: 8 of 10 ARKF companies have EV/EBITDA outliers (>20x or negative). Robinhood had NEGATIVE enterprise value. When EV/EBITDA breaks down, EV/Sales (2x-10x) becomes the fallback metric.
  • Takeaway: adjusted EBITDA is ONE lens. It's useful for understanding unit economics, but can be manipulated to justify any price.
Transition: "We said DCF doesn't work for banks. So how DO you value a bank? Let's look at JPMorgan."
Slide 10 ~4 min
Bank Valuation — JPMorgan
  • Banks lack traditional "sales" and "EBITDA." Interest income IS the product. Enterprise multiples and DCF cannot be applied. Analysts use equity multiples (P/E, P/Book) and performance ratios.
  • Key ratios for JPMorgan (2022): NIM 2.5% (net interest income / average interest-earning assets; North American range 1-3%), Efficiency Ratio 59% (noninterest expense / revenue; lower = better), ROE 15.2% (ROA x leverage). Revenue split: 52% NII, 48% noninterest income.
  • Valuation anchor: Table 4.10 shows JPMorgan vs. peer US banks. P/E and P/Book are the only multiples that work. Banks with higher ROE trade at premium P/Book — this is the key relationship.
  • Ask students: "Why can't we use EV/EBITDA for a bank?" (Answer: debt is NOT financing for a bank — it IS the product. Deposits are liabilities that generate interest income. Enterprise value loses meaning.)
  • This slide matters because later we'll compare bank multiples to neobank multiples — the gap is dramatic.
Transition: "Now let's put actual fintech companies side by side and see what their multiples look like."
Slide 11 ~3 min
Fintech Multiples Comparison (Apr 2023 Baseline)
  • Table 4.11 is the textbook's snapshot (Refinitiv, April 2023). Seven listed fintechs with extreme variation in NTM multiples. This is the "before" picture — we'll update it in slides 14-15.
  • PayPal looks most attractive on traditional metrics: highest ROA (3.1%), lowest P/E NTM (14.9x), lowest EV/EBITDA (11x). But it's also the slowest grower. Cheap for a reason?
  • Wise operates like a bank: Assets/Equity = 18.5x (vs. median 3.4x). High ROE (14.4%) but low ROA (0.6%). The leverage is customer float, not debt.
  • Square, Robinhood, SoFi, Paytm: all negative ROE in April 2023. P/E is meaningless — validates ARK's argument for adjusted metrics. But this was the trough. By 2025, all four became profitable.
  • Key takeaway: a single "fintech multiple" does not exist. The variation here is 3x-4x across companies in the same sector.
Transition: "That was April 2023. The world didn't stand still. Let's see what the 2024-2026 reset looks like."
Slide 12 ~3 min
Closing — The Valuation Toolkit
  • Synthesize the toolkit by stage: Early-stage (pre-revenue) = angel scorecards. Growth-stage (Series A+) = VC method. Mature/public = market multiples or DCF. Banks = P/E and P/Book only. Disruptive/pre-profit fintechs = adjusted EBITDA as ONE lens, always verify.
  • Three numbers to remember: $2M (Payne Scorecard base), 56.25% (VC discount rate at 20% survival), 70-80% (terminal value share of DCF enterprise value).
  • The big lesson from 2024-2026 data: relative valuation snapshots decay fast. The comparable set matters more than the multiple. Sub-sector dispersion is 10x (from 2.6x lending to 26.6x blockchain). AI-native and Rule-of-40 are the new gatekeepers for premium multiples.
  • Preview next class: Chapter 5 — Bitcoin, Blockchain & Cryptocurrencies. We'll go deeper into the asset class that several of today's slides touched on (Strategy, Circle, tokenized RWA).
  • Final question to leave students with: "If valuation is an art and not a science, what makes one analyst better than another?" (Answer: better assumptions, better comparable selection, and intellectual honesty about uncertainty.)
