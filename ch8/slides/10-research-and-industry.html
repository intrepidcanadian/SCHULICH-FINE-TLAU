What the evidence says — and how small robo really is

Robo is <2% of a $100T industry.

At ~$1.8T AUM in 2022, robo-advice is under 2% of the $100T wealth management industry. The industry splits into a wholesale side (institutional investors paid on AUM/AUA, fees up to 3.5%) and a retail side (advisors who must be registered — Series 7, CSC, CFA, CFP). AUM vs AUA: under AUM the institution makes the decisions; under AUA it merely holds the assets while the client decides.

The fintech opening. HNW clients ($1M+) and family offices (UHNW, $30M+) get personalized service that was once uneconomic for retail. Code now delivers it — daily rebalancing, tax-loss harvesting, smart beta — to overcharged retail customers paying an opaque 2–3% of AUM.
The most prominent robo study · who benefits?
Under-diversified investors won. Switching to a robo raised their diversification and market-adjusted returns.
Already-diversified investors lost. They traded more, but the extra activity did not improve performance.
Both reduced behavioral biases such as trend chasing — though the biases did not disappear entirely.
A traditional advisor manages ~200 clients. At 2% of AUM, a $100K client yields $400K in fees — a $10K client just $40K. The math is why retail was underserved.
