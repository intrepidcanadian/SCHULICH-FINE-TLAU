Everyone loves a David-vs.-Goliath story — this time Goliath won

Adoption by giants, not disruption by start-ups.

Robo-advisors offer automated online portals that use algorithms to recommend customized portfolios of low-cost ETFs. They emerged in 2008 as a cheap, simple option for investors unwilling to pay an advisor. But low fees, high acquisition costs, low barriers to entry, and fierce competition meant only scale survived — and incumbents had the scale.

In the end, robo advice is a story of adoption at major firms, not one of disruption by independent start-ups.
— Backend Benchmarking
The hard lesson · why technology alone is not enough
Customers — not tech — are the moat
The algorithms are cheap and easy to copy, so incumbents simply replicated or bought them. The hard part is winning customers at a low enough cost — and that is where giants with existing scale and trust already had the edge.
$100T industry, still in play
Robo-advice captured under 2% of global wealth-management assets. So robos lost the headline battle, but 98%+ of a $100T industry is still open — fintechs keep disrupting the rest of the value chain (onboarding, advice, back office, fees).
Consumers win, profits shrink
Even though start-ups did not take over, the technology stuck: cheaper fees, simpler access, and more transparency for investors — and thinner margins for the incumbents who adopted it.
